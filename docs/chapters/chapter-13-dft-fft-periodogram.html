Part IV. Frequency-Domain and Multiresolution Methods
Chapter goal: move from the population spectral density of Chapter 12 to finite-sample spectral estimation. We develop the discrete Fourier transform as a change of basis, the fast Fourier transform as an efficient algorithm, the periodogram as a sample spectrum, and smoothed periodograms as practical estimators of dominant frequencies.

15.1 Learning objectives

After studying this chapter, you should be able to:

  1. represent a finite time series as a vector in a complex Fourier basis;
  2. define Fourier frequencies and explain why they are natural for data of length \(n\);
  3. derive the discrete Fourier transform from orthogonal projection;
  4. use Parseval’s identity to connect time-domain energy and frequency-domain energy;
  5. define the periodogram and interpret it as variance assigned to frequencies;
  6. explain why the raw periodogram is noisy and why smoothing is needed;
  7. compute DFTs and periodograms using direct formulas and the FFT;
  8. recognize leakage, resolution limits, and aliasing in finite samples;
  9. use the periodogram to propose periodic structure, then verify it by time-domain modeling;
  10. use AI tools to help interpret spectral evidence while checking scaling, leakage, and sampling assumptions.

15.2 13.1 From population spectrum to sample spectrum

In Chapter 12 we studied the spectral density \(f(\omega)\) of a stationary process. It is a population quantity satisfying

\[ \gamma(h)=\int_{-1/2}^{1/2} e^{2\pi i\omega h} f(\omega)\,d\omega, \]

and, when \(\sum_h |\gamma(h)|<\infty\),

\[ f(\omega)=\sum_{h=-\infty}^{\infty}\gamma(h)e^{-2\pi i\omega h}. \]

In practice, we do not observe the entire process. We observe one finite realization

\[ x_0,x_1,\ldots,x_{n-1}. \]

The main problem of this chapter is:

Given \(x_0,\ldots,x_{n-1}\), how can we estimate where the variance of the series lives in frequency?

There are two closely related answers.

First, estimate the autocovariance \(\gamma(h)\) by the sample autocovariance \(\widehat{\gamma}(h)\) and plug it into the Fourier-series formula for \(f(\omega)\).

Second, compute the discrete Fourier transform of the observed sequence and square its magnitude. This gives the periodogram.

At the Fourier frequencies \(\omega_k=k/n\), these two approaches are essentially the same, up to details of normalization and centering. The periodogram is the computationally natural form because the fast Fourier transform computes it extremely quickly.

NotePopulation versus sample

The spectral density \(f(\omega)\) is a property of the data-generating process. The periodogram \(I(\omega_k)\) is computed from one finite sample. It is a noisy finite-sample object, not the true spectrum itself.

15.3 13.2 Fourier frequencies

For a sample of length \(n\), the natural discrete frequencies are

\[ \omega_k=\frac{k}{n}, \qquad k=0,1,\ldots,n-1. \]

The corresponding complex exponential sequence is

\[ e_k(t)=\exp(2\pi i\omega_k t)=\exp\left(2\pi i\frac{kt}{n}\right), \qquad t=0,1,\ldots,n-1. \]

These frequencies are natural because each sequence \(e_k(t)\) completes exactly \(k\) cycles over a block of length \(n\). Also,

\[ e_k(t+n)=e_k(t), \]

so these functions are periodic on the observed sample grid.

For real-valued time series, the positive and negative frequencies are paired. Since

\[ \exp(2\pi i(1-\omega)t)=\exp(-2\pi i\omega t), \]

the frequencies above \(1/2\) correspond to negative frequencies. Therefore, one-sided periodogram plots usually show only

\[ 0\leq \omega\leq \frac{1}{2}. \]

The upper endpoint \(1/2\) is the Nyquist frequency.

15.3.1 Period interpretation

If a periodogram has a peak at \(\omega=0.05\), the corresponding period is

\[ \frac{1}{0.05}=20 \]

time steps per cycle.

If the data are monthly and a peak occurs at \(\omega=1/12\), the period is \(12\) months. If the data are daily and a peak occurs at \(\omega=1/7\), the period is \(7\) days.

15.4 13.3 The Fourier basis as an orthonormal basis

Let \(\mathbb{C}^n\) be the vector space of complex-valued sequences of length \(n\). Define the inner product

\[ \langle a,b\rangle=\sum_{t=0}^{n-1} a_t\overline{b_t}. \]

For \(k=0,1,\ldots,n-1\), define

\[ \phi_k(t)=\frac{1}{\sqrt n}\exp\left(2\pi i\frac{kt}{n}\right). \]

Then \(\{\phi_0,\phi_1,\ldots,\phi_{n-1}\}\) is an orthonormal basis of \(\mathbb{C}^n\). Indeed,

\[ \langle \phi_j,\phi_k\rangle =\frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}\exp\left(2\pi i\frac{(j-k)t}{n}\right). \]

If \(j=k\), every term is \(1\), so the sum is \(1\). If \(j\neq k\), this is a finite geometric series with ratio not equal to \(1\), and the sum is \(0\).

Thus every finite sequence \(x=(x_0,\ldots,x_{n-1})\) has the expansion

\[ x=\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} c_k\phi_k, \]

where

\[ c_k=\langle x,\phi_k\rangle =\frac{1}{\sqrt n}\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}x_t\exp\left(-2\pi i\frac{kt}{n}\right). \]

These coefficients \(c_k\) are the unitary discrete Fourier transform coefficients.

ImportantDFT as coordinates

The DFT is not mysterious. It is the coordinate vector of \(x\) in the Fourier basis. It tells us how much of each sinusoidal basis direction is present in the observed sequence.

15.5 13.4 DFT conventions and scaling

Different books and software packages use slightly different normalizations. The mathematics is the same, but the scaling changes.

A common unnormalized DFT is

\[ D_k=\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}x_t\exp\left(-2\pi i\frac{kt}{n}\right). \]

The inverse transform is

\[ x_t=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}D_k\exp\left(2\pi i\frac{kt}{n}\right). \]

The unitary coefficient from the previous section is

\[ c_k=\frac{D_k}{\sqrt n}. \]

Some time-series texts use the scaled coefficient

\[ X(\omega_k)=\frac{1}{n}D_k =\frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}x_t\exp(-2\pi i\omega_k t). \]

In this chapter, when we define the periodogram, we use

\[ I(\omega_k)=\frac{1}{n}|D_k|^2=|c_k|^2. \]

With this convention, Parseval’s identity becomes particularly simple:

\[ \sum_{t=0}^{n-1}|x_t|^2 =\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}I(\omega_k). \]

If the data are centered, then

\[ \frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}(x_t-\bar x)^2 =\frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}I(\omega_k). \]

Thus the periodogram divides the sample energy, or variance after centering, across Fourier frequencies.

15.5.1 Python: direct DFT versus NumPy FFT

Code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(7339)
n = 32
t = np.arange(n)
x = 1.2 * np.cos(2 * np.pi * 4 * t / n) + 0.5 * rng.normal(size=n)
x = x - x.mean()

# Direct DFT matrix: D_k = sum_t x_t exp(-2 pi i k t / n)
k = np.arange(n)
W = np.exp(-2j * np.pi * np.outer(k, t) / n)
D_direct = W @ x

# NumPy FFT uses the same unnormalized convention.
D_fft = np.fft.fft(x)

print("maximum absolute difference:", np.max(np.abs(D_direct - D_fft)))

freq = np.fft.fftfreq(n, d=1)
periodogram = (np.abs(D_fft) ** 2) / n

plt.figure(figsize=(9, 4))
plt.stem(freq[: n // 2 + 1], periodogram[: n // 2 + 1])
plt.xlabel("frequency")
plt.ylabel("periodogram")
plt.title("One-sided periodogram from the FFT")
plt.show()

15.6 13.5 The periodogram

The periodogram at Fourier frequency \(\omega_k=k/n\) is

\[ I(\omega_k)=\frac{1}{n}\left|\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}x_t e^{-2\pi i\omega_k t}\right|^2. \]

Equivalently, writing the real and imaginary components,

\[ \sum_{t=0}^{n-1}x_t e^{-2\pi i\omega_k t} =\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}x_t\cos(2\pi\omega_k t) -i\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}x_t\sin(2\pi\omega_k t). \]

Therefore,

\[ I(\omega_k) =\frac{1}{n}\left(\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}x_t\cos(2\pi\omega_k t)\right)^2 +\frac{1}{n}\left(\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}x_t\sin(2\pi\omega_k t)\right)^2. \]

This formula shows the regression interpretation: the periodogram is large at a frequency if the data have a strong projection onto the sine and cosine waves at that frequency.

15.6.1 Centering and the zero frequency

The frequency \(\omega_0=0\) corresponds to the constant basis vector. Since

\[ D_0=\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}x_t=n\bar x, \]

the zero-frequency periodogram is

\[ I(0)=n\bar x^2. \]

If the mean is not removed, the zero-frequency spike may dominate the plot. For spectral analysis of fluctuations around a mean, we usually center the data first.

WarningCenter before interpreting peaks

A strong mean or trend can create large low-frequency power. Before interpreting the periodogram, ask whether you should center, detrend, difference, or otherwise remove deterministic structure.

15.6.2 Python: periodogram of a hidden periodic signal

Code 
rng = np.random.default_rng(2026)

15.7 13.6 The periodogram and sample autocovariance

For centered data, define a circular sample autocovariance

\[ \widetilde{\gamma}(h)=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}x_t x_{t+h \;\mathrm{mod}\; n}. \]

Then the periodogram can be written as the discrete Fourier transform of \(\widetilde{\gamma}(h)\):

\[ I(\omega_k)=\sum_{h=0}^{n-1}\widetilde{\gamma}(h)e^{-2\pi i\omega_k h}. \]

This is the finite-sample analog of

\[ f(\omega)=\sum_{h=-\infty}^{\infty}\gamma(h)e^{-2\pi i\omega h}. \]

The usual non-circular sample autocovariance is

\[ \widehat{\gamma}(h)=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=0}^{n-1-h}(x_{t+h}-\bar x)(x_t-\bar x), \qquad h\geq 0. \]

Using \(\widehat{\gamma}(h)\) in a finite Fourier sum gives another form of spectral estimate. At Fourier frequencies, the periodogram is the most common computational object.

15.8 13.7 Why the raw periodogram is noisy

The periodogram is asymptotically unbiased under suitable regularity conditions, but it is not a consistent estimator of \(f(\omega)\) without smoothing. Informally:

  • as \(n\) grows, the frequency grid becomes denser;
  • the expected periodogram approaches the true spectrum;
  • but the variance of the periodogram at a fixed frequency does not vanish.

This means the raw periodogram remains jagged even for long series. It is useful for detecting strong peaks, but it is usually too variable for estimating a smooth spectral density.

A common remedy is to average nearby periodogram values:

\[ \widehat f(\omega_k)=\sum_{j=-m}^{m} a_j I(\omega_{k+j}), \]

where \(a_j\geq 0\) and \(\sum_{j=-m}^{m}a_j=1\).

The window width controls a bias-variance tradeoff:

  • more smoothing decreases variance but blurs narrow peaks;
  • less smoothing preserves sharp peaks but leaves high variance.

15.8.1 Python: smoothing a periodogram

Code 
rng = np.random.default_rng(1)
n = 512
t = np.arange(n)

# AR(1)-like smooth process plus a periodic component.
phi = 0.85
w = rng.normal(size=n)
y = np.zeros(n)
for i in range(1, n):
    y[i] = phi * y[i-1] + w[i]

x = y + 1.5 * np.cos(2 * np.pi * (1/40) * t)
x = x - x.mean()

freq = np.fft.rfftfreq(n, d=1)
I = np.abs(np.fft.rfft(x)) ** 2 / n

# Simple moving-average smoother over frequencies.
width = 9
kernel = np.ones(width) / width
I_smooth = np.convolve(I, kernel, mode="same")

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 4))
plt.plot(freq[1:], I[1:], alpha=0.5, label="raw periodogram")
plt.plot(freq[1:], I_smooth[1:], linewidth=2, label="smoothed periodogram")
plt.xlabel("frequency")

15.9 13.8 The fast Fourier transform

The direct DFT formula requires computing

\[ D_k=\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}x_t e^{-2\pi i kt/n} \]

for \(k=0,1,\ldots,n-1\). This involves about \(n^2\) complex multiplications. For large \(n\), this is expensive.

The fast Fourier transform is an algorithm that computes the same DFT coefficients in approximately

\[ O(n\log n) \]

operations.

The key idea is to exploit symmetry. When \(n\) is even, split the DFT into even and odd time indices:

\[ D_k =\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}x_t e^{-2\pi i kt/n} =\sum_{m=0}^{n/2-1}x_{2m} e^{-2\pi i k(2m)/n} +\sum_{m=0}^{n/2-1}x_{2m+1} e^{-2\pi i k(2m+1)/n}. \]

This becomes

\[ D_k =\sum_{m=0}^{n/2-1}x_{2m} e^{-2\pi i km/(n/2)} +e^{-2\pi i k/n}\sum_{m=0}^{n/2-1}x_{2m+1} e^{-2\pi i km/(n/2)}. \]

The two sums are DFTs of length \(n/2\). Thus a DFT of length \(n\) can be computed using two DFTs of length \(n/2\) plus a small amount of recombination. Repeating the split gives the FFT.

NoteAlgorithm, not a new transform

The FFT does not define a new mathematical transform. It is a fast algorithm for computing the DFT.

15.9.1 Python: timing direct DFT and FFT

Code 
import time

rng = np.random.default_rng(42)

for n in [128, 256, 512]:
    x = rng.normal(size=n)
    t = np.arange(n)
    k = np.arange(n)

    start = time.perf_counter()
    W = np.exp(-2j * np.pi * np.outer(k, t) / n)
    D_direct = W @ x
    direct_time = time.perf_counter() - start

    start = time.perf_counter()
    D_fft = np.fft.fft(x)
    fft_time = time.perf_counter() - start

    error = np.max(np.abs(D_direct - D_fft))
    print(f"n={n:4d}  direct={direct_time:.4f}s  fft={fft_time:.6f}s  error={error:.2e}")

15.10 13.9 Spectral leakage and finite-sample resolution

The Fourier basis frequencies are exactly \(k/n\). What happens if the true signal has frequency not exactly equal to one of these grid frequencies?

Suppose

\[ x_t=A\cos(2\pi\omega_0 t) \]

where \(\omega_0\) is not equal to \(k/n\) for any integer \(k\). Then the energy cannot be placed into one Fourier bin. It spreads into neighboring bins. This is called spectral leakage.

Leakage is caused by observing a finite window of a signal. Mathematically, truncating a signal in time corresponds to spreading its spectrum in frequency.

A common practical remedy is to multiply the data by a taper or window before applying the DFT. For example, a Hann taper is

\[ w_t=\frac{1}{2}\left(1-\cos\left(\frac{2\pi t}{n-1}\right)\right). \]

Then we analyze \(w_t x_t\) rather than \(x_t\) directly. Tapering reduces edge discontinuities, which often reduces leakage far away from the main peak, but it also widens the main lobe.

15.10.1 Python: leakage and tapering

Code 
n = 128
t = np.arange(n)

# Not an integer number of cycles over the sample.
cycles = 10.5
omega0 = cycles / n
x = np.cos(2 * np.pi * omega0 * t)
x = x - x.mean()

hann = 0.5 * (1 - np.cos(2 * np.pi * t / (n - 1)))
x_hann = hann * x

freq = np.fft.rfftfreq(n, d=1)
I_raw = np.abs(np.fft.rfft(x)) ** 2 / n
I_hann = np.abs(np.fft.rfft(x_hann)) ** 2 / n

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 4))
plt.plot(freq, I_raw / I_raw.max(), label="raw, normalized")
plt.plot(freq, I_hann / I_hann.max(), label="Hann tapered, normalized")
plt.axvline(omega0, linestyle="--", label="true frequency")
plt.xlim(0, 0.18)
plt.xlabel("frequency")
plt.ylabel("normalized periodogram")
plt.title("Spectral leakage and tapering")
plt.legend()

15.13 13.11 Detecting a period is not the same as proving a model

The periodogram is a powerful exploratory tool. A large peak suggests that a sinusoidal component may be present. But a peak alone is not a complete model.

A responsible workflow is:

  1. center, detrend, or difference if necessary;
  2. inspect the periodogram;
  3. identify candidate frequencies;
  4. translate frequencies into periods;
  5. fit a time-domain model using sine and cosine regressors;
  6. inspect residuals using time plots, ACF, and diagnostics;
  7. compare with baseline forecasts.

15.13.1 Python: peak detection followed by sinusoidal regression

Code 
rng = np.random.default_rng(9)
n = 365
t = np.arange(n)

# A synthetic daily series with a weekly component and a mild trend.
x = 0.005 * t + 1.8 * np.cos(2 * np.pi * t / 7 + 0.3) + rng.normal(scale=0.7, size=n)

# Remove a linear trend before period search.
Z_trend = np.column_stack([np.ones(n), t])
trend_hat = Z_trend @ np.linalg.lstsq(Z_trend, x, rcond=None)[0]
r = x - trend_hat

freq = np.fft.rfftfreq(n, d=1)

peak_idx = 1 + np.argmax(I[1:])
omega_hat = freq[peak_idx]
period_hat = 1 / omega_hat

print("periodogram peak frequency:", round(omega_hat, 4))
print("periodogram peak period:", round(period_hat, 2))

    np.ones(n),
    t,
    np.cos(2 * np.pi * omega_hat * t),
    np.sin(2 * np.pi * omega_hat * t),
])
beta = np.linalg.lstsq(Z, x, rcond=None)[0]
fitted = Z @ beta
resid = x - fitted

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 4))
plt.plot(t, x, label="observed", alpha=0.7)
plt.plot(t, fitted, label="trend + detected cycle", linewidth=2)
plt.xlabel("time")
plt.ylabel("value")
plt.title("Time-domain verification of a spectral peak")
plt.legend()
plt.show()

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 4))
plt.plot(freq[1:], I[1:])

plt.xlabel("frequency")
plt.ylabel("periodogram")
plt.title("Periodogram of detrended residuals")
plt.legend()
plt.show()

15.14 13.12 Periodogram for stochastic processes

For a stationary stochastic process, the periodogram is random. Even if the true spectrum is smooth, the observed periodogram can be very irregular.

For example, white noise has a flat spectral density. But the periodogram of one white-noise realization is not flat. It has random spikes. Those spikes do not necessarily indicate meaningful periodicity.

This is one of the most common mistakes in spectral analysis: overinterpreting peaks from a short noisy sequence.

15.15 13.13 Connection with linear filters

A linear filter transforms a time series by

\[ y_t=\sum_{j=-\infty}^{\infty}a_j x_{t-j}. \]

In the frequency domain, filtering is multiplication. If the input has spectral density \(f_x(\omega)\), then under suitable conditions the output has spectral density

\[ f_y(\omega)=|A(\omega)|^2 f_x(\omega), \]

where

\[ A(\omega)=\sum_j a_j e^{-2\pi i\omega j} \]

is the frequency response of the filter.

This explains why moving averages smooth time series. A simple moving average suppresses high-frequency variation and preserves low-frequency variation. In the next chapter, we will use this idea to study filters and wavelets.

15.17 13.15 Common mistakes

  1. Forgetting to center the data. The zero-frequency component can dominate the periodogram.
  2. Interpreting trend as periodicity. Strong trend creates low-frequency power.
  3. Confusing frequency and period. Frequency is cycles per time step; period is time steps per cycle.

  5. Overinterpreting raw periodogram peaks. White noise can produce random peaks.
  6. Forgetting leakage. If the true frequency is not on the Fourier grid, energy spreads across bins.

  8. Comparing periodograms with different scaling conventions. Always check how the software defines the DFT and periodogram.
  9. Ignoring missing or irregular sampling. The standard DFT assumes evenly spaced observations.
  10. Skipping residual diagnostics. A detected frequency should be checked in a fitted model.

## 13.1 From population spectrum to sample spectrum

In Chapter 12 we studied the **spectral density** $f(\omega)$ of a stationary process. It is a population quantity satisfying

$$
\gamma(h)=\int_{-1/2}^{1/2} e^{2\pi i\omega h} f(\omega)\,d\omega,
$$

and, when $\sum_h |\gamma(h)|<\infty$,

$$
f(\omega)=\sum_{h=-\infty}^{\infty}\gamma(h)e^{-2\pi i\omega h}.
$$

In practice, we do not observe the entire process. We observe one finite realization

$$
x_0,x_1,\ldots,x_{n-1}.
$$

The main problem of this chapter is:

> Given $x_0,\ldots,x_{n-1}$, how can we estimate where the variance of the series lives in frequency?

There are two closely related answers.

First, estimate the autocovariance $\gamma(h)$ by the sample autocovariance $\widehat{\gamma}(h)$ and plug it into the Fourier-series formula for $f(\omega)$.

Second, compute the discrete Fourier transform of the observed sequence and square its magnitude. This gives the **periodogram**.

At the Fourier frequencies $\omega_k=k/n$, these two approaches are essentially the same, up to details of normalization and centering. The periodogram is the computationally natural form because the fast Fourier transform computes it extremely quickly.

::: {.callout-note}
## Population versus sample
The spectral density $f(\omega)$ is a property of the data-generating process. The periodogram $I(\omega_k)$ is computed from one finite sample. It is a noisy finite-sample object, not the true spectrum itself.
:::

## 13.2 Fourier frequencies

For a sample of length $n$, the natural discrete frequencies are

$$
\omega_k=\frac{k}{n}, \qquad k=0,1,\ldots,n-1.
$$

The corresponding complex exponential sequence is

$$
e_k(t)=\exp(2\pi i\omega_k t)=\exp\left(2\pi i\frac{kt}{n}\right),
\qquad t=0,1,\ldots,n-1.
$$

These frequencies are natural because each sequence $e_k(t)$ completes exactly $k$ cycles over a block of length $n$. Also,

$$
e_k(t+n)=e_k(t),
$$

so these functions are periodic on the observed sample grid.

For real-valued time series, the positive and negative frequencies are paired. Since

$$
\exp(2\pi i(1-\omega)t)=\exp(-2\pi i\omega t),
$$

the frequencies above $1/2$ correspond to negative frequencies. Therefore, one-sided periodogram plots usually show only

$$
0\leq \omega\leq \frac{1}{2}.
$$

The upper endpoint $1/2$ is the Nyquist frequency.

### Period interpretation

If a periodogram has a peak at $\omega=0.05$, the corresponding period is

$$
\frac{1}{0.05}=20
$$

time steps per cycle.

If the data are monthly and a peak occurs at $\omega=1/12$, the period is $12$ months. If the data are daily and a peak occurs at $\omega=1/7$, the period is $7$ days.

## 13.3 The Fourier basis as an orthonormal basis

Let $\mathbb{C}^n$ be the vector space of complex-valued sequences of length $n$. Define the inner product

$$
\langle a,b\rangle=\sum_{t=0}^{n-1} a_t\overline{b_t}.
$$

For $k=0,1,\ldots,n-1$, define

$$
\phi_k(t)=\frac{1}{\sqrt n}\exp\left(2\pi i\frac{kt}{n}\right).
$$

Then $\{\phi_0,\phi_1,\ldots,\phi_{n-1}\}$ is an orthonormal basis of $\mathbb{C}^n$. Indeed,

$$
\langle \phi_j,\phi_k\rangle
=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}\exp\left(2\pi i\frac{(j-k)t}{n}\right).
$$

If $j=k$, every term is $1$, so the sum is $1$. If $j\neq k$, this is a finite geometric series with ratio not equal to $1$, and the sum is $0$.

Thus every finite sequence $x=(x_0,\ldots,x_{n-1})$ has the expansion

$$
x=\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} c_k\phi_k,
$$

where

$$
c_k=\langle x,\phi_k\rangle
=\frac{1}{\sqrt n}\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}x_t\exp\left(-2\pi i\frac{kt}{n}\right).
$$

These coefficients $c_k$ are the unitary discrete Fourier transform coefficients.

::: {.callout-important}
## DFT as coordinates
The DFT is not mysterious. It is the coordinate vector of $x$ in the Fourier basis. It tells us how much of each sinusoidal basis direction is present in the observed sequence.
:::

## 13.4 DFT conventions and scaling

Different books and software packages use slightly different normalizations. The mathematics is the same, but the scaling changes.

A common unnormalized DFT is

$$
D_k=\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}x_t\exp\left(-2\pi i\frac{kt}{n}\right).
$$

The inverse transform is

$$
x_t=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}D_k\exp\left(2\pi i\frac{kt}{n}\right).
$$

The unitary coefficient from the previous section is

$$
c_k=\frac{D_k}{\sqrt n}.
$$

Some time-series texts use the scaled coefficient

$$
X(\omega_k)=\frac{1}{n}D_k
=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}x_t\exp(-2\pi i\omega_k t).
$$

In this chapter, when we define the periodogram, we use

$$
I(\omega_k)=\frac{1}{n}|D_k|^2=|c_k|^2.
$$

With this convention, Parseval's identity becomes particularly simple:

$$
\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}|x_t|^2
=\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}I(\omega_k).
$$

If the data are centered, then

$$
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}(x_t-\bar x)^2
=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}I(\omega_k).
$$

Thus the periodogram divides the sample energy, or variance after centering, across Fourier frequencies.

```{python}

#| warning: false
#| message: false

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x = x - x.mean()

# Direct DFT matrix: D_k = sum_t x_t exp(-2 pi i k t / n)

# NumPy FFT uses the same unnormalized convention.

periodogram = (np.abs(D_fft) ** 2) / n

plt.figure(figsize=(9, 4))
plt.stem(freq[: n // 2 + 1], periodogram[: n // 2 + 1])
plt.xlabel("frequency")
plt.ylabel("periodogram")
plt.title("One-sided periodogram from the FFT")
plt.show()
```

## 13.5 The periodogram

The **periodogram** at Fourier frequency $\omega_k=k/n$ is

$$
I(\omega_k)=\frac{1}{n}\left|\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}x_t e^{-2\pi i\omega_k t}\right|^2.
$$

Equivalently, writing the real and imaginary components,

$$
\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}x_t e^{-2\pi i\omega_k t}
=\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}x_t\cos(2\pi\omega_k t)
-i\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}x_t\sin(2\pi\omega_k t).
$$

Therefore,

$$
I(\omega_k)
=\frac{1}{n}\left(\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}x_t\cos(2\pi\omega_k t)\right)^2
+\frac{1}{n}\left(\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}x_t\sin(2\pi\omega_k t)\right)^2.
$$

This formula shows the regression interpretation: the periodogram is large at a frequency if the data have a strong projection onto the sine and cosine waves at that frequency.

### Centering and the zero frequency

The frequency $\omega_0=0$ corresponds to the constant basis vector. Since

$$
D_0=\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}x_t=n\bar x,
$$

the zero-frequency periodogram is

$$
I(0)=n\bar x^2.
$$

If the mean is not removed, the zero-frequency spike may dominate the plot. For spectral analysis of fluctuations around a mean, we usually center the data first.

::: {.callout-warning}
## Center before interpreting peaks
A strong mean or trend can create large low-frequency power. Before interpreting the periodogram, ask whether you should center, detrend, difference, or otherwise remove deterministic structure.
:::

## 13.6 The periodogram and sample autocovariance

For centered data, define a circular sample autocovariance

$$
\widetilde{\gamma}(h)=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}x_t x_{t+h \;\mathrm{mod}\; n}.
$$

Then the periodogram can be written as the discrete Fourier transform of $\widetilde{\gamma}(h)$:

$$
I(\omega_k)=\sum_{h=0}^{n-1}\widetilde{\gamma}(h)e^{-2\pi i\omega_k h}.
$$

This is the finite-sample analog of

$$
f(\omega)=\sum_{h=-\infty}^{\infty}\gamma(h)e^{-2\pi i\omega h}.
$$

The usual non-circular sample autocovariance is

$$
\widehat{\gamma}(h)=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=0}^{n-1-h}(x_{t+h}-\bar x)(x_t-\bar x),
\qquad h\geq 0.
$$

Using $\widehat{\gamma}(h)$ in a finite Fourier sum gives another form of spectral estimate. At Fourier frequencies, the periodogram is the most common computational object.

## 13.7 Why the raw periodogram is noisy

The periodogram is asymptotically unbiased under suitable regularity conditions, but it is not a consistent estimator of $f(\omega)$ without smoothing. Informally:

- as $n$ grows, the frequency grid becomes denser;
- the expected periodogram approaches the true spectrum;
- but the variance of the periodogram at a fixed frequency does not vanish.

This means the raw periodogram remains jagged even for long series. It is useful for detecting strong peaks, but it is usually too variable for estimating a smooth spectral density.

A common remedy is to average nearby periodogram values:

$$
\widehat f(\omega_k)=\sum_{j=-m}^{m} a_j I(\omega_{k+j}),
$$

where $a_j\geq 0$ and $\sum_{j=-m}^{m}a_j=1$.

The window width controls a bias-variance tradeoff:

- more smoothing decreases variance but blurs narrow peaks;
- less smoothing preserves sharp peaks but leaves high variance.

## 13.8 The fast Fourier transform

The direct DFT formula requires computing

$$
D_k=\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}x_t e^{-2\pi i kt/n}
$$

for $k=0,1,\ldots,n-1$. This involves about $n^2$ complex multiplications. For large $n$, this is expensive.

The **fast Fourier transform** is an algorithm that computes the same DFT coefficients in approximately

$$
O(n\log n)
$$

operations.

The key idea is to exploit symmetry. When $n$ is even, split the DFT into even and odd time indices:

$$
D_k
=\sum_{t=0}^{n-1}x_t e^{-2\pi i kt/n}
=\sum_{m=0}^{n/2-1}x_{2m} e^{-2\pi i k(2m)/n}
+\sum_{m=0}^{n/2-1}x_{2m+1} e^{-2\pi i k(2m+1)/n}.
$$

This becomes

$$
D_k
=\sum_{m=0}^{n/2-1}x_{2m} e^{-2\pi i km/(n/2)}
+e^{-2\pi i k/n}\sum_{m=0}^{n/2-1}x_{2m+1} e^{-2\pi i km/(n/2)}.
$$

The two sums are DFTs of length $n/2$. Thus a DFT of length $n$ can be computed using two DFTs of length $n/2$ plus a small amount of recombination. Repeating the split gives the FFT.

::: {.callout-note}
## Algorithm, not a new transform
The FFT does not define a new mathematical transform. It is a fast algorithm for computing the DFT.
:::

### Python: timing direct DFT and FFT

```{python}
#| label: ch13-fft-speed
#| warning: false
#| message: false

import time

rng = np.random.default_rng(42)

for n in [128, 256, 512]:
    x = rng.normal(size=n)
    t = np.arange(n)
    k = np.arange(n)

    start = time.perf_counter()
    W = np.exp(-2j * np.pi * np.outer(k, t) / n)
    D_direct = W @ x
    direct_time = time.perf_counter() - start

    start = time.perf_counter()
    D_fft = np.fft.fft(x)
    fft_time = time.perf_counter() - start

    error = np.max(np.abs(D_direct - D_fft))
    print(f"n={n:4d}  direct={direct_time:.4f}s  fft={fft_time:.6f}s  error={error:.2e}")
```

## 13.9 Spectral leakage and finite-sample resolution

The Fourier basis frequencies are exactly $k/n$. What happens if the true signal has frequency not exactly equal to one of these grid frequencies?

Suppose

$$
x_t=A\cos(2\pi\omega_0 t)
$$

where $\omega_0$ is not equal to $k/n$ for any integer $k$. Then the energy cannot be placed into one Fourier bin. It spreads into neighboring bins. This is called **spectral leakage**.

Leakage is caused by observing a finite window of a signal. Mathematically, truncating a signal in time corresponds to spreading its spectrum in frequency.

A common practical remedy is to multiply the data by a taper or window before applying the DFT. For example, a Hann taper is

$$
w_t=\frac{1}{2}\left(1-\cos\left(\frac{2\pi t}{n-1}\right)\right).
$$

Then we analyze $w_t x_t$ rather than $x_t$ directly. Tapering reduces edge discontinuities, which often reduces leakage far away from the main peak, but it also widens the main lobe.

### Python: leakage and tapering


## 13.11 Detecting a period is not the same as proving a model

The periodogram is a powerful exploratory tool. A large peak suggests that a sinusoidal component may be present. But a peak alone is not a complete model.

A responsible workflow is:

1. center, detrend, or difference if necessary;
2. inspect the periodogram;
3. identify candidate frequencies;
4. translate frequencies into periods;
5. fit a time-domain model using sine and cosine regressors;
6. inspect residuals using time plots, ACF, and diagnostics;
7. compare with baseline forecasts.

### Python: peak detection followed by sinusoidal regression

```{python}
#| label: ch13-periodogram-to-regression
#| fig-cap: "A periodogram peak can be converted into sine/cosine regressors and checked by residual diagnostics."
#| warning: false
#| message: false

rng = np.random.default_rng(9)
n = 365
t = np.arange(n)

# A synthetic daily series with a weekly component and a mild trend.
x = 0.005 * t + 1.8 * np.cos(2 * np.pi * t / 7 + 0.3) + rng.normal(scale=0.7, size=n)

# Remove a linear trend before period search.
Z_trend = np.column_stack([np.ones(n), t])
trend_hat = Z_trend @ np.linalg.lstsq(Z_trend, x, rcond=None)[0]
r = x - trend_hat

freq = np.fft.rfftfreq(n, d=1)
I = np.abs(np.fft.rfft(r - r.mean())) ** 2 / n
peak_idx = 1 + np.argmax(I[1:])
omega_hat = freq[peak_idx]
period_hat = 1 / omega_hat

print("periodogram peak frequency:", round(omega_hat, 4))
print("periodogram peak period:", round(period_hat, 2))

# Fit trend plus sine/cosine at the detected frequency.
Z = np.column_stack([
    np.ones(n),
    t,
    np.cos(2 * np.pi * omega_hat * t),
    np.sin(2 * np.pi * omega_hat * t),
])
beta = np.linalg.lstsq(Z, x, rcond=None)[0]
fitted = Z @ beta
resid = x - fitted

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 4))
plt.plot(t, x, label="observed", alpha=0.7)
plt.plot(t, fitted, label="trend + detected cycle", linewidth=2)
plt.xlabel("time")
plt.ylabel("value")
plt.title("Time-domain verification of a spectral peak")
plt.legend()
plt.show()

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 4))
plt.plot(freq[1:], I[1:])
```

## 13.12 Periodogram for stochastic processes

For a stationary stochastic process, the periodogram is random. Even if the true spectrum is smooth, the observed periodogram can be very irregular.

For example, white noise has a flat spectral density. But the periodogram of one white-noise realization is not flat. It has random spikes. Those spikes do not necessarily indicate meaningful periodicity.

This is one of the most common mistakes in spectral analysis: overinterpreting peaks from a short noisy sequence.

### Python: white noise has random periodogram peaks

```{python}
#| label: ch13-white-noise-periodogram

plt.ylabel("periodogram")

plt.show()
```

## 13.13 Connection with linear filters

A linear filter transforms a time series by

$$
y_t=\sum_{j=-\infty}^{\infty}a_j x_{t-j}.
$$

In the frequency domain, filtering is multiplication. If the input has spectral density $f_x(\omega)$, then under suitable conditions the output has spectral density

$$
f_y(\omega)=|A(\omega)|^2 f_x(\omega),
$$

where

$$
A(\omega)=\sum_j a_j e^{-2\pi i\omega j}
$$

is the frequency response of the filter.

This explains why moving averages smooth time series. A simple moving average suppresses high-frequency variation and preserves low-frequency variation. In the next chapter, we will use this idea to study filters and wavelets.

## 13.14 AI-assisted spectral analysis

AI tools are useful for planning and checking spectral analysis, but they should not replace mathematical verification.

Do not ask an AI assistant to simply “find the true period” without providing:

- the sampling interval;
- whether the data have missing observations;
- whether the data were detrended or differenced;
- whether the peak aligns with a Fourier frequency;
- whether there is leakage or aliasing;
- whether the peak appears in independent validation data.

## 13.15 Common mistakes

1. **Forgetting to center the data.** The zero-frequency component can dominate the periodogram.
2. **Interpreting trend as periodicity.** Strong trend creates low-frequency power.
3. **Confusing frequency and period.** Frequency is cycles per time step; period is time steps per cycle.
4. **Ignoring the sampling interval.** A frequency in cycles per observation must be converted to cycles per day, week, month, or second.
5. **Overinterpreting raw periodogram peaks.** White noise can produce random peaks.
6. **Forgetting leakage.** If the true frequency is not on the Fourier grid, energy spreads across bins.
7. **Using the FFT as if it were a model.** The FFT is a computational transform, not a probabilistic model.
8. **Comparing periodograms with different scaling conventions.** Always check how the software defines the DFT and periodogram.
9. **Ignoring missing or irregular sampling.** The standard DFT assumes evenly spaced observations.
10. **Skipping residual diagnostics.** A detected frequency should be checked in a fitted model.

## 13.19 Looking ahead

The DFT and periodogram decompose a finite sequence into global sine and cosine components. This is powerful when periodic behavior is stable over time. But many modern sequence-data problems involve local changes: a burst, a regime shift, a transient oscillation, a sudden anomaly, or a frequency that evolves over time.

The next chapter studies filtering and wavelets. Filters modify the spectrum, while wavelets localize patterns in both time and frequency. Together, they extend the Fourier viewpoint toward multiresolution analysis and modern signal-processing tools for time series.
