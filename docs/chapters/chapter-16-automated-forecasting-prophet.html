Part V. Forecasting at Scale and Machine Learning Methods
Chapter goal: understand automated forecasting as a modeling pipeline, not as a black box. We study Prophet-type additive models, changepoints, Fourier seasonality, holiday effects, cross-validation, hyperparameter tuning, and the analyst-in-the-loop workflow used when many time series must be forecast repeatedly.

18.1 Learning objectives

After studying this chapter, you should be able to:

  1. explain why manual ARIMA/SARIMA modeling becomes difficult at scale;
  2. distinguish time-domain models, frequency-domain methods, additive forecasting models, and deep-learning sequence models;
  3. write the Prophet-style additive model \[ y(t)=g(t)+s(t)+h(t)+\varepsilon_t; \]
  4. derive the main ingredients of piecewise linear trend, saturating logistic trend, Fourier seasonality, and holiday indicators;
  5. explain why changepoint priors and seasonality priors act as regularization parameters;
  6. construct a simplified Prophet-like model using linear regression, Fourier features, changepoint basis functions, and holiday indicators;
  7. design rolling-origin cross-validation for automated forecasting;
  8. tune changepoint, seasonality, holiday, and seasonality-mode parameters without leaking future information;
  9. compare Prophet-type models with SARIMA and machine-learning feature models;
  10. use AI assistants to generate candidate forecasting pipelines while independently checking assumptions, leakage, and diagnostics.

18.2 16.1 Why automated forecasting?

Classical chapters built models by hand. For a single important series, hand modeling is valuable: plot the data, transform it, inspect the ACF/PACF, choose ARIMA or SARIMA orders, estimate parameters, diagnose residuals, and backtest forecasts. This workflow gives mathematical control.

At scale, however, an organization may need forecasts for hundreds, thousands, or millions of related time series:

  • daily demand for many products;
  • hourly load for many regions;
  • weekly admissions for many programs;
  • traffic, rideshare, health, finance, climate, or website metrics;
  • multiple horizons and repeated updates.

Manual modeling becomes slow and inconsistent. Automated forecasting asks for a procedure that can:

  1. fit reasonable models with minimal manual intervention;
  2. incorporate common forecasting structure such as trend, seasonality, holidays, and outliers;
  3. support analyst knowledge through interpretable parameters;
  4. evaluate forecasts with rolling-origin backtests;
  5. scale across many time series.

The lecture notes place Prophet after ARIMA/SARIMA, spectral analysis, and deep-learning approaches. That is the right position pedagogically: Prophet is not a replacement for time-series theory; it is a practical additive modeling strategy that borrows ideas from regression, splines, Fourier analysis, Bayesian regularization, and forecasting evaluation.

NoteAutomated does not mean assumption-free

An automated model still makes assumptions. Prophet-type models assume that trend, seasonality, holiday effects, and noise give a useful decomposition. A good analyst checks whether this decomposition matches the forecasting problem.

18.3 16.2 A taxonomy of forecasting models

A useful high-level taxonomy is:

  1. Time-domain statistical models: AR, MA, ARMA, ARIMA, SARIMA, state-space models. These model dependence through lagged values and lagged shocks.
  2. Frequency-domain methods: Fourier analysis, spectral density, periodograms, wavelets, filtering. These model oscillatory structure.
  3. Additive automated models: Prophet-type models, generalized additive models, spline trend plus seasonality and holiday effects.
  4. Machine-learning feature models: lag features, rolling statistics, calendar features, gradient boosting, random forests.
  5. Deep sequence models: RNN, LSTM, GRU, temporal CNN, TCN, attention, and transformers.

The categories overlap. Prophet uses Fourier features for seasonality, regression-like additive components, changepoint regularization, and rolling-origin cross-validation. NeuralProphet and related models add autoregression or neural-network components.

A practical forecaster compares at least:

\[ \text{naive},\quad \text{seasonal naive},\quad \text{SARIMA},\quad \text{Prophet-type additive model},\quad \text{ML feature model}. \]

The winning model depends on the data-generating mechanism, forecast horizon, cost function, and operational constraints.

18.4 16.3 The Prophet-style additive model

A Prophet-type model represents the observed series as

\[ y(t)=g(t)+s(t)+h(t)+\varepsilon_t. \]

The components are:

  • \(g(t)\): trend, modeling nonperiodic long-term change;
  • \(s(t)\): seasonal component, modeling periodic behavior;
  • \(h(t)\): holiday or event component, modeling irregular known events;
  • \(\varepsilon_t\): residual error.

This is a generalized additive forecasting model. The model is interpretable because each term has a separate role.

For a fixed set of basis functions, the additive model can be written as a regression:

\[ y_t =\beta_0 +\sum_{j=1}^{p_g}\beta^{(g)}_j G_j(t) +\sum_{j=1}^{p_s}\beta^{(s)}_j S_j(t) +\sum_{j=1}^{p_h}\beta^{(h)}_j H_j(t) +\varepsilon_t. \]

Here:

  • \(G_j(t)\) are trend/changepoint features;
  • \(S_j(t)\) are seasonal Fourier features;
  • \(H_j(t)\) are holiday/event indicators.

The model is simple in form, but the feature design and regularization are important.

18.5 Additive model as projection

If the noise has mean zero and the features are fixed, the least-squares fit is the orthogonal projection of the data vector \(y\) onto the span of the trend, seasonal, and holiday feature vectors. Regularization shrinks this projection toward simpler trend and seasonal components.

18.6 16.4 Trend component I: piecewise linear trend

A common trend model is piecewise linear. Suppose changepoints occur at

\[ s_1<s_2<\cdots<s_J. \]

Define the hinge basis function

\[ (t-s_j)_+=\max(t-s_j,0). \]

A continuous piecewise linear trend can be written as

\[ g(t)=m+kt+\sum_{j=1}^J\delta_j(t-s_j)_+. \]

Before the first changepoint, the slope is \(k\). After changepoint \(s_j\), the slope changes by \(\delta_j\). Therefore, after all changepoints up to time \(t\), the local slope is

\[ k+\sum_{j:s_j<t}\delta_j. \]

This form is closely related to linear splines. It is useful because:

  1. the trend remains continuous;
  2. changes in growth rate are explicit;
  3. changepoints can be chosen automatically or specified by the analyst;
  4. regularizing the coefficients \(\delta_j\) controls flexibility.

18.6.1 Changepoint regularization

If too many changepoints are allowed without regularization, the trend can overfit noise. A Laplace prior

\[ \delta_j\sim \operatorname{Laplace}(0,\tau) \]

encourages many \(\delta_j\) to be close to zero. In optimization terms, this behaves like an \(\ell^1\) penalty:

\[ \min_\beta \sum_{t=1}^T \left(y_t-\widehat y_t\right)^2 +\lambda\sum_{j=1}^J|\delta_j|. \]

Small \(\tau\) or large \(\lambda\) gives a smoother trend. Large \(\tau\) or small \(\lambda\) allows more changepoint flexibility.

WarningChangepoints are not magic

A changepoint basis can explain historical bends in the curve, but it does not know whether those bends will continue. Long-horizon forecasts can be very sensitive to trend assumptions.

18.7 16.5 Trend component II: saturating logistic growth

For growth that cannot increase forever, Prophet-type models may use a saturating logistic trend. The basic logistic curve is

\[ g(t)=\frac{C}{1+\exp(-k(t-m))}, \]

where:

  • \(C\) is the carrying capacity;
  • \(k\) is the growth rate;
  • \(m\) is an offset parameter controlling the midpoint.

This model is appropriate when there is a natural ceiling: population growth in an ecosystem, market adoption, number of users in a bounded region, or capacity-limited demand.

A time-varying capacity version replaces \(C\) by \(C(t)\):

\[ g(t)=\frac{C(t)}{1+\exp(-k(t-m))}. \]

Changepoints can also modify the growth rate. Conceptually,

\[ k(t)=k+\sum_{j:s_j<t}\delta_j. \]

The technical implementation adjusts the offset terms so that the curve remains continuous at changepoints.

TipLinear or logistic trend?

Use a logistic trend only when a meaningful capacity is available. Without a meaningful carrying capacity, a piecewise linear trend is usually easier to explain and diagnose.

18.8 16.6 Seasonality through Fourier features

A periodic function with period \(P\) satisfies

\[ s(t+P)=s(t). \]

Prophet models seasonality using a truncated Fourier series:

\[ s(t)= \sum_{n=1}^N \left[ a_n\cos\left(\frac{2\pi nt}{P}\right) +b_n\sin\left(\frac{2\pi nt}{P}\right) \right]. \]

For weekly seasonality in daily data, \(P=7\). For yearly seasonality in daily data, \(P\approx365.25\). For hourly data with daily seasonality, \(P=24\) if time is measured in hours.

The Fourier order \(N\) controls flexibility:

  • small \(N\): smooth seasonal curve;
  • large \(N\): more wiggly seasonal curve;
  • too large \(N\): overfitting.

The number of seasonal coefficients for one seasonal period is \(2N\).

Fourier seasonality connects this chapter directly to spectral analysis. The difference is that here the Fourier basis is used as a deterministic regression basis, not as a full stochastic spectral model.

18.9 16.7 Holiday and event effects

Some effects are neither smooth trends nor regular seasonality. Examples include:

  • Thanksgiving, Black Friday, Christmas, Lunar New Year;
  • semester start, graduation, application deadlines;
  • product launches;
  • policy changes;
  • weather events;
  • one-time disruptions.

For holiday \(i\), let \(D_i\) be the set of dates associated with that holiday. Define

\[ H_i(t)=\mathbf 1_{\{t\in D_i\}}. \]

A holiday component is

\[ h(t)=\sum_{i=1}^K\kappa_i H_i(t). \]

In matrix form,

\[ h(t)=Z(t)^T\kappa. \]

A Gaussian prior

\[ \kappa_i\sim N(0,\nu^2) \]

shrinks holiday effects toward zero unless the data support them. This is important when many holidays or event windows are included.

Holiday effects can also include windows. For example, a Black Friday effect may include Thanksgiving Day, Black Friday, and Cyber Monday:

\[ H_{\text{BlackFriday}}(t)=1 \quad\text{if }t\in\{\text{Thu},\text{Fri},\text{Mon}\} \]

for the relevant calendar year.

18.10 16.8 Additive versus multiplicative seasonality

The default additive model is

\[ y(t)=g(t)+s(t)+h(t)+\varepsilon_t. \]

This assumes that seasonal fluctuations have roughly constant absolute size. For example, the seasonal amplitude is about \(10\) units whether the trend is \(100\) or \(1000\).

A multiplicative seasonal model has the form

\[ y(t)=g(t)\{1+s(t)+h(t)\}+\varepsilon_t. \]

This assumes that seasonal fluctuations scale with the level. For example, the seasonal effect might be about \(10\%\) of the trend.

A common alternative is to log-transform the response:

\[ z(t)=\log y(t), \]

then fit an additive model to \(z(t)\). On the original scale, additive structure in logs becomes multiplicative structure.

NoteRule of thumb

If seasonal swings get larger as the series level increases, consider multiplicative seasonality or a log transformation.

18.12 16.10 Python example: simulate additive forecasting data

We now build a synthetic data set with trend, changepoints, weekly seasonality, yearly seasonality, holiday effects, and noise.

Code 
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(7339)

n = 3 * 365
dates = pd.date_range("2020-01-01", periods=n, freq="D")
t = np.arange(n)

# Piecewise linear trend with two changepoints
cp1, cp2 = 320, 760
trend = 40 + 0.025 * t + 0.060 * np.maximum(t - cp1, 0) - 0.080 * np.maximum(t - cp2, 0)

# Weekly and yearly seasonal components
weekly = 3.0 * np.sin(2 * np.pi * t / 7) + 1.2 * np.cos(2 * np.pi * t / 7)
yearly = 6.0 * np.sin(2 * np.pi * t / 365.25) + 2.5 * np.cos(4 * np.pi * t / 365.25)

# Holiday pulses: one event per year
holiday = np.zeros(n)
for day in [80, 445, 810]:
    holiday[np.abs(t - day) <= 2] = 12

noise = rng.normal(0, 2.0, size=n)
y = trend + weekly + yearly + holiday + noise

df = pd.DataFrame({
    "ds": dates,
    "t": t,
    "y": y,
    "trend": trend,
    "weekly": weekly,
    "yearly": yearly,
    "holiday": holiday
})

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(10, 4))
ax.plot(df["ds"], df["y"], linewidth=1, label="observed")
ax.plot(df["ds"], df["trend"], linewidth=2, label="true trend")
ax.set_title("Synthetic additive forecasting data")
ax.set_xlabel("date")
ax.set_ylabel("value")
ax.legend()
plt.show()

The data frame uses Prophet-style column names:

  • ds: date stamp;
  • y: target value.

Even if we do not use the Prophet package, this naming convention is useful because it reminds students that automated forecasting starts with clean time stamps and a well-defined target.

18.13 16.11 Python example: Fourier features

A Fourier seasonal design matrix for period \(P\) and order \(N\) is

\[ X_{\text{season}}(t)= \left( \cos\frac{2\pi t}{P}, \sin\frac{2\pi t}{P}, \ldots, \cos\frac{2\pi Nt}{P}, \sin\frac{2\pi Nt}{P} \right). \]

Code 
def fourier_features(t, period, order, prefix):
    t = np.asarray(t)
    data = {}
    for k in range(1, order + 1):
        data[f"{prefix}_cos_{k}"] = np.cos(2 * np.pi * k * t / period)
        data[f"{prefix}_sin_{k}"] = np.sin(2 * np.pi * k * t / period)
    return pd.DataFrame(data)

18.14 16.12 Python example: a simplified Prophet-like regression

We can build a Prophet-like model from scratch using:

  1. intercept and linear trend;
  2. hinge functions for changepoints;
  3. Fourier features for weekly and yearly seasonality;
  4. holiday indicators;
  5. ridge regularization.

For clarity, we use a closed-form ridge estimator:

\[ \widehat\beta_{\lambda} =(X^TX+\lambda I)^{-1}X^Ty. \]

We usually do not penalize the intercept; below we set the intercept penalty to zero.

Code 
from numpy.linalg import solve

def make_design_matrix(dates, t, changepoints, weekly_order=3, yearly_order=5, holiday_days=None):
    t = np.asarray(t)
    parts = []

    # Intercept and linear trend
    base = pd.DataFrame({
        "intercept": np.ones_like(t, dtype=float),
        "linear_t": t.astype(float)
    })
    parts.append(base)

    # Hinge functions for changepoints
    cp_data = {}
    for cp in changepoints:
        cp_data[f"hinge_{cp}"] = np.maximum(t - cp, 0)
    parts.append(pd.DataFrame(cp_data))

    # Seasonal Fourier features
    parts.append(fourier_features(t, period=7, order=weekly_order, prefix="weekly"))
    parts.append(fourier_features(t, period=365.25, order=yearly_order, prefix="yearly"))

    # Holiday indicators, with a small window around each event
    if holiday_days is not None:
        h = np.zeros_like(t, dtype=float)
        for day in holiday_days:
            h[np.abs(t - day) <= 2] = 1.0
        parts.append(pd.DataFrame({"holiday_window": h}))

    X = pd.concat(parts, axis=1)
    return X

changepoints = [180, 320, 500, 760, 900]
holiday_days = [80, 445, 810]

# Standardize non-intercept columns for numerical stability.
X_mat = X.to_numpy(dtype=float)
y_vec = df["y"].to_numpy()

means = X_mat.mean(axis=0)
stds = X_mat.std(axis=0)
means[0] = 0.0
stds[0] = 1.0
stds[stds == 0] = 1.0
X_scaled = (X_mat - means) / stds

lam = 25.0

penalty[0, 0] = 0.0

beta = solve(X_scaled.T @ X_scaled + lam * penalty, X_scaled.T @ y_vec)

rmse = np.sqrt(np.mean((y_vec - yhat) ** 2))
mae = np.mean(np.abs(y_vec - yhat))

18.15 16.13 Forecasting future dates

To forecast future dates, we create the same features for future time indices and multiply by the fitted coefficient vector.

Code 
h = 120
future_dates = pd.date_range(df["ds"].iloc[-1] + pd.Timedelta(days=1), periods=h, freq="D")
future_t = np.arange(n, n + h)

future_X = make_design_matrix(
    future_dates,
    future_t,

future_scaled = (future_mat - means) / stds

18.16 16.14 Cross-validation for automated forecasting

Prophet popularized an analyst-friendly cross-validation interface with three key quantities:

  • initial: length of the first training window;
  • period: spacing between forecast cutoffs;
  • horizon: forecast horizon.

For cutoffs \(c_1,c_2,\ldots,c_M\), the model is trained using data up to cutoff \(c_m\) and predicts

\[ y(c_m+1),\ldots,y(c_m+H). \]

The evaluation table contains rows of the form

\[ (c_m,h,y_{c_m+h},\widehat y_{c_m+h\mid c_m}). \]

Then scores can be summarized by horizon.

Code

    X_train = make_design_matrix(
        train_df["ds"], train_df["t"], changepoints,
        weekly_order=weekly_order,
        yearly_order=yearly_order,
        holiday_days=holiday_days
    )
    X_mat = X_train.to_numpy(dtype=float)
    y_train = train_df["y"].to_numpy()

    means = X_mat.mean(axis=0)
    stds = X_mat.std(axis=0)
    means[0] = 0.0
    stds[0] = 1.0
    stds[stds == 0] = 1.0
    X_scaled = (X_mat - means) / stds

    penalty = np.eye(X_scaled.shape[1])
    penalty[0, 0] = 0.0
    beta = solve(X_scaled.T @ X_scaled + lam * penalty, X_scaled.T @ y_train)
    return beta, means, stds, X_train.columns

        test_df["ds"], test_df["t"], changepoints,
        weekly_order=weekly_order,
        yearly_order=yearly_order,
        holiday_days=holiday_days
    )
    X_mat = X_test.to_numpy(dtype=float)
    X_scaled = (X_mat - means) / stds
    return X_scaled @ beta

18.17 16.15 Forecast metrics in automated workflows

Chapter 15 introduced several metrics. Automated forecasting pipelines often report:

18.17.1 Absolute and squared error

\[ \operatorname{MAE}=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n|y_i-\widehat y_i|, \]

\[ \operatorname{RMSE}=\sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n(y_i-\widehat y_i)^2}. \]

MAE is robust and easy to interpret. RMSE penalizes large errors more strongly.

18.17.2 Percent error

\[ \operatorname{MAPE} =\frac{100}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \left|\frac{y_i-\widehat y_i}{y_i}\right|. \]

MAPE is easy to communicate, but it fails when \(y_i=0\) and behaves poorly when \(y_i\) is near zero.

18.17.3 Scaled error

A scaled error compares the model to a baseline. For seasonal period \(s\), the mean absolute scaled error is

\[ \operatorname{MASE} =\frac{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n|y_i-\widehat y_i|} {\frac{1}{T-s}\sum_{t=s+1}^T |y_t-y_{t-s}|}. \]

A MASE below \(1\) means the model improves over the seasonal naive baseline.

18.17.4 Relative error

A relative error compares directly to a benchmark forecast:

\[ \operatorname{RelMAE} =\frac{\operatorname{MAE}(\text{model})} {\operatorname{MAE}(\text{benchmark})}. \]

This is useful at scale because raw MAE values are not comparable across series with different units and scales.

TipUse benchmark-relative metrics

When reporting many automated forecasts, include a baseline-relative metric such as MASE or relative MAE. A raw MAPE of \(5\%\) may be excellent for one series and poor for another.

18.18 16.16 Hyperparameter tuning

Important Prophet-type tuning parameters include:

  1. changepoint flexibility: controls how much the trend can bend;
  2. seasonality flexibility: controls the size and smoothness of seasonal effects;
  3. holiday flexibility: controls how strongly holiday effects are allowed to move the forecast;
  4. seasonality mode: additive or multiplicative;
  5. Fourier order: number of Fourier terms for each seasonal period;
  6. changepoint range: portion of history where changepoints are allowed.

Hyperparameter tuning should be done by time-aware cross-validation. A generic procedure is:

  1. choose a grid of candidate parameters;
  2. for each parameter combination, run rolling-origin cross-validation;
  3. compute horizon-wise and aggregate metrics;
  4. choose a parameter set using validation performance and model simplicity;
  5. refit the selected model on all available training data;
  6. evaluate once on a final untouched test set, if available.

18.20 16.18 Analyst-in-the-loop modeling

Prophet-type models are designed for analyst intervention. The analyst can supply:

  • known capacities for logistic growth;
  • manually selected changepoints;
  • country, institutional, or domain-specific holidays;
  • custom seasonalities;
  • prior scales controlling smoothness;
  • outlier handling decisions;
  • external regressors.

This is a strength when domain knowledge is reliable. It is a weakness when analyst choices are not validated.

An analyst-in-the-loop workflow:

  1. Fit simple default model.
  2. Inspect trend, seasonality, holiday components.
  3. Compare with naive and seasonal naive baselines.
  4. Check residuals and forecast errors by horizon.
  5. Add known holidays or events.
  6. Tune prior scales using rolling-origin cross-validation.
  7. Refit and communicate the final assumptions.

18.21 16.19 Diagnostics and failure modes

A Prophet-type model can fail for several reasons.

18.21.1 Autocorrelated residuals

Because the model is closer to curve fitting than ARIMA, it may leave serial dependence in residuals. If residual ACF shows strong structure, consider:

  • adding autoregressive features;
  • using SARIMA/state-space models;
  • using a hybrid model;
  • using ML lag features.

18.21.2 Extrapolation error

Piecewise linear trend extrapolates the final trend regime. If the last trend segment is temporary, long-range forecasts can be poor.

18.21.3 Overfitting changepoints

Too much changepoint flexibility can fit noise and create unstable future trends.

18.21.4 Overfitting seasonalities

Large Fourier order can produce unrealistic seasonal wiggles.

18.21.5 Bad holiday design

Too many event indicators can fit random spikes. Missing important events can leave systematic errors.

18.21.6 Multiplicative structure ignored

If variance and seasonal amplitude grow with level, additive errors may be inappropriate.

18.21.7 Irregular data without context

Prophet can handle missing dates better than many ARIMA implementations, but missingness may still be informative. For example, no sales on a closed day is different from missing data.

18.22 16.20 Comparing Prophet-type models with SARIMA

SARIMA and Prophet-type models use different modeling philosophies.

Issue SARIMA Prophet-type additive model
Main structure lag dependence and shocks trend + seasonality + events
Stationarity often requires differencing trend handled directly
Seasonality seasonal lags and seasonal differencing Fourier seasonal basis
Holidays not built in by default natural event indicators
Missing dates can be inconvenient usually easier
Interpretability AR/MA coefficients and residuals decomposed components
Residual dependence modeled directly may remain unless added
Scaling to many series possible but tuning can be slow designed for fast iteration

A practical approach is not to ask, “Which model is universally better?” Instead ask:

Which model wins under the actual backtest, forecast horizon, metric, and operational constraints?

18.27 16.22 Common mistakes

  1. Treating Prophet as a magic black box. It is an additive model with assumptions.
  2. Skipping baselines. Seasonal naive forecasts are often strong.
  3. Using random cross-validation. This leaks future information.
  4. Over-tuning on the test set. Use validation backtests for tuning and preserve a final test set when possible.
  5. Overusing changepoints. Historical fit improves, future forecasts may worsen.
  6. Using high Fourier order automatically. More terms can mean more seasonal overfitting.
  7. Adding holidays without enough repeated evidence. One spike does not prove a stable holiday effect.
  8. Ignoring residual autocorrelation. Additive components may not capture lag dependence.
  9. Forgetting uncertainty. Point forecasts alone are insufficient for planning.
  10. Interpreting components causally. A holiday coefficient is an association unless causal design supports it.

## 16.1 Why automated forecasting?

Classical chapters built models by hand. For a single important series, hand modeling is valuable: plot the data, transform it, inspect the ACF/PACF, choose ARIMA or SARIMA orders, estimate parameters, diagnose residuals, and backtest forecasts. This workflow gives mathematical control.

At scale, however, an organization may need forecasts for hundreds, thousands, or millions of related time series:

- daily demand for many products;
- hourly load for many regions;
- weekly admissions for many programs;
- traffic, rideshare, health, finance, climate, or website metrics;
- multiple horizons and repeated updates.

Manual modeling becomes slow and inconsistent. Automated forecasting asks for a procedure that can:

1. fit reasonable models with minimal manual intervention;
2. incorporate common forecasting structure such as trend, seasonality, holidays, and outliers;
3. support analyst knowledge through interpretable parameters;
4. evaluate forecasts with rolling-origin backtests;
5. scale across many time series.

The lecture notes place Prophet after ARIMA/SARIMA, spectral analysis, and deep-learning approaches. That is the right position pedagogically: Prophet is not a replacement for time-series theory; it is a practical additive modeling strategy that borrows ideas from regression, splines, Fourier analysis, Bayesian regularization, and forecasting evaluation.

::: {.callout-note}
## Automated does not mean assumption-free
An automated model still makes assumptions. Prophet-type models assume that trend, seasonality, holiday effects, and noise give a useful decomposition. A good analyst checks whether this decomposition matches the forecasting problem.
:::

## 16.2 A taxonomy of forecasting models

A useful high-level taxonomy is:

1. **Time-domain statistical models**: AR, MA, ARMA, ARIMA, SARIMA, state-space models. These model dependence through lagged values and lagged shocks.
2. **Frequency-domain methods**: Fourier analysis, spectral density, periodograms, wavelets, filtering. These model oscillatory structure.
3. **Additive automated models**: Prophet-type models, generalized additive models, spline trend plus seasonality and holiday effects.
4. **Machine-learning feature models**: lag features, rolling statistics, calendar features, gradient boosting, random forests.
5. **Deep sequence models**: RNN, LSTM, GRU, temporal CNN, TCN, attention, and transformers.

The categories overlap. Prophet uses Fourier features for seasonality, regression-like additive components, changepoint regularization, and rolling-origin cross-validation. NeuralProphet and related models add autoregression or neural-network components.

A practical forecaster compares at least:

$$
\text{naive},\quad
\text{seasonal naive},\quad
\text{SARIMA},\quad
\text{Prophet-type additive model},\quad
\text{ML feature model}.
$$

The winning model depends on the data-generating mechanism, forecast horizon, cost function, and operational constraints.

## 16.3 The Prophet-style additive model

A Prophet-type model represents the observed series as

$$
y(t)=g(t)+s(t)+h(t)+\varepsilon_t.
$$

The components are:

- $g(t)$: trend, modeling nonperiodic long-term change;
- $s(t)$: seasonal component, modeling periodic behavior;
- $h(t)$: holiday or event component, modeling irregular known events;
- $\varepsilon_t$: residual error.

This is a **generalized additive forecasting model**. The model is interpretable because each term has a separate role.

For a fixed set of basis functions, the additive model can be written as a regression:

$$
y_t
=\beta_0
+\sum_{j=1}^{p_g}\beta^{(g)}_j G_j(t)
+\sum_{j=1}^{p_s}\beta^{(s)}_j S_j(t)
+\sum_{j=1}^{p_h}\beta^{(h)}_j H_j(t)
+\varepsilon_t.
$$

Here:

- $G_j(t)$ are trend/changepoint features;
- $S_j(t)$ are seasonal Fourier features;
- $H_j(t)$ are holiday/event indicators.

The model is simple in form, but the feature design and regularization are important.

::: {.theorem-box}
## Additive model as projection
If the noise has mean zero and the features are fixed, the least-squares fit is the orthogonal projection of the data vector $y$ onto the span of the trend, seasonal, and holiday feature vectors. Regularization shrinks this projection toward simpler trend and seasonal components.
:::

## 16.4 Trend component I: piecewise linear trend

A common trend model is piecewise linear. Suppose changepoints occur at

$$
s_1<s_2<\cdots<s_J.
$$

Define the hinge basis function

$$
(t-s_j)_+=\max(t-s_j,0).
$$

A continuous piecewise linear trend can be written as

$$
g(t)=m+kt+\sum_{j=1}^J\delta_j(t-s_j)_+.
$$

Before the first changepoint, the slope is $k$. After changepoint $s_j$, the slope changes by $\delta_j$. Therefore, after all changepoints up to time $t$, the local slope is

$$
k+\sum_{j:s_j<t}\delta_j.
$$

This form is closely related to linear splines. It is useful because:

1. the trend remains continuous;
2. changes in growth rate are explicit;
3. changepoints can be chosen automatically or specified by the analyst;
4. regularizing the coefficients $\delta_j$ controls flexibility.

### Changepoint regularization

If too many changepoints are allowed without regularization, the trend can overfit noise. A Laplace prior

$$
\delta_j\sim \operatorname{Laplace}(0,\tau)
$$

encourages many $\delta_j$ to be close to zero. In optimization terms, this behaves like an $\ell^1$ penalty:

$$
\min_\beta
\sum_{t=1}^T
\left(y_t-\widehat y_t\right)^2
+\lambda\sum_{j=1}^J|\delta_j|.
$$

Small $\tau$ or large $\lambda$ gives a smoother trend. Large $\tau$ or small $\lambda$ allows more changepoint flexibility.

::: {.callout-warning}
## Changepoints are not magic
A changepoint basis can explain historical bends in the curve, but it does not know whether those bends will continue. Long-horizon forecasts can be very sensitive to trend assumptions.
:::

## 16.5 Trend component II: saturating logistic growth

For growth that cannot increase forever, Prophet-type models may use a saturating logistic trend. The basic logistic curve is

$$
g(t)=\frac{C}{1+\exp(-k(t-m))},
$$

where:

- $C$ is the carrying capacity;
- $k$ is the growth rate;
- $m$ is an offset parameter controlling the midpoint.

This model is appropriate when there is a natural ceiling: population growth in an ecosystem, market adoption, number of users in a bounded region, or capacity-limited demand.

A time-varying capacity version replaces $C$ by $C(t)$:

$$
g(t)=\frac{C(t)}{1+\exp(-k(t-m))}.
$$

Changepoints can also modify the growth rate. Conceptually,

$$
k(t)=k+\sum_{j:s_j<t}\delta_j.
$$

The technical implementation adjusts the offset terms so that the curve remains continuous at changepoints.

::: {.callout-tip}
## Linear or logistic trend?
Use a logistic trend only when a meaningful capacity is available. Without a meaningful carrying capacity, a piecewise linear trend is usually easier to explain and diagnose.
:::

## 16.6 Seasonality through Fourier features

A periodic function with period $P$ satisfies

$$
s(t+P)=s(t).
$$

Prophet models seasonality using a truncated Fourier series:

$$
s(t)=
\sum_{n=1}^N
\left[
 a_n\cos\left(\frac{2\pi nt}{P}\right)
+b_n\sin\left(\frac{2\pi nt}{P}\right)
\right].
$$

For weekly seasonality in daily data, $P=7$. For yearly seasonality in daily data, $P\approx365.25$. For hourly data with daily seasonality, $P=24$ if time is measured in hours.

The Fourier order $N$ controls flexibility:

- small $N$: smooth seasonal curve;
- large $N$: more wiggly seasonal curve;
- too large $N$: overfitting.

The number of seasonal coefficients for one seasonal period is $2N$.

Fourier seasonality connects this chapter directly to spectral analysis. The difference is that here the Fourier basis is used as a deterministic regression basis, not as a full stochastic spectral model.

## 16.7 Holiday and event effects

Some effects are neither smooth trends nor regular seasonality. Examples include:

- Thanksgiving, Black Friday, Christmas, Lunar New Year;
- semester start, graduation, application deadlines;
- product launches;
- policy changes;
- weather events;
- one-time disruptions.

For holiday $i$, let $D_i$ be the set of dates associated with that holiday. Define

$$
H_i(t)=\mathbf 1_{\{t\in D_i\}}.
$$

A holiday component is

$$
h(t)=\sum_{i=1}^K\kappa_i H_i(t).
$$

In matrix form,

$$
h(t)=Z(t)^T\kappa.
$$

A Gaussian prior

$$
\kappa_i\sim N(0,\nu^2)
$$

shrinks holiday effects toward zero unless the data support them. This is important when many holidays or event windows are included.

Holiday effects can also include windows. For example, a Black Friday effect may include Thanksgiving Day, Black Friday, and Cyber Monday:

$$
H_{\text{BlackFriday}}(t)=1
\quad\text{if }t\in\{\text{Thu},\text{Fri},\text{Mon}\}
$$

for the relevant calendar year.

## 16.8 Additive versus multiplicative seasonality

The default additive model is

$$
y(t)=g(t)+s(t)+h(t)+\varepsilon_t.
$$

This assumes that seasonal fluctuations have roughly constant absolute size. For example, the seasonal amplitude is about $10$ units whether the trend is $100$ or $1000$.

A multiplicative seasonal model has the form

$$
y(t)=g(t)\{1+s(t)+h(t)\}+\varepsilon_t.
$$

This assumes that seasonal fluctuations scale with the level. For example, the seasonal effect might be about $10\%$ of the trend.

A common alternative is to log-transform the response:

$$
z(t)=\log y(t),
$$

then fit an additive model to $z(t)$. On the original scale, additive structure in logs becomes multiplicative structure.

::: {.callout-note}
## Rule of thumb
If seasonal swings get larger as the series level increases, consider multiplicative seasonality or a log transformation.
:::

## 16.11 Python example: Fourier features

A Fourier seasonal design matrix for period $P$ and order $N$ is

$$
X_{\text{season}}(t)=
\left(
\cos\frac{2\pi t}{P},
\sin\frac{2\pi t}{P},
\ldots,
\cos\frac{2\pi Nt}{P},
\sin\frac{2\pi Nt}{P}
\right).
$$

```{python}
#| label: ch16-fourier-features
#| fig-cap: "Fourier basis functions for weekly seasonality."
def fourier_features(t, period, order, prefix):
    t = np.asarray(t)
    data = {}
    for k in range(1, order + 1):
        data[f"{prefix}_cos_{k}"] = np.cos(2 * np.pi * k * t / period)
        data[f"{prefix}_sin_{k}"] = np.sin(2 * np.pi * k * t / period)
    return pd.DataFrame(data)
```

## 16.12 Python example: a simplified Prophet-like regression

We can build a Prophet-like model from scratch using:

1. intercept and linear trend;
2. hinge functions for changepoints;
3. Fourier features for weekly and yearly seasonality;
4. holiday indicators;
5. ridge regularization.

For clarity, we use a closed-form ridge estimator:

$$
\widehat\beta_{\lambda}
=(X^TX+\lambda I)^{-1}X^Ty.
$$

We usually do not penalize the intercept; below we set the intercept penalty to zero.

```{python}
#| label: ch16-build-design-matrix
from numpy.linalg import solve

def make_design_matrix(dates, t, changepoints, weekly_order=3, yearly_order=5, holiday_days=None):
    t = np.asarray(t)
    parts = []

    # Intercept and linear trend
    base = pd.DataFrame({
        "intercept": np.ones_like(t, dtype=float),
        "linear_t": t.astype(float)
    })
    parts.append(base)

    # Hinge functions for changepoints
    cp_data = {}
    for cp in changepoints:
        cp_data[f"hinge_{cp}"] = np.maximum(t - cp, 0)
    parts.append(pd.DataFrame(cp_data))

    # Seasonal Fourier features
    parts.append(fourier_features(t, period=7, order=weekly_order, prefix="weekly"))
    parts.append(fourier_features(t, period=365.25, order=yearly_order, prefix="yearly"))

    # Holiday indicators, with a small window around each event
    if holiday_days is not None:
        h = np.zeros_like(t, dtype=float)
        for day in holiday_days:
            h[np.abs(t - day) <= 2] = 1.0
        parts.append(pd.DataFrame({"holiday_window": h}))

    X = pd.concat(parts, axis=1)
    return X

changepoints = [180, 320, 500, 760, 900]
holiday_days = [80, 445, 810]
X = make_design_matrix(df["ds"], df["t"], changepoints, weekly_order=3, yearly_order=6, holiday_days=holiday_days)
X.head()
```

```{python}
#| label: ch16-fit-ridge-prophet-like
# Standardize non-intercept columns for numerical stability.
X_mat = X.to_numpy(dtype=float)
y_vec = df["y"].to_numpy()

means = X_mat.mean(axis=0)
stds = X_mat.std(axis=0)
means[0] = 0.0
stds[0] = 1.0
stds[stds == 0] = 1.0
X_scaled = (X_mat - means) / stds

lam = 25.0
penalty = np.eye(X_scaled.shape[1])
penalty[0, 0] = 0.0

beta = solve(X_scaled.T @ X_scaled + lam * penalty, X_scaled.T @ y_vec)
yhat = X_scaled @ beta

rmse = np.sqrt(np.mean((y_vec - yhat) ** 2))
mae = np.mean(np.abs(y_vec - yhat))
print(f"In-sample RMSE: {rmse:.3f}")
print(f"In-sample MAE:  {mae:.3f}")
```

## 16.14 Cross-validation for automated forecasting

Prophet popularized an analyst-friendly cross-validation interface with three key quantities:

- **initial**: length of the first training window;
- **period**: spacing between forecast cutoffs;
- **horizon**: forecast horizon.

For cutoffs $c_1,c_2,\ldots,c_M$, the model is trained using data up to cutoff $c_m$ and predicts

$$
y(c_m+1),\ldots,y(c_m+H).
$$

The evaluation table contains rows of the form

$$
(c_m,h,y_{c_m+h},\widehat y_{c_m+h\mid c_m}).
$$

Then scores can be summarized by horizon.

```{python}
#| label: ch16-rolling-origin-cv
#| fig-cap: "Rolling-origin cross-validation errors by horizon."
def fit_ridge_additive(train_df, changepoints, weekly_order, yearly_order, holiday_days, lam=25.0):
    X_train = make_design_matrix(
        train_df["ds"], train_df["t"], changepoints,
        weekly_order=weekly_order,
        yearly_order=yearly_order,
        holiday_days=holiday_days
    )
    X_mat = X_train.to_numpy(dtype=float)
    y_train = train_df["y"].to_numpy()

    means = X_mat.mean(axis=0)
    stds = X_mat.std(axis=0)
    means[0] = 0.0
    stds[0] = 1.0
    stds[stds == 0] = 1.0
    X_scaled = (X_mat - means) / stds

    penalty = np.eye(X_scaled.shape[1])
    penalty[0, 0] = 0.0
    beta = solve(X_scaled.T @ X_scaled + lam * penalty, X_scaled.T @ y_train)
    return beta, means, stds, X_train.columns


def predict_ridge_additive(test_df, beta, means, stds, changepoints, weekly_order, yearly_order, holiday_days):
    X_test = make_design_matrix(
        test_df["ds"], test_df["t"], changepoints,
        weekly_order=weekly_order,
        yearly_order=yearly_order,
        holiday_days=holiday_days
    )
    X_mat = X_test.to_numpy(dtype=float)
    X_scaled = (X_mat - means) / stds
    return X_scaled @ beta

horizon = 60
initial = 2 * 365
period = 90
cutoffs = list(range(initial, n - horizon, period))

records = []
for cutoff in cutoffs:
    train_df = df.iloc[:cutoff].copy()
    test_df = df.iloc[cutoff:cutoff + horizon].copy()

    beta_cv, means_cv, stds_cv, _ = fit_ridge_additive(
        train_df, changepoints=[180, 320, 500, 760],
        weekly_order=3, yearly_order=6,
        holiday_days=holiday_days,
        lam=25.0
    )
    pred = predict_ridge_additive(
        test_df, beta_cv, means_cv, stds_cv,
        changepoints=[180, 320, 500, 760],
        weekly_order=3, yearly_order=6,
        holiday_days=holiday_days
    )

    for h_i, (actual, forecast) in enumerate(zip(test_df["y"].to_numpy(), pred), start=1):
        records.append({
            "cutoff": cutoff,
            "horizon": h_i,
            "y": actual,
            "yhat": forecast,
            "error": actual - forecast,
            "abs_error": abs(actual - forecast)
        })

cv = pd.DataFrame(records)
mae_by_h = cv.groupby("horizon")["abs_error"].mean()

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(8, 3.5))
ax.plot(mae_by_h.index, mae_by_h.values)
ax.set_title("Cross-validation MAE by horizon")
ax.set_xlabel("forecast horizon")
ax.set_ylabel("MAE")
plt.show()
```

Averaging over horizons can hide systematic deterioration. Horizon-wise plots are often more informative than one number.

## 16.15 Forecast metrics in automated workflows

Chapter 15 introduced several metrics. Automated forecasting pipelines often report:

### Absolute and squared error

$$
\operatorname{MAE}=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n|y_i-\widehat y_i|,
$$

$$
\operatorname{RMSE}=\sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n(y_i-\widehat y_i)^2}.
$$

MAE is robust and easy to interpret. RMSE penalizes large errors more strongly.

### Percent error

$$
\operatorname{MAPE}
=\frac{100}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n
\left|\frac{y_i-\widehat y_i}{y_i}\right|.
$$

MAPE is easy to communicate, but it fails when $y_i=0$ and behaves poorly when $y_i$ is near zero.

### Scaled error

A scaled error compares the model to a baseline. For seasonal period $s$, the mean absolute scaled error is

$$
\operatorname{MASE}
=\frac{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n|y_i-\widehat y_i|}
{\frac{1}{T-s}\sum_{t=s+1}^T |y_t-y_{t-s}|}.
$$

A MASE below $1$ means the model improves over the seasonal naive baseline.

### Relative error

A relative error compares directly to a benchmark forecast:

$$
\operatorname{RelMAE}
=\frac{\operatorname{MAE}(\text{model})}
{\operatorname{MAE}(\text{benchmark})}.
$$

This is useful at scale because raw MAE values are not comparable across series with different units and scales.

::: {.callout-tip}
## Use benchmark-relative metrics
When reporting many automated forecasts, include a baseline-relative metric such as MASE or relative MAE. A raw MAPE of $5\%$ may be excellent for one series and poor for another.
:::

## 16.16 Hyperparameter tuning

Important Prophet-type tuning parameters include:

1. **changepoint flexibility**: controls how much the trend can bend;
2. **seasonality flexibility**: controls the size and smoothness of seasonal effects;
3. **holiday flexibility**: controls how strongly holiday effects are allowed to move the forecast;
4. **seasonality mode**: additive or multiplicative;
5. **Fourier order**: number of Fourier terms for each seasonal period;
6. **changepoint range**: portion of history where changepoints are allowed.

Hyperparameter tuning should be done by time-aware cross-validation. A generic procedure is:

1. choose a grid of candidate parameters;
2. for each parameter combination, run rolling-origin cross-validation;
3. compute horizon-wise and aggregate metrics;
4. choose a parameter set using validation performance and model simplicity;
5. refit the selected model on all available training data;
6. evaluate once on a final untouched test set, if available.

```{python}
#| label: ch16-small-grid-search
```

## 16.18 Analyst-in-the-loop modeling

Prophet-type models are designed for analyst intervention. The analyst can supply:

- known capacities for logistic growth;
- manually selected changepoints;
- country, institutional, or domain-specific holidays;
- custom seasonalities;
- prior scales controlling smoothness;
- outlier handling decisions;
- external regressors.

This is a strength when domain knowledge is reliable. It is a weakness when analyst choices are not validated.

An analyst-in-the-loop workflow:

1. Fit simple default model.
2. Inspect trend, seasonality, holiday components.
3. Compare with naive and seasonal naive baselines.
4. Check residuals and forecast errors by horizon.
5. Add known holidays or events.
6. Tune prior scales using rolling-origin cross-validation.
7. Refit and communicate the final assumptions.

## 16.19 Diagnostics and failure modes

A Prophet-type model can fail for several reasons.

### Autocorrelated residuals

Because the model is closer to curve fitting than ARIMA, it may leave serial dependence in residuals. If residual ACF shows strong structure, consider:

- adding autoregressive features;
- using SARIMA/state-space models;
- using a hybrid model;
- using ML lag features.

### Extrapolation error

Piecewise linear trend extrapolates the final trend regime. If the last trend segment is temporary, long-range forecasts can be poor.

### Overfitting changepoints

Too much changepoint flexibility can fit noise and create unstable future trends.

### Overfitting seasonalities

Large Fourier order can produce unrealistic seasonal wiggles.

### Bad holiday design

Too many event indicators can fit random spikes. Missing important events can leave systematic errors.

### Multiplicative structure ignored

If variance and seasonal amplitude grow with level, additive errors may be inappropriate.

### Irregular data without context

Prophet can handle missing dates better than many ARIMA implementations, but missingness may still be informative. For example, no sales on a closed day is different from missing data.

## 16.20 Comparing Prophet-type models with SARIMA

SARIMA and Prophet-type models use different modeling philosophies.

| Issue | SARIMA | Prophet-type additive model |
|---|---|---|
| Main structure | lag dependence and shocks | trend + seasonality + events |
| Stationarity | often requires differencing | trend handled directly |
| Seasonality | seasonal lags and seasonal differencing | Fourier seasonal basis |
| Holidays | not built in by default | natural event indicators |
| Missing dates | can be inconvenient | usually easier |
| Interpretability | AR/MA coefficients and residuals | decomposed components |
| Residual dependence | modeled directly | may remain unless added |
| Scaling to many series | possible but tuning can be slow | designed for fast iteration |

A practical approach is not to ask, “Which model is universally better?” Instead ask:

> Which model wins under the actual backtest, forecast horizon, metric, and operational constraints?

When using AI-generated forecasting code, verify:

1. the data are sorted by time;
2. no future values appear in training features;
3. train/test splitting respects forecast origin;
4. transformations are fit only on training data;
5. holidays/events are known at forecast time;
6. cross-validation uses the correct horizon;
7. baselines are included;
8. metrics match the application;
9. residuals and forecast errors are checked;
10. the model assumptions are communicated honestly.

## 16.22 Common mistakes

1. **Treating Prophet as a magic black box.** It is an additive model with assumptions.
2. **Skipping baselines.** Seasonal naive forecasts are often strong.
3. **Using random cross-validation.** This leaks future information.
4. **Over-tuning on the test set.** Use validation backtests for tuning and preserve a final test set when possible.
5. **Overusing changepoints.** Historical fit improves, future forecasts may worsen.
6. **Using high Fourier order automatically.** More terms can mean more seasonal overfitting.
7. **Adding holidays without enough repeated evidence.** One spike does not prove a stable holiday effect.
8. **Ignoring residual autocorrelation.** Additive components may not capture lag dependence.
9. **Forgetting uncertainty.** Point forecasts alone are insufficient for planning.
10. **Interpreting components causally.** A holiday coefficient is an association unless causal design supports it.

## 16.23 Chapter summary

Prophet-type models automate a practical and interpretable decomposition:

$$
y(t)=g(t)+s(t)+h(t)+\varepsilon_t.
$$

The trend $g(t)$ may be piecewise linear or saturating logistic. Seasonality $s(t)$ is represented using Fourier features. Holiday effects $h(t)$ are represented using event indicators. Regularization controls flexibility. Forecasting performance must be measured by time-aware cross-validation and benchmark-relative metrics.

The main lesson is that automated forecasting is a pipeline:

$$
\text{data cleaning}
\rightarrow
\text{component specification}
\rightarrow
\text{regularized fitting}
\rightarrow
\text{cross-validation}
\rightarrow
\text{diagnostics}
\rightarrow
\text{communication}.
$$
