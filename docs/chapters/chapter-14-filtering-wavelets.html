Part IV. Frequency-Domain and Multiresolution Methods
Chapter goal: learn how filters reshape a time series in the time and frequency domains, then extend the idea from global Fourier frequencies to local time-frequency analysis with wavelets. We use filters for smoothing, detrending, denoising, transient detection, and feature extraction for modern forecasting and sequence-learning models.

16.1 Learning objectives

After studying this chapter, you should be able to:

  1. define a linear time-invariant filter using convolution and the backshift operator;
  2. distinguish finite impulse response and infinite impulse response filters;
  3. compute the frequency response of a filter and interpret its magnitude and phase;
  4. prove that filtering transforms spectra by \(f_Y(\omega)=|A(\omega)|^2 f_X(\omega)\);
  5. explain moving-average smoothers, difference filters, seasonal-difference filters, and band-pass filters;
  6. understand why Fourier methods can miss local frequency changes;
  7. define short-time Fourier analysis as a first solution to localization;
  8. explain the Haar wavelet transform and multiresolution decomposition;
  9. use wavelet coefficients for denoising, compression, change-point detection, and feature extraction;
  10. use AI tools to critique filtering choices, leakage risks, and interpretability claims.

16.2 14.1 Why filtering?

In Chapters 12 and 13 we learned that a stationary time series can be viewed through its frequency content. This chapter asks a complementary question:

How can we transform a time series so that certain frequencies, trends, or localized events become easier to see?

A filter is a rule that takes an input series \(\{X_t\}\) and produces an output series \(\{Y_t\}\). A simple example is the centered moving average

\[ Y_t=\frac{1}{2m+1}\sum_{j=-m}^m X_{t-j}. \]

This filter smooths the series by replacing each observation with a local average. Short-scale variation is reduced; slow variation is preserved. In frequency language, this is a low-pass filter.

A first difference

\[ Y_t=\nabla X_t=X_t-X_{t-1} \]

does the opposite: it removes constant levels and emphasizes short-scale changes. It is a high-pass filter.

Filtering is used for several purposes:

  • remove noise before forecasting;
  • remove trend or seasonality before fitting stationary models;
  • isolate business-cycle or seasonal frequencies;
  • detect transients, jumps, bursts, or anomalies;
  • construct features for machine-learning models;
  • precondition data before deep sequence models.
WarningFiltering changes the problem

Filtering is not a neutral preprocessing step. A filtered series has a different dependence structure from the original series. Before filtering, ask: Which part of the signal am I trying to preserve, and which part am I trying to suppress?

16.3 14.2 Linear time-invariant filters

A linear time-invariant filter has the form

\[ Y_t=\sum_{j=-\infty}^{\infty} a_j X_{t-j}, \]

where the sequence \(\{a_j\}\) is called the filter kernel, impulse response, or filter coefficients.

The filter is linear because

\[ L(\alpha X_t+\beta Z_t)=\alpha L(X_t)+\beta L(Z_t), \]

and it is time-invariant because the same coefficients \(a_j\) are used at every time \(t\).

A sufficient condition for the sum to be well behaved is absolute summability:

\[ \sum_{j=-\infty}^{\infty}|a_j|<\infty. \]

When the filter uses only finitely many coefficients, it is called a finite impulse response filter, or FIR filter. When infinitely many coefficients are used, it is called an infinite impulse response filter, or IIR filter.

16.3.1 Backshift notation

Using the backshift operator \(B X_t=X_{t-1}\), we can write

\[ Y_t=a(B)X_t, \]

where

\[ a(B)=\sum_{j=-\infty}^{\infty}a_jB^j. \]

For example, the first-difference filter is

\[ \nabla X_t=(1-B)X_t. \]

The seasonal-difference filter with period \(s\) is

\[ \nabla_s X_t=(1-B^s)X_t=X_t-X_{t-s}. \]

The centered three-point moving average is

\[ Y_t=\frac{1}{3}(X_{t-1}+X_t+X_{t+1}) =\left(\frac{1}{3}B+\frac{1}{3}+\frac{1}{3}B^{-1}\right)X_t. \]

NoteCausal versus centered filters

A forecasting filter must be causal: it can use \(X_t,X_{t-1},\ldots\) but not future values. A centered smoother is useful for retrospective visualization, but it leaks future information if used inside a forecasting pipeline.

16.4 14.3 Frequency response

To understand a filter, feed it a complex sinusoid

\[ X_t=e^{2\pi i\omega t}. \]

Then

\[ \begin{aligned} Y_t &=\sum_j a_j X_{t-j} \\ &=\sum_j a_j e^{2\pi i\omega(t-j)} \\ &=e^{2\pi i\omega t}\sum_j a_j e^{-2\pi i\omega j}. \end{aligned} \]

Thus the sinusoid remains at the same frequency. Only its amplitude and phase are changed. The multiplier

\[ A(\omega)=\sum_{j=-\infty}^{\infty}a_j e^{-2\pi i\omega j} \]

is called the frequency response of the filter.

The magnitude

\[ |A(\omega)| \]

measures how much the filter amplifies or attenuates frequency \(\omega\). The squared magnitude

\[ |A(\omega)|^2 \]

measures the effect on power or variance at frequency \(\omega\).

The phase

\[ \arg A(\omega) \]

measures how much the filter shifts that frequency component in time.

16.4.1 Examples

The first-difference filter has coefficients \(a_0=1\) and \(a_1=-1\). Therefore,

\[ A(\omega)=1-e^{-2\pi i\omega}. \]

Using \(|1-e^{-ix}|^2=2-2\cos x=4\sin^2(x/2)\), we get

\[ |A(\omega)|^2=4\sin^2(\pi\omega). \]

At \(\omega=0\), the response is \(0\), so differencing removes constant components. At \(\omega=1/2\), the response is \(4\), so high-frequency oscillations are amplified.

For the two-sided moving average with window \(2m+1\),

\[ a_j=\frac{1}{2m+1},\qquad j=-m,\ldots,m. \]

The response is

\[ A(\omega)=\frac{1}{2m+1}\sum_{j=-m}^{m}e^{-2\pi i\omega j}. \]

This response is large near \(\omega=0\) and small at many higher frequencies, so the filter smooths the series.

16.5 14.4 Filtering and spectral density

Suppose \(\{X_t\}\) is a zero-mean stationary time series with spectral density \(f_X(\omega)\), and define

\[ Y_t=\sum_j a_jX_{t-j}. \]

Then the output process \(\{Y_t\}\) has spectral density

\[ f_Y(\omega)=|A(\omega)|^2f_X(\omega). \]

This formula is one of the central results of frequency-domain time-series analysis.

16.5.1 Derivation

The autocovariance of \(Y_t\) is

\[ \begin{aligned} \gamma_Y(h) &=\operatorname{Cov}(Y_{t+h},Y_t)\\ &=\operatorname{Cov}\left(\sum_j a_jX_{t+h-j},\sum_k a_kX_{t-k}\right)\\ &=\sum_j\sum_k a_ja_k\gamma_X(h-j+k). \end{aligned} \]

Taking the Fourier transform gives

\[ \begin{aligned} f_Y(\omega) &=\sum_h \gamma_Y(h)e^{-2\pi i\omega h}\\ &=\sum_h\sum_j\sum_k a_ja_k\gamma_X(h-j+k)e^{-2\pi i\omega h}. \end{aligned} \]

Let \(r=h-j+k\), so \(h=r+j-k\). Then

\[ \begin{aligned} f_Y(\omega) &=\sum_j\sum_k a_ja_k e^{-2\pi i\omega(j-k)} \sum_r \gamma_X(r)e^{-2\pi i\omega r}\\ &=\left(\sum_j a_je^{-2\pi i\omega j}\right) \left(\sum_k a_ke^{2\pi i\omega k}\right)f_X(\omega)\\ &=A(\omega)\overline{A(\omega)}f_X(\omega)\\ &=|A(\omega)|^2f_X(\omega). \end{aligned} \]

ImportantFilter design principle

To suppress a frequency band, make \(|A(\omega)|^2\) small on that band. To preserve a frequency band, make \(|A(\omega)|^2\) close to \(1\) on that band.

16.6 14.5 Common filters in time-series analysis

16.6.1 Moving-average smoother

A trailing moving average of length \(m\) is

\[ Y_t=\frac{1}{m}\sum_{j=0}^{m-1}X_{t-j}. \]

It is causal and therefore can be used in real time. However, it introduces a delay because it averages current and past observations.

A centered moving average is

\[ Y_t=\frac{1}{2m+1}\sum_{j=-m}^{m}X_{t-j}. \]

It is symmetric and has no phase distortion, but it uses future values and therefore should not be used to create forecasting features unless it is computed only inside the training window and with careful leakage prevention.

16.6.2 Difference filter

The first difference

\[ Y_t=X_t-X_{t-1} \]

removes constants and weakens low-frequency trend. In ARIMA notation, this is \(\nabla X_t=(1-B)X_t\).

The second difference

\[ \nabla^2X_t=X_t-2X_{t-1}+X_{t-2} \]

removes linear trends.

16.6.3 Seasonal-difference filter

For seasonal period \(s\),

\[ Y_t=X_t-X_{t-s}. \]

The response is

\[ A(\omega)=1-e^{-2\pi i\omega s}, \]

so

\[ |A(\omega)|^2=4\sin^2(\pi s\omega). \]

This filter removes components at frequencies

\[ \omega=0,\frac{1}{s},\frac{2}{s},\ldots, \]

up to aliases on the discrete frequency grid.

16.6.4 Exponential smoothing as an IIR filter

Simple exponential smoothing is

\[ Y_t=\alpha X_t+(1-\alpha)Y_{t-1},\qquad 0<\alpha<1. \]

Iterating gives

\[ Y_t=\alpha\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}(1-\alpha)^jX_{t-j}. \]

Thus exponential smoothing is a causal IIR low-pass filter with geometrically decaying weights.

16.9 14.8 Filters, leakage, and forecasting validation

Filtering can introduce look-ahead leakage. Suppose the goal is to forecast \(X_{t+1}\) from information available at time \(t\). If we create a centered moving-average feature

\[ Z_t=\frac{1}{3}(X_{t-1}+X_t+X_{t+1}), \]

then \(Z_t\) contains the future value \(X_{t+1}\). A supervised-learning model trained on \(Z_t\) is given information that would not be available at prediction time.

For forecasting, causal features are safer:

\[ Z_t=\frac{1}{m}\sum_{j=0}^{m-1}X_{t-j}. \]

Even then, validation must respect time order. The filter must be fit or applied inside each training window, and no operation should use future observations when producing training or validation features.

ImportantPractical rule

For exploratory plots, centered smoothers are often acceptable. For forecasting features, use causal filters unless you can prove that no future information enters the feature construction.

16.10 14.9 From Fourier to local time-frequency analysis

The Fourier transform represents a series using global sinusoids. This works well when frequency content is stable over time. But many real sequences are nonstationary in frequency:

  • a machine sensor develops a high-frequency vibration only near failure;
  • an ECG signal has local transient events;
  • a financial return series has short bursts of volatility;
  • a traffic series changes its weekly pattern after a disruption;
  • a speech signal changes frequency content from phoneme to phoneme.

A single periodogram tells us which frequencies are present overall, but not when they occur.

16.10.1 Short-time Fourier transform

A natural solution is to compute Fourier transforms on local windows. Given a window function \(g\), the short-time Fourier transform is

\[ \operatorname{STFT}_x(u,\omega) =\sum_t x_t g(t-u)e^{-2\pi i\omega t}. \]

The parameter \(u\) localizes time, and \(\omega\) localizes frequency. The squared magnitude

\[ |\operatorname{STFT}_x(u,\omega)|^2 \]

is a local time-frequency energy measure.

The limitation is that the window width is fixed. A narrow window gives good time resolution but poor frequency resolution. A wide window gives good frequency resolution but poor time resolution.

Wavelets address this by changing the window width with scale.

16.11 14.10 Wavelets and multiresolution analysis

A wavelet is a localized oscillatory function. Unlike a sine wave, which extends forever, a wavelet is concentrated in time.

Starting from a mother wavelet \(\psi(t)\), define scaled and translated wavelets by

\[ \psi_{a,b}(t)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{|a|}}\psi\left(\frac{t-b}{a}\right), \]

where \(a\) is scale and \(b\) is location.

  • Small \(a\): compressed wavelet, good for high-frequency local events.
  • Large \(a\): stretched wavelet, good for low-frequency broad behavior.

The continuous wavelet transform is

\[ W_x(a,b)=\int x(t)\overline{\psi_{a,b}(t)}\,dt. \]

For discrete data, we often use dyadic scales \(a=2^j\) and translations aligned with the sample grid. This leads to the discrete wavelet transform.

NoteWavelet idea

Fourier analysis asks: how much frequency \(\omega\) is present in the whole signal? Wavelet analysis asks: how much scale \(a\) is present near time \(b\)?

16.12 14.11 The Haar wavelet

The simplest wavelet is the Haar wavelet:

\[ \psi(t)= \begin{cases} 1, & 0\leq t<1/2,\\ -1, & 1/2\leq t<1,\\ 0, & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases} \]

For a pair of observations \((x_0,x_1)\), the Haar transform computes

\[ a=\frac{x_0+x_1}{\sqrt 2}, \qquad d=\frac{x_0-x_1}{\sqrt 2}. \]

The coefficient \(a\) is an average, and \(d\) is a detail. The inverse transform is

\[ x_0=\frac{a+d}{\sqrt 2}, \qquad x_1=\frac{a-d}{\sqrt 2}. \]

For a longer sequence of length \(n=2^J\), the Haar transform repeatedly averages neighboring pairs and stores the differences. This gives a multiresolution representation:

\[ \text{data} = \text{coarse approximation} + \text{details at several scales}. \]

The Haar transform is orthonormal, so it preserves energy:

\[ \sum_t x_t^2 = \sum_k c_k^2, \]

where \(c_k\) are the wavelet coefficients.

16.13 14.12 Python: Haar wavelet transform from scratch

The next code block implements a simple orthonormal Haar transform. This avoids hiding the mathematics inside a library.

Code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt


def haar_transform(x):
    """Return the orthonormal Haar wavelet coefficients of a length-2^J vector."""
    x = np.asarray(x, dtype=float).copy()
    n = len(x)
    coeffs = []
    current = x.copy()
    while len(current) > 1:
        avg = (current[0::2] + current[1::2]) / np.sqrt(2)
        detail = (current[0::2] - current[1::2]) / np.sqrt(2)
        coeffs.append(detail)
        current = avg
    coeffs.append(current)
    # Store as [coarsest average, finest-to-coarsest details reversed]
    return coeffs[-1], coeffs[-2::-1]


def inverse_haar_transform(coarse, details):
    """Invert coefficients returned by haar_transform."""
    current = np.asarray(coarse, dtype=float)
    for detail in details:
        detail = np.asarray(detail, dtype=float)
        out = np.empty(current.size * 2)
        out[0::2] = (current + detail) / np.sqrt(2)
        out[1::2] = (current - detail) / np.sqrt(2)
        current = out
    return current

n = 256
t = np.arange(n)
clean = np.sin(2 * np.pi * t / 64)
clean += 0.7 * ((t >= 110) & (t <= 135))  # local transient
noisy = clean + 0.35 * rng.normal(size=n)

coarse, details = haar_transform(noisy)
reconstructed = inverse_haar_transform(coarse, details)

print("Maximum reconstruction error:", np.max(np.abs(noisy - reconstructed)))
print("Time-domain energy:", np.sum(noisy ** 2))
print("Wavelet-domain energy:", np.sum(coarse ** 2) + sum(np.sum(d ** 2) for d in details))

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 4))
plt.plot(t, noisy, label="noisy signal", alpha=0.65)
plt.plot(t, clean, label="clean signal", linewidth=2)
plt.xlabel("time")
plt.ylabel("value")
plt.title("Signal with a local transient")
plt.legend()

16.14 14.13 Wavelet denoising

Wavelet denoising is based on a simple observation. For many signals, meaningful structure is represented by a relatively small number of large wavelet coefficients, while noise is spread across many small coefficients.

A common procedure is:

  1. compute wavelet coefficients;
  2. estimate the noise level;
  3. shrink or remove small detail coefficients;
  4. invert the wavelet transform.

A standard universal threshold is

\[ \lambda=\widehat\sigma\sqrt{2\log n}. \]

A robust estimate of \(\sigma\) from the finest-scale detail coefficients is

\[ \widehat\sigma=\frac{\operatorname{median}(|d_{J,k}|)}{0.6745}. \]

Hard thresholding uses

\[ \eta_\lambda(d)= \begin{cases} d, & |d|>\lambda,\\ 0, & |d|\leq \lambda. \end{cases} \]

Soft thresholding uses

\[ \eta_\lambda(d)=\operatorname{sign}(d)(|d|-\lambda)_+. \]

Soft thresholding is continuous and often produces smoother reconstructions.

Code 
def soft_threshold(x, lam):
    return np.sign(x) * np.maximum(np.abs(x) - lam, 0.0)

coarse, details = haar_transform(noisy)
sigma_hat = np.median(np.abs(details[-1])) / 0.6745
lam = sigma_hat * np.sqrt(2 * np.log(n))

details_soft = [soft_threshold(d, lam) for d in details]
denoised = inverse_haar_transform(coarse, details_soft)

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 4))
plt.plot(t, noisy, label="noisy", alpha=0.45)
plt.plot(t, clean, label="clean", linewidth=2)
plt.plot(t, denoised, label="wavelet denoised", linewidth=2)
plt.xlabel("time")
plt.ylabel("value")
plt.title("Wavelet denoising with soft thresholding")
plt.legend()
plt.show()

16.16 14.15 Wavelets as features for machine learning

Wavelet transforms are not only visualization tools. They can be used to construct features for supervised or unsupervised learning.

For a time window \(x_{t-L+1:t}\), possible wavelet features include:

  • energy at each scale;
  • maximum absolute detail coefficient at each scale;
  • number of coefficients exceeding a threshold;
  • entropy of normalized wavelet energy;
  • scale where the largest event occurs;
  • reconstructed low-frequency trend;
  • reconstructed high-frequency residual.

These features are useful for:

  • anomaly detection in sensors;
  • classification of ECG or EEG segments;
  • machine-health monitoring;
  • event detection in audio and seismic data;
  • multiscale forecasting features for tree-based models.

A scale-energy feature is

\[ E_j=\sum_k d_{j,k}^2, \]

where \(d_{j,k}\) is the detail coefficient at scale \(j\) and location \(k\).

The normalized energy distribution is

\[ p_j=\frac{E_j}{\sum_\ell E_\ell}. \]

A wavelet entropy feature is

\[ H=-\sum_j p_j\log p_j. \]

Low entropy means the signal’s variation is concentrated at a few scales. High entropy means variation is spread over many scales.

Code 
coarse, details = haar_transform(noisy)
scale_energies = np.array([np.sum(d ** 2) for d in details])
p = scale_energies / scale_energies.sum()
entropy = -np.sum(p * np.log(p + 1e-12))

## 14.1 Why filtering?

In Chapters 12 and 13 we learned that a stationary time series can be viewed through its frequency content. This chapter asks a complementary question:

> How can we transform a time series so that certain frequencies, trends, or localized events become easier to see?

A **filter** is a rule that takes an input series $\{X_t\}$ and produces an output series $\{Y_t\}$. A simple example is the centered moving average

$$
Y_t=\frac{1}{2m+1}\sum_{j=-m}^m X_{t-j}.
$$

This filter smooths the series by replacing each observation with a local average. Short-scale variation is reduced; slow variation is preserved. In frequency language, this is a **low-pass filter**.

A first difference

$$
Y_t=\nabla X_t=X_t-X_{t-1}
$$

does the opposite: it removes constant levels and emphasizes short-scale changes. It is a **high-pass filter**.

Filtering is used for several purposes:

- remove noise before forecasting;
- remove trend or seasonality before fitting stationary models;
- isolate business-cycle or seasonal frequencies;
- detect transients, jumps, bursts, or anomalies;
- construct features for machine-learning models;
- precondition data before deep sequence models.

::: {.callout-warning}
## Filtering changes the problem
Filtering is not a neutral preprocessing step. A filtered series has a different dependence structure from the original series. Before filtering, ask: Which part of the signal am I trying to preserve, and which part am I trying to suppress?
:::

## 14.2 Linear time-invariant filters

A **linear time-invariant filter** has the form

$$
Y_t=\sum_{j=-\infty}^{\infty} a_j X_{t-j},
$$

where the sequence $\{a_j\}$ is called the **filter kernel**, **impulse response**, or **filter coefficients**.

The filter is **linear** because

$$
L(\alpha X_t+\beta Z_t)=\alpha L(X_t)+\beta L(Z_t),
$$

and it is **time-invariant** because the same coefficients $a_j$ are used at every time $t$.

A sufficient condition for the sum to be well behaved is absolute summability:

$$
\sum_{j=-\infty}^{\infty}|a_j|<\infty.
$$

When the filter uses only finitely many coefficients, it is called a **finite impulse response** filter, or FIR filter. When infinitely many coefficients are used, it is called an **infinite impulse response** filter, or IIR filter.

### Backshift notation

Using the backshift operator $B X_t=X_{t-1}$, we can write

$$
Y_t=a(B)X_t,
$$

where

$$
a(B)=\sum_{j=-\infty}^{\infty}a_jB^j.
$$

For example, the first-difference filter is

$$
\nabla X_t=(1-B)X_t.
$$

The seasonal-difference filter with period $s$ is

$$
\nabla_s X_t=(1-B^s)X_t=X_t-X_{t-s}.
$$

The centered three-point moving average is

$$
Y_t=\frac{1}{3}(X_{t-1}+X_t+X_{t+1})
=\left(\frac{1}{3}B+\frac{1}{3}+\frac{1}{3}B^{-1}\right)X_t.
$$

::: {.callout-note}
## Causal versus centered filters
A forecasting filter must be causal: it can use $X_t,X_{t-1},\ldots$ but not future values. A centered smoother is useful for retrospective visualization, but it leaks future information if used inside a forecasting pipeline.
:::

## 14.3 Frequency response

To understand a filter, feed it a complex sinusoid

$$
X_t=e^{2\pi i\omega t}.
$$

Then

$$
\begin{aligned}
Y_t
&=\sum_j a_j X_{t-j} \\
&=\sum_j a_j e^{2\pi i\omega(t-j)} \\
&=e^{2\pi i\omega t}\sum_j a_j e^{-2\pi i\omega j}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus the sinusoid remains at the same frequency. Only its amplitude and phase are changed. The multiplier

$$
A(\omega)=\sum_{j=-\infty}^{\infty}a_j e^{-2\pi i\omega j}
$$

is called the **frequency response** of the filter.

The magnitude

$$
|A(\omega)|
$$

measures how much the filter amplifies or attenuates frequency $\omega$. The squared magnitude

$$
|A(\omega)|^2
$$

measures the effect on power or variance at frequency $\omega$.

The phase

$$
\arg A(\omega)
$$

measures how much the filter shifts that frequency component in time.

### Examples

The first-difference filter has coefficients $a_0=1$ and $a_1=-1$. Therefore,

$$
A(\omega)=1-e^{-2\pi i\omega}.
$$

Using $|1-e^{-ix}|^2=2-2\cos x=4\sin^2(x/2)$, we get

$$
|A(\omega)|^2=4\sin^2(\pi\omega).
$$

At $\omega=0$, the response is $0$, so differencing removes constant components. At $\omega=1/2$, the response is $4$, so high-frequency oscillations are amplified.

For the two-sided moving average with window $2m+1$,

$$
a_j=\frac{1}{2m+1},\qquad j=-m,\ldots,m.
$$

The response is

$$
A(\omega)=\frac{1}{2m+1}\sum_{j=-m}^{m}e^{-2\pi i\omega j}.
$$

This response is large near $\omega=0$ and small at many higher frequencies, so the filter smooths the series.

## 14.4 Filtering and spectral density

Suppose $\{X_t\}$ is a zero-mean stationary time series with spectral density $f_X(\omega)$, and define

$$
Y_t=\sum_j a_jX_{t-j}.
$$

Then the output process $\{Y_t\}$ has spectral density

$$
f_Y(\omega)=|A(\omega)|^2f_X(\omega).
$$

This formula is one of the central results of frequency-domain time-series analysis.

### Derivation

The autocovariance of $Y_t$ is

$$
\begin{aligned}
\gamma_Y(h)
&=\operatorname{Cov}(Y_{t+h},Y_t)\\
&=\operatorname{Cov}\left(\sum_j a_jX_{t+h-j},\sum_k a_kX_{t-k}\right)\\
&=\sum_j\sum_k a_ja_k\gamma_X(h-j+k).
\end{aligned}
$$

Taking the Fourier transform gives

$$
\begin{aligned}
f_Y(\omega)
&=\sum_h \gamma_Y(h)e^{-2\pi i\omega h}\\
&=\sum_h\sum_j\sum_k a_ja_k\gamma_X(h-j+k)e^{-2\pi i\omega h}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Let $r=h-j+k$, so $h=r+j-k$. Then

$$
\begin{aligned}
f_Y(\omega)
&=\sum_j\sum_k a_ja_k e^{-2\pi i\omega(j-k)}
\sum_r \gamma_X(r)e^{-2\pi i\omega r}\\
&=\left(\sum_j a_je^{-2\pi i\omega j}\right)
\left(\sum_k a_ke^{2\pi i\omega k}\right)f_X(\omega)\\
&=A(\omega)\overline{A(\omega)}f_X(\omega)\\
&=|A(\omega)|^2f_X(\omega).
\end{aligned}
$$

::: {.callout-important}
## Filter design principle
To suppress a frequency band, make $|A(\omega)|^2$ small on that band. To preserve a frequency band, make $|A(\omega)|^2$ close to $1$ on that band.
:::

## 14.5 Common filters in time-series analysis

### Moving-average smoother

A trailing moving average of length $m$ is

$$
Y_t=\frac{1}{m}\sum_{j=0}^{m-1}X_{t-j}.
$$

It is causal and therefore can be used in real time. However, it introduces a delay because it averages current and past observations.

A centered moving average is

$$
Y_t=\frac{1}{2m+1}\sum_{j=-m}^{m}X_{t-j}.
$$

It is symmetric and has no phase distortion, but it uses future values and therefore should not be used to create forecasting features unless it is computed only inside the training window and with careful leakage prevention.

### Difference filter

The first difference

$$
Y_t=X_t-X_{t-1}
$$

removes constants and weakens low-frequency trend. In ARIMA notation, this is $\nabla X_t=(1-B)X_t$.

The second difference

$$
\nabla^2X_t=X_t-2X_{t-1}+X_{t-2}
$$

removes linear trends.

### Seasonal-difference filter

For seasonal period $s$,

$$
Y_t=X_t-X_{t-s}.
$$

The response is

$$
A(\omega)=1-e^{-2\pi i\omega s},
$$

so

$$
|A(\omega)|^2=4\sin^2(\pi s\omega).
$$

This filter removes components at frequencies

$$
\omega=0,\frac{1}{s},\frac{2}{s},\ldots,
$$

up to aliases on the discrete frequency grid.

### Exponential smoothing as an IIR filter

Simple exponential smoothing is

$$
Y_t=\alpha X_t+(1-\alpha)Y_{t-1},\qquad 0<\alpha<1.
$$

Iterating gives

$$
Y_t=\alpha\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}(1-\alpha)^jX_{t-j}.
$$

Thus exponential smoothing is a causal IIR low-pass filter with geometrically decaying weights.

## 14.8 Filters, leakage, and forecasting validation

Filtering can introduce **look-ahead leakage**. Suppose the goal is to forecast $X_{t+1}$ from information available at time $t$. If we create a centered moving-average feature

$$
Z_t=\frac{1}{3}(X_{t-1}+X_t+X_{t+1}),
$$

then $Z_t$ contains the future value $X_{t+1}$. A supervised-learning model trained on $Z_t$ is given information that would not be available at prediction time.

For forecasting, causal features are safer:

$$
Z_t=\frac{1}{m}\sum_{j=0}^{m-1}X_{t-j}.
$$

Even then, validation must respect time order. The filter must be fit or applied inside each training window, and no operation should use future observations when producing training or validation features.

::: {.callout-important}
## Practical rule
For exploratory plots, centered smoothers are often acceptable. For forecasting features, use causal filters unless you can prove that no future information enters the feature construction.
:::

## 14.9 From Fourier to local time-frequency analysis

The Fourier transform represents a series using global sinusoids. This works well when frequency content is stable over time. But many real sequences are **nonstationary in frequency**:

- a machine sensor develops a high-frequency vibration only near failure;
- an ECG signal has local transient events;
- a financial return series has short bursts of volatility;
- a traffic series changes its weekly pattern after a disruption;
- a speech signal changes frequency content from phoneme to phoneme.

A single periodogram tells us which frequencies are present overall, but not when they occur.

### Short-time Fourier transform

A natural solution is to compute Fourier transforms on local windows. Given a window function $g$, the short-time Fourier transform is

$$
\operatorname{STFT}_x(u,\omega)
=\sum_t x_t g(t-u)e^{-2\pi i\omega t}.
$$

The parameter $u$ localizes time, and $\omega$ localizes frequency. The squared magnitude

$$
|\operatorname{STFT}_x(u,\omega)|^2
$$

is a local time-frequency energy measure.

The limitation is that the window width is fixed. A narrow window gives good time resolution but poor frequency resolution. A wide window gives good frequency resolution but poor time resolution.

Wavelets address this by changing the window width with scale.

## 14.10 Wavelets and multiresolution analysis

A **wavelet** is a localized oscillatory function. Unlike a sine wave, which extends forever, a wavelet is concentrated in time.

Starting from a mother wavelet $\psi(t)$, define scaled and translated wavelets by

$$
\psi_{a,b}(t)=\frac{1}{\sqrt{|a|}}\psi\left(\frac{t-b}{a}\right),
$$

where $a$ is scale and $b$ is location.

- Small $a$: compressed wavelet, good for high-frequency local events.
- Large $a$: stretched wavelet, good for low-frequency broad behavior.

The continuous wavelet transform is

$$
W_x(a,b)=\int x(t)\overline{\psi_{a,b}(t)}\,dt.
$$

For discrete data, we often use dyadic scales $a=2^j$ and translations aligned with the sample grid. This leads to the discrete wavelet transform.

::: {.callout-note}
## Wavelet idea
Fourier analysis asks: how much frequency $\omega$ is present in the whole signal? Wavelet analysis asks: how much scale $a$ is present near time $b$?
:::

## 14.11 The Haar wavelet

The simplest wavelet is the **Haar wavelet**:

$$
\psi(t)=
\begin{cases}
1, & 0\leq t<1/2,\\
-1, & 1/2\leq t<1,\\
0, & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
$$

For a pair of observations $(x_0,x_1)$, the Haar transform computes

$$
a=\frac{x_0+x_1}{\sqrt 2},
\qquad
 d=\frac{x_0-x_1}{\sqrt 2}.
$$

The coefficient $a$ is an average, and $d$ is a detail. The inverse transform is

$$
x_0=\frac{a+d}{\sqrt 2},
\qquad
x_1=\frac{a-d}{\sqrt 2}.
$$

For a longer sequence of length $n=2^J$, the Haar transform repeatedly averages neighboring pairs and stores the differences. This gives a multiresolution representation:

$$
\text{data} = \text{coarse approximation} + \text{details at several scales}.
$$

The Haar transform is orthonormal, so it preserves energy:

$$
\sum_t x_t^2 = \sum_k c_k^2,
$$

where $c_k$ are the wavelet coefficients.

## 14.12 Python: Haar wavelet transform from scratch

The next code block implements a simple orthonormal Haar transform. This avoids hiding the mathematics inside a library.

```{python}
#| label: ch14-haar-transform-from-scratch
#| fig-cap: "A Haar wavelet transform separates a signal into coarse and detail coefficients."
#| warning: false
#| message: false

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt


def haar_transform(x):
    """Return the orthonormal Haar wavelet coefficients of a length-2^J vector."""
    x = np.asarray(x, dtype=float).copy()
    n = len(x)
    coeffs = []
    current = x.copy()
    while len(current) > 1:
        avg = (current[0::2] + current[1::2]) / np.sqrt(2)
        detail = (current[0::2] - current[1::2]) / np.sqrt(2)
        coeffs.append(detail)
        current = avg
    coeffs.append(current)
    # Store as [coarsest average, finest-to-coarsest details reversed]
    return coeffs[-1], coeffs[-2::-1]


def inverse_haar_transform(coarse, details):
    """Invert coefficients returned by haar_transform."""
    current = np.asarray(coarse, dtype=float)
    for detail in details:
        detail = np.asarray(detail, dtype=float)
        out = np.empty(current.size * 2)
        out[0::2] = (current + detail) / np.sqrt(2)
        out[1::2] = (current - detail) / np.sqrt(2)
        current = out
    return current

n = 256
t = np.arange(n)
clean = np.sin(2 * np.pi * t / 64)
clean += 0.7 * ((t >= 110) & (t <= 135))  # local transient
noisy = clean + 0.35 * rng.normal(size=n)

coarse, details = haar_transform(noisy)
reconstructed = inverse_haar_transform(coarse, details)

print("Maximum reconstruction error:", np.max(np.abs(noisy - reconstructed)))
print("Time-domain energy:", np.sum(noisy ** 2))
print("Wavelet-domain energy:", np.sum(coarse ** 2) + sum(np.sum(d ** 2) for d in details))

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 4))
plt.plot(t, noisy, label="noisy signal", alpha=0.65)
plt.plot(t, clean, label="clean signal", linewidth=2)
plt.xlabel("time")
plt.ylabel("value")
plt.title("Signal with a local transient")
plt.legend()
plt.show()
```

## 14.13 Wavelet denoising

Wavelet denoising is based on a simple observation. For many signals, meaningful structure is represented by a relatively small number of large wavelet coefficients, while noise is spread across many small coefficients.

A common procedure is:

1. compute wavelet coefficients;
2. estimate the noise level;
3. shrink or remove small detail coefficients;
4. invert the wavelet transform.

A standard universal threshold is

$$
\lambda=\widehat\sigma\sqrt{2\log n}.
$$

A robust estimate of $\sigma$ from the finest-scale detail coefficients is

$$
\widehat\sigma=\frac{\operatorname{median}(|d_{J,k}|)}{0.6745}.
$$

Hard thresholding uses

$$
\eta_\lambda(d)=
\begin{cases}
d, & |d|>\lambda,\\
0, & |d|\leq \lambda.
\end{cases}
$$

Soft thresholding uses

$$
\eta_\lambda(d)=\operatorname{sign}(d)(|d|-\lambda)_+.
$$

Soft thresholding is continuous and often produces smoother reconstructions.

```{python}
#| label: ch14-wavelet-denoising
#| fig-cap: "Wavelet thresholding removes small coefficients and reconstructs a denoised signal."
#| warning: false
#| message: false


def soft_threshold(x, lam):
    return np.sign(x) * np.maximum(np.abs(x) - lam, 0.0)

coarse, details = haar_transform(noisy)
sigma_hat = np.median(np.abs(details[-1])) / 0.6745
lam = sigma_hat * np.sqrt(2 * np.log(n))

details_soft = [soft_threshold(d, lam) for d in details]
denoised = inverse_haar_transform(coarse, details_soft)

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 4))
plt.plot(t, noisy, label="noisy", alpha=0.45)
plt.plot(t, clean, label="clean", linewidth=2)
plt.plot(t, denoised, label="wavelet denoised", linewidth=2)
plt.xlabel("time")
plt.ylabel("value")
plt.title("Wavelet denoising with soft thresholding")
plt.legend()
plt.show()

print("Noise estimate:", sigma_hat)
print("Universal threshold:", lam)
print("RMSE noisy:", np.sqrt(np.mean((noisy - clean) ** 2)))
print("RMSE denoised:", np.sqrt(np.mean((denoised - clean) ** 2)))
```

::: {.callout-warning}
## Denoising is not always forecasting improvement
A denoised curve may look better but forecast worse if the threshold removes predictive short-term variation. Always compare filtered and unfiltered pipelines using time-aware validation.
:::

## 14.15 Wavelets as features for machine learning

Wavelet transforms are not only visualization tools. They can be used to construct features for supervised or unsupervised learning.

For a time window $x_{t-L+1:t}$, possible wavelet features include:

- energy at each scale;
- maximum absolute detail coefficient at each scale;
- number of coefficients exceeding a threshold;
- entropy of normalized wavelet energy;
- scale where the largest event occurs;
- reconstructed low-frequency trend;
- reconstructed high-frequency residual.

These features are useful for:

- anomaly detection in sensors;
- classification of ECG or EEG segments;
- machine-health monitoring;
- event detection in audio and seismic data;
- multiscale forecasting features for tree-based models.

A scale-energy feature is

$$
E_j=\sum_k d_{j,k}^2,
$$

where $d_{j,k}$ is the detail coefficient at scale $j$ and location $k$.

The normalized energy distribution is

$$
p_j=\frac{E_j}{\sum_\ell E_\ell}.
$$

A wavelet entropy feature is

$$
H=-\sum_j p_j\log p_j.
$$

Low entropy means the signal's variation is concentrated at a few scales. High entropy means variation is spread over many scales.

```{python}
#| label: ch14-wavelet-features
#| fig-cap: "Wavelet scale energies summarize multiscale structure."
#| warning: false
#| message: false

coarse, details = haar_transform(noisy)
scale_energies = np.array([np.sum(d ** 2) for d in details])
p = scale_energies / scale_energies.sum()
entropy = -np.sum(p * np.log(p + 1e-12))

plt.figure(figsize=(8, 4))
plt.bar(np.arange(1, len(scale_energies) + 1), scale_energies)
plt.xlabel("detail scale")
plt.ylabel("energy")
plt.title(f"Wavelet scale energies; entropy = {entropy:.3f}")
plt.show()
```

## 14.18 Common mistakes

1. **Using centered filters for forecasting features.**  
   This leaks future observations into the training data.

2. **Assuming smoothing always improves forecasting.**  
   Smoothing can remove short-term signals that are useful for prediction.

3. **Interpreting filter output without understanding the frequency response.**  
   Always ask which frequencies were amplified and which were suppressed.

4. **Treating wavelet coefficients as magic anomaly scores.**  
   Large coefficients may indicate jumps, bursts, edges, noise, or boundary artifacts.

5. **Ignoring boundary effects.**  
   Convolution and wavelet transforms need conventions near the beginning and end of the sample.

6. **Comparing filtered and unfiltered models on different validation schemes.**  
   Use the same rolling-origin evaluation design.

7. **Forgetting scale.**  
   A wavelet coefficient is meaningful only relative to its scale, noise level, and boundary position.
