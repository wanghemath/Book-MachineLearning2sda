Part II. Stationarity and Linear Time-Domain Models
Chapter goal: build the central linear time-domain model class: linear processes, autoregressive models, moving-average models, and ARMA models. The emphasis is on backshift notation, causality, invertibility, root conditions, impulse responses, autocovariance, simulation, and model interpretation.

6.1 Learning objectives

After studying this chapter, you should be able to:

  1. use the backshift operator \(B\) to write AR, MA, and ARMA models compactly;
  2. explain why ARMA models are linear filters applied to white noise;
  3. distinguish AR(\(p\)), MA(\(q\)), ARMA(\(p,q\)), and general linear processes;
  4. compute causal MA\((\infty)\) representations by matching coefficients;
  5. state and apply root conditions for stationarity, causality, and invertibility;
  6. derive the autocovariance and ACF of MA(1) and AR(1);
  7. explain why model redundancy occurs when AR and MA polynomials have common factors;
  8. simulate AR, MA, and ARMA processes in Python;
  9. connect impulse responses, roots, and time-domain behavior;
  10. use AI tools to check algebraic derivations and code while verifying the mathematical assumptions yourself.

6.2 4.1 Motivation: linear filters as models of memory

In Chapter 3, we studied stationarity and autocorrelation. We saw that white noise has no linear memory, MA(1) has memory that cuts off after one lag, and AR(1) has memory that decays geometrically. In this chapter we organize these examples into a general modeling framework.

The starting idea is simple:

NoteTime-domain modeling principle

A stationary time series can often be modeled as a transformed version of white noise. The transformation is a linear filter that spreads the effect of each shock forward or backward through time.

Let \(\{W_t\}\) be white noise with mean \(0\) and variance \(\sigma^2\):

\[ E[W_t]=0,\qquad \operatorname{Var}(W_t)=\sigma^2,\qquad \operatorname{Cov}(W_t,W_s)=0 \quad (s\ne t). \]

A linear time-series model describes \(X_t\) as a weighted sum of shocks:

\[ X_t=\sum_{j=-\infty}^{\infty} a_j W_{t-j}. \]

For forecasting, the most important special case is the causal case:

\[ X_t=\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\psi_j W_{t-j}. \]

This means the present depends on current and past shocks, not future shocks. The coefficient \(\psi_j\) is the effect at time \(t\) of a shock that occurred \(j\) periods ago. The sequence

\[ \psi_0,\psi_1,\psi_2,\dots \]

is called the impulse-response sequence.

TipInterpretation

If \(W_t\) is a surprise at time \(t\), then \(\psi_j\) tells us how much of that surprise remains visible \(j\) steps later. ARMA models are compact ways to describe these impulse responses.

6.3 4.2 The backshift operator

The backshift operator \(B\) moves a time series one step into the past:

\[ BX_t=X_{t-1}. \]

More generally,

\[ B^jX_t=X_{t-j}. \]

For example,

\[ (1-B)X_t=X_t-X_{t-1} \]

is the first difference used in exploratory analysis and ARIMA modeling.

Backshift notation allows us to rewrite long recurrence equations as polynomial equations. For example,

\[ X_t=\phi X_{t-1}+W_t \]

can be written as

\[ (1-\phi B)X_t=W_t. \]

The expression

\[ \phi(B)=1-\phi B \]

is an operator polynomial. It acts on the sequence \(\{X_t\}\).

ImportantAlgebra with operators

Backshift notation looks like ordinary polynomial algebra, but the objects are operators applied to time series. The notation is powerful because many time-series questions become questions about polynomial roots and series expansions.

6.4 4.3 Linear processes

A zero-mean process \(\{X_t\}\) is a linear process if it can be written as

\[ X_t=\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\psi_jW_{t-j}, \]

where \(\{W_t\}\sim WN(0,\sigma^2)\) and

\[ \sum_{j=0}^{\infty}|\psi_j|<\infty. \]

Using backshift notation,

\[ X_t=\psi(B)W_t, \]

where

\[ \psi(B)=\psi_0+\psi_1B+\psi_2B^2+\cdots. \]

The absolute summability condition ensures the infinite sum is well behaved and the process has finite variance.

6.4.1 Mean and variance

If \(E[W_t]=0\), then

\[ E[X_t]=\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\psi_jE[W_{t-j}]=0. \]

The variance is

\[ \operatorname{Var}(X_t) =\operatorname{Var}\left(\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\psi_jW_{t-j}\right) =\sigma^2\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\psi_j^2, \]

because the white-noise terms are uncorrelated.

6.4.2 Autocovariance of a linear process

For \(h\ge0\),

\[ \gamma(h)=\operatorname{Cov}(X_{t+h},X_t). \]

Using

\[ X_{t+h}=\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\psi_jW_{t+h-j} \]

and

\[ X_t=\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\psi_kW_{t-k}, \]

only terms with equal noise indices contribute to covariance. The condition

\[ t+h-j=t-k \]

is equivalent to \(j=k+h\). Therefore,

\[ \gamma(h)=\sigma^2\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\psi_{k+h}\psi_k, \qquad h\ge0. \]

Because autocovariance is symmetric,

\[ \gamma(-h)=\gamma(h). \]

NoteCentral formula

For a causal linear process \[ X_t=\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\psi_jW_{t-j}, \] the autocovariance is \[ \gamma(h)=\sigma^2\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\psi_j\psi_{j+|h|}. \] This formula is one of the most useful bridges between model parameters and ACF behavior.

6.5 4.4 Moving-average models

A moving-average model of order \(q\), written MA(\(q\)), is

\[ X_t=W_t+\theta_1W_{t-1}+\cdots+\theta_qW_{t-q}, \]

where \(\{W_t\}\sim WN(0,\sigma^2)\).

Using the backshift operator,

\[ X_t=(1+\theta_1B+\cdots+\theta_qB^q)W_t. \]

Define the MA polynomial

\[ \theta(B)=1+\theta_1B+\cdots+\theta_qB^q. \]

Then

\[ X_t=\theta(B)W_t. \]

An MA(\(q\)) process is a finite linear process with

\[ \psi_0=1,\qquad \psi_j=\theta_j \quad (1\le j\le q),\qquad \psi_j=0\quad(j>q). \]

Therefore every MA(\(q\)) process is weakly stationary.

6.5.1 Autocovariance of MA(\(q\))

For \(h\ge0\),

\[ \gamma(h)=\sigma^2\sum_{j=0}^{q-h}\theta_j\theta_{j+h},\qquad 0\le h\le q, \]

where \(\theta_0=1\). For \(h>q\),

\[ \gamma(h)=0. \]

Therefore the ACF of an MA(\(q\)) process cuts off after lag \(q\).

TipDiagnostic signature of MA models

An MA(\(q\)) process has theoretical ACF equal to zero after lag \(q\). In real data, the sample ACF will not be exactly zero after lag \(q\), but it should fluctuate near zero if the model is appropriate.

6.6 4.5 Example: MA(1)

Let

\[ X_t=W_t+\theta W_{t-1}. \]

Then

\[ E[X_t]=0. \]

The variance is

\[ \gamma(0)=\operatorname{Var}(X_t) =\operatorname{Var}(W_t+\theta W_{t-1}) =\sigma^2(1+\theta^2). \]

The lag-one autocovariance is

\[ \gamma(1)=\operatorname{Cov}(X_{t+1},X_t). \]

Since

\[ X_{t+1}=W_{t+1}+\theta W_t, \]

we have

\[ \gamma(1) =\operatorname{Cov}(W_{t+1}+\theta W_t, W_t+\theta W_{t-1}) =\theta\operatorname{Var}(W_t) =\theta\sigma^2. \]

For \(|h|>1\),

\[ \gamma(h)=0. \]

Thus

\[ \rho(1)=\frac{\theta}{1+\theta^2}, \]

and

\[ \rho(h)=0\qquad |h|>1. \]

Notice that the map

\[ \theta\mapsto \frac{\theta}{1+\theta^2} \]

is not one-to-one. In fact,

\[ \frac{\theta}{1+\theta^2}=\frac{1/\theta}{1+1/\theta^2} \]

for \(\theta\ne0\). This is the first warning that MA models may not have unique parameterizations unless we impose invertibility.

6.7 4.6 Autoregressive models

An autoregressive model of order \(p\), written AR(\(p\)), is

\[ X_t=\phi_1X_{t-1}+\phi_2X_{t-2}+\cdots+\phi_pX_{t-p}+W_t, \]

where \(\{W_t\}\sim WN(0,\sigma^2)\).

Using backshift notation,

\[ (1-\phi_1B-\phi_2B^2-\cdots-\phi_pB^p)X_t=W_t. \]

Define the AR polynomial

\[ \phi(B)=1-\phi_1B-\phi_2B^2-\cdots-\phi_pB^p. \]

Then

\[ \phi(B)X_t=W_t. \]

An AR process explains the present by regressing it on its own past plus a new shock.

NoteAR versus MA intuition

An MA model remembers a finite number of shocks. An AR model remembers past observations. Because each past observation already contains earlier shocks, a causal AR process usually has infinitely long but decaying memory.

6.8 4.7 Example: AR(1)

Consider

\[ X_t=\phi X_{t-1}+W_t, \]

or

\[ (1-\phi B)X_t=W_t. \]

If \(|\phi|<1\), then we can formally invert \(1-\phi B\):

\[ \frac{1}{1-\phi B}=1+\phi B+\phi^2B^2+\cdots. \]

Thus

\[ X_t=(1+\phi B+\phi^2B^2+\cdots)W_t \]

and

\[ X_t=\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\phi^jW_{t-j}. \]

This is a causal linear process with impulse-response weights

\[ \psi_j=\phi^j. \]

6.8.1 Variance

Using the linear-process variance formula,

\[ \gamma(0)=\sigma^2\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\phi^{2j} =\frac{\sigma^2}{1-\phi^2}. \]

6.8.2 Autocovariance

For \(h\ge0\),

\[ \gamma(h)=\sigma^2\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\phi^j\phi^{j+h} =\sigma^2\phi^h\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\phi^{2j} =\frac{\sigma^2\phi^h}{1-\phi^2}. \]

Therefore

\[ \rho(h)=\phi^h,\qquad h\ge0, \]

and by symmetry,

\[ \rho(h)=\phi^{|h|}. \]

If \(0<\phi<1\), the ACF decays positively and geometrically. If \(-1<\phi<0\), the ACF alternates signs and decays in magnitude.

6.8.3 The random walk as a boundary case

If \(\phi=1\), then

\[ X_t=X_{t-1}+W_t, \]

which is the random walk. It is not stationary. The root of \(1-\phi z=0\) is \(z=1\), exactly on the unit circle. This boundary between stationary and nonstationary behavior is central in ARIMA modeling.

6.9 4.8 ARMA models

An autoregressive moving-average model, ARMA(\(p,q\)), combines AR and MA structure:

\[ X_t-\phi_1X_{t-1}-\cdots-\phi_pX_{t-p} = W_t+\theta_1W_{t-1}+\cdots+\theta_qW_{t-q}. \]

Using backshift notation,

\[ \phi(B)X_t=\theta(B)W_t, \]

where

\[ \phi(B)=1-\phi_1B-\cdots-\phi_pB^p \]

and

\[ \theta(B)=1+\theta_1B+\cdots+\theta_qB^q. \]

Special cases:

\[ \text{AR}(p)=\text{ARMA}(p,0), \]

and

\[ \text{MA}(q)=\text{ARMA}(0,q). \]

ARMA models are popular because they can represent many short-memory stationary dependence patterns with relatively few parameters.

ImportantARMA modeling goal

ARMA models do not try to explain every observation independently. They model the dependence structure: how shocks propagate and how the present depends on the past.

6.10 4.9 Parameter redundancy and common factors

Usually we require that \(\phi(z)\) and \(\theta(z)\) have no common factors. Otherwise, the same process can be written as a higher-order ARMA model than necessary.

For example, suppose

\[ X_t=W_t. \]

Then \(X_t\) is white noise. But it also satisfies

\[ (1-B+0.5B^2)X_t=(1-B+0.5B^2)W_t. \]

This looks like an ARMA(2,2) model, but the same polynomial appears on both sides and cancels. The process is still just white noise.

WarningModel order is not only notation

An ARMA\((p,q)\) representation is meaningful only after removing common factors. Otherwise, the apparent order may be artificially high.

6.11 4.10 Causality

A process is causal with respect to \(\{W_t\}\) if it can be written as

\[ X_t=\psi(B)W_t=\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\psi_jW_{t-j}, \]

where

\[ \sum_{j=0}^{\infty}|\psi_j|<\infty. \]

Causality means \(X_t\) is determined by current and past innovations only. This is the form we want for forecasting: the future should not be needed to explain the present.

For an ARMA model

\[ \phi(B)X_t=\theta(B)W_t, \]

a causal representation exists when

\[ \psi(B)=\frac{\theta(B)}{\phi(B)} \]

has a convergent power-series expansion in nonnegative powers of \(B\).

6.11.1 Root condition for causality

For the ARMA model with no common factors, the process is causal if all roots of

\[ \phi(z)=0 \]

lie outside the unit circle:

\[ |z|>1. \]

For AR(1),

\[ \phi(z)=1-\phi z. \]

The root is

\[ z=\frac{1}{\phi}. \]

The condition \(|z|>1\) is equivalent to

\[ |\phi|<1. \]

6.12 4.11 Invertibility

A process is invertible with respect to \(\{W_t\}\) if the innovations can be recovered from current and past observations:

\[ W_t=\pi(B)X_t=\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\pi_jX_{t-j}, \]

where

\[ \sum_{j=0}^{\infty}|\pi_j|<\infty. \]

For an ARMA model,

\[ \phi(B)X_t=\theta(B)W_t, \]

invertibility means

\[ W_t=\frac{\phi(B)}{\theta(B)}X_t \]

has a stable power-series expansion.

6.12.1 Root condition for invertibility

The model is invertible if all roots of

\[ \theta(z)=0 \]

lie outside the unit circle:

\[ |z|>1. \]

For MA(1),

\[ X_t=W_t+\theta W_{t-1}=(1+\theta B)W_t. \]

The MA polynomial is

\[ \theta(z)=1+\theta z. \]

Its root is

\[ z=-\frac{1}{\theta}. \]

The root is outside the unit circle exactly when

\[ |\theta|<1. \]

If \(|\theta|<1\), then

\[ W_t=\frac{1}{1+\theta B}X_t =(1-\theta B+\theta^2B^2-\theta^3B^3+\cdots)X_t. \]

Thus

\[ W_t=X_t-\theta X_{t-1}+\theta^2X_{t-2}-\theta^3X_{t-3}+\cdots. \]

NoteWhy invertibility matters

Invertibility gives a unique and stable way to recover shocks from observations. Without it, different MA parameter values can produce the same autocovariance structure, making estimation and interpretation ambiguous.

6.13 4.12 Stationarity, causality, and invertibility: summary

For

\[ \phi(B)X_t=\theta(B)W_t, \]

with \(\phi\) and \(\theta\) sharing no common factors:

  • a stationary solution exists if the roots of \(\phi(z)=0\) do not lie on the unit circle;
  • a causal stationary solution exists if the roots of \(\phi(z)=0\) lie outside the unit circle;
  • the model is invertible if the roots of \(\theta(z)=0\) lie outside the unit circle.

In most statistical software, fitted ARMA models are typically constrained or reported in a causal and invertible parameterization because that parameterization is identifiable and forecastable.

ImportantPractical convention

For standard time-series forecasting, when we say an ARMA model is valid, we usually mean it is stationary, causal, and invertible unless stated otherwise.

6.14 4.13 Matching coefficients: computing \(\psi\)-weights

Suppose

\[ \phi(B)X_t=\theta(B)W_t \]

and we want the causal representation

\[ X_t=\psi(B)W_t. \]

Substituting gives

\[ \phi(B)\psi(B)W_t=\theta(B)W_t. \]

Thus

\[ \phi(B)\psi(B)=\theta(B). \]

Write

\[ \psi(B)=\psi_0+\psi_1B+\psi_2B^2+\cdots. \]

Because \(\theta_0=1\), we usually have

\[ \psi_0=1. \]

For ARMA(\(p,q\)), matching the coefficient of \(B^k\) gives

\[ \psi_k-\phi_1\psi_{k-1}-\cdots-\phi_p\psi_{k-p}=\theta_k, \]

where

\[ \theta_k=0\quad \text{for } k>q, \]

and

\[ \psi_j=0\quad \text{for }j<0. \]

Equivalently,

\[ \psi_k=\theta_k+\phi_1\psi_{k-1}+\cdots+\phi_p\psi_{k-p}. \]

This recursion computes the impulse response.

6.14.1 Example: ARMA(1,1)

Let

\[ (1-\phi B)X_t=(1+\theta B)W_t. \]

We seek

\[ X_t=(\psi_0+\psi_1B+\psi_2B^2+\cdots)W_t. \]

Matching coefficients in

\[ (1-\phi B)(\psi_0+\psi_1B+\psi_2B^2+\cdots)=1+\theta B \]

gives

\[ \psi_0=1, \]

\[ \psi_1-\phi\psi_0=\theta, \]

so

\[ \psi_1=\phi+\theta. \]

For \(k\ge2\),

\[ \psi_k-\phi\psi_{k-1}=0, \]

hence

\[ \psi_k=\phi\psi_{k-1}. \]

Therefore

\[ \psi_k=(\phi+\theta)\phi^{k-1},\qquad k\ge1. \]

If \(|\phi|<1\), this impulse response decays geometrically.

6.15 4.14 Autocovariance of ARMA models

One way to compute autocovariances is to use the causal linear-process representation:

\[ X_t=\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\psi_jW_{t-j}. \]

Then

\[ \gamma(h)=\sigma^2\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\psi_j\psi_{j+|h|}. \]

This is conceptually simple and computationally useful. In practice, we truncate the infinite sum after enough terms when the \(\psi_j\) decay quickly.

Another way is to multiply the ARMA equation by \(X_{t-h}\) and take expectations. For large enough lags, the right-hand side involving finite MA terms disappears, leaving a homogeneous difference equation for \(\gamma(h)\).

For example, an AR(\(p\)) process satisfies the Yule-Walker equations:

\[ \gamma(h)=\phi_1\gamma(h-1)+\cdots+\phi_p\gamma(h-p),\qquad h\ge1. \]

These equations show that the ACF of an AR process follows the same recurrence structure as the process itself.

6.16 4.15 ACF and PACF signatures

Classical model identification often uses the sample ACF and PACF.

6.16.1 MA(\(q\))

The theoretical ACF cuts off after lag \(q\):

\[ \rho(h)=0\qquad h>q. \]

The PACF usually decays gradually.

6.16.2 AR(\(p\))

The theoretical PACF cuts off after lag \(p\), while the ACF usually decays gradually according to the roots of the AR polynomial.

6.16.3 ARMA(\(p,q\))

Both ACF and PACF usually decay gradually. The orders \(p\) and \(q\) are not usually obvious from visual inspection alone.

WarningDo not overread ACF/PACF plots

Sample ACF and PACF are noisy. They are diagnostic tools, not proofs. Model identification should combine plots, parameter estimation, information criteria, residual diagnostics, and forecasting validation.

6.18 4.17 Python example: simulate AR, MA, and ARMA processes

The following code simulates several classical processes. It uses only NumPy for the simulation so that the recursion is visible.

Code 
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(7339)

n = 400
burnin = 200
total = n + burnin
sigma = 1.0

w = rng.normal(0, sigma, size=total)

# AR(1): X_t = phi X_{t-1} + W_t
phi_ar = 0.75
x_ar1_full = np.zeros(total)

for t in range(1, total):
    x_ar1_full[t] = phi_ar * x_ar1_full[t - 1] + w[t]

6.20 4.19 Python example: root checking

AR and MA roots are central for causality and invertibility.

For an AR(\(p\)) model

\[ \phi(z)=1-\phi_1z-\cdots-\phi_pz^p. \]

For an MA(\(q\)) model

\[ \theta(z)=1+\theta_1z+\cdots+\theta_qz^q. \]

In Python, numpy.roots expects coefficients in descending powers. The following helper functions compute roots of these polynomials.

Code 
import numpy as np

def ar_roots(phi):
    """Roots of phi(z)=1-phi1 z-...-phip z^p."""
    phi = np.asarray(phi, dtype=float)
    coeff_desc = np.r_[-phi[::-1], 1.0]
    return np.roots(coeff_desc)

def ma_roots(theta):
    """Roots of theta(z)=1+theta1 z+...+thetaq z^q."""
    theta = np.asarray(theta, dtype=float)
    coeff_desc = np.r_[theta[::-1], 1.0]
    return np.roots(coeff_desc)

def outside_unit_circle(roots, tol=1e-10):
    return np.all(np.abs(roots) > 1 + tol)

6.21 4.20 Python example: compute \(\psi\)-weights recursively

The following function computes the causal impulse-response coefficients of an ARMA model by matching coefficients in

\[ \phi(B)\psi(B)=\theta(B). \]

Code 
import numpy as np

def psi_weights(ar=None, ma=None, horizon=30):
    """
    Compute psi_0,...,psi_horizon for
    phi(B) X_t = theta(B) W_t,
    phi(B)=1 - ar[0]B - ...,
    theta(B)=1 + ma[0]B + ... .
    """
    ar = np.asarray([] if ar is None else ar, dtype=float)
    ma = np.asarray([] if ma is None else ma, dtype=float)
    p = len(ar)
    q = len(ma)
    psi = np.zeros(horizon + 1)
    psi[0] = 1.0
    for k in range(1, horizon + 1):
        theta_k = ma[k - 1] if k <= q else 0.0
        ar_part = 0.0
        for j in range(1, p + 1):
            if k - j >= 0:
                ar_part += ar[j - 1] * psi[k - j]
        psi[k] = theta_k + ar_part
    return psi

6.22 4.21 Python example: autocovariance from \(\psi\)-weights

For a causal linear process,

\[ \gamma(h)=\sigma^2\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\psi_j\psi_{j+h}. \]

def acf_from_psi(psi, max_lag, sigma2=1.0):
    psi = np.asarray(psi, dtype=float)
    gamma = []
    for h in range(max_lag + 1):
        gamma_h = sigma2 * np.sum(psi[:len(psi) - h] * psi[h:])
        gamma.append(gamma_h)
    gamma = np.array(gamma)
    return gamma / gamma[0]

6.25 4.24 Connection to machine learning

ARMA models can be viewed as a simple and interpretable machine-learning architecture for sequential data.

6.25.1 AR as linear supervised learning on lag features

An AR(\(p\)) model is

\[ X_t=\phi_1X_{t-1}+\cdots+\phi_pX_{t-p}+W_t. \]

This is linear regression with features

\[ (X_{t-1},X_{t-2},\dots,X_{t-p}). \]

The difference from ordinary regression is that the features are generated from the same dependent time series, so validation and uncertainty require time-series methods.

6.25.2 MA as latent-shock modeling

An MA(\(q\)) model uses unobserved shocks:

\[ X_t=W_t+\theta_1W_{t-1}+\cdots+\theta_qW_{t-q}. \]

The predictors \(W_{t-j}\) are not directly observed. They must be inferred through model fitting.

6.25.3 ARMA as a linear recurrent system

An ARMA model combines recurrence through \(X_{t-j}\) and shock filtering through \(W_{t-j}\). This makes it an ancestor of modern sequence models: it has hidden memory, impulse responses, and recursive prediction. RNNs, LSTMs, temporal convolutional networks, and state-space sequence models generalize these ideas in nonlinear and high-dimensional ways.

6.26 AI connection

A modern AI model may learn complex nonlinear sequence representations, but the same questions remain:

  • What past information matters?
  • How long does memory persist?
  • Are residuals still dependent?
  • Is the model stable when rolled forward?
  • Does it generalize under time-aware validation?

ARMA models give a mathematically transparent setting for learning these questions before moving to deep models.

6.27 4.25 Common mistakes

6.27.1 Mistake 1: confusing stationarity with causality

A stationary solution may exist even when the model is not causal. For forecasting, we usually need a causal representation because we cannot use future shocks.

6.27.2 Mistake 2: forgetting invertibility

An MA model can be stationary for any parameter value, but not every parameter value gives a unique and stable innovation representation. Invertibility is needed for canonical estimation and interpretation.

6.27.3 Mistake 3: treating AR and MA roots the same way without checking signs

AR and MA polynomials are written with different sign conventions:

\[ \phi(z)=1-\phi_1z-\cdots-\phi_pz^p, \]

while

\[ \theta(z)=1+\theta_1z+\cdots+\theta_qz^q. \]

When coding root checks, pay close attention to the coefficient order and signs.

6.27.4 Mistake 4: believing sample ACF cutoffs are exact

The theoretical ACF of MA(\(q\)) cuts off. The sample ACF only fluctuates around zero after lag \(q\).

6.27.5 Mistake 5: fitting ARMA to nonstationary data

ARMA models are for stationary data. If the time plot or ACF suggests trend, seasonality, or changing variance, consider transformation, detrending, differencing, ARIMA, SARIMA, or another model class.

6.31 4.29 Chapter summary

ARMA models are the central stationary linear models of classical time-series analysis. A linear process writes a time series as a weighted sum of white-noise shocks:

\[ X_t=\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\psi_jW_{t-j}. \]

An MA(\(q\)) model has finite shock memory:

\[ X_t=W_t+\theta_1W_{t-1}+\cdots+\theta_qW_{t-q}. \]

An AR(\(p\)) model regresses the present on the past:

\[ X_t=\phi_1X_{t-1}+\cdots+\phi_pX_{t-p}+W_t. \]

An ARMA(\(p,q\)) model combines both:

\[ \phi(B)X_t=\theta(B)W_t. \]

Causality concerns whether \(X_t\) can be written using current and past shocks. Invertibility concerns whether \(W_t\) can be recovered from current and past observations. Root conditions determine both.

The impulse-response coefficients \(\psi_j\) connect algebra to interpretation: they show how shocks propagate through time. The autocovariance formula

\[ \gamma(h)=\sigma^2\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\psi_j\psi_{j+|h|} \]

connects impulse responses to dependence diagnostics. These ideas prepare us for forecasting, ARIMA, SARIMA, spectral analysis, state-space models, and deep sequence models.

## 4.1 Motivation: linear filters as models of memory

In Chapter 3, we studied stationarity and autocorrelation. We saw that white noise has no linear memory, MA(1) has memory that cuts off after one lag, and AR(1) has memory that decays geometrically. In this chapter we organize these examples into a general modeling framework.

The starting idea is simple:

::: {.callout-note}
## Time-domain modeling principle
A stationary time series can often be modeled as a transformed version of white noise. The transformation is a **linear filter** that spreads the effect of each shock forward or backward through time.
:::

Let $\{W_t\}$ be white noise with mean $0$ and variance $\sigma^2$:

$$
E[W_t]=0,\qquad \operatorname{Var}(W_t)=\sigma^2,\qquad \operatorname{Cov}(W_t,W_s)=0 \quad (s\ne t).
$$

A linear time-series model describes $X_t$ as a weighted sum of shocks:

$$
X_t=\sum_{j=-\infty}^{\infty} a_j W_{t-j}.
$$

For forecasting, the most important special case is the **causal** case:

$$
X_t=\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\psi_j W_{t-j}.
$$

This means the present depends on current and past shocks, not future shocks. The coefficient $\psi_j$ is the effect at time $t$ of a shock that occurred $j$ periods ago. The sequence

$$
\psi_0,\psi_1,\psi_2,\dots
$$

is called the **impulse-response sequence**.

::: {.callout-tip}
## Interpretation
If $W_t$ is a surprise at time $t$, then $\psi_j$ tells us how much of that surprise remains visible $j$ steps later. ARMA models are compact ways to describe these impulse responses.
:::

## 4.2 The backshift operator

The **backshift operator** $B$ moves a time series one step into the past:

$$
BX_t=X_{t-1}.
$$

More generally,

$$
B^jX_t=X_{t-j}.
$$

For example,

$$
(1-B)X_t=X_t-X_{t-1}
$$

is the first difference used in exploratory analysis and ARIMA modeling.

Backshift notation allows us to rewrite long recurrence equations as polynomial equations. For example,

$$
X_t=\phi X_{t-1}+W_t
$$

can be written as

$$
(1-\phi B)X_t=W_t.
$$

The expression

$$
\phi(B)=1-\phi B
$$

is an operator polynomial. It acts on the sequence $\{X_t\}$.

::: {.callout-important}
## Algebra with operators
Backshift notation looks like ordinary polynomial algebra, but the objects are operators applied to time series. The notation is powerful because many time-series questions become questions about polynomial roots and series expansions.
:::

## 4.3 Linear processes

A zero-mean process $\{X_t\}$ is a **linear process** if it can be written as

$$
X_t=\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\psi_jW_{t-j},
$$

where $\{W_t\}\sim WN(0,\sigma^2)$ and

$$
\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}|\psi_j|<\infty.
$$

Using backshift notation,

$$
X_t=\psi(B)W_t,
$$

where

$$
\psi(B)=\psi_0+\psi_1B+\psi_2B^2+\cdots.
$$

The absolute summability condition ensures the infinite sum is well behaved and the process has finite variance.

### Mean and variance

If $E[W_t]=0$, then

$$
E[X_t]=\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\psi_jE[W_{t-j}]=0.
$$

The variance is

$$
\operatorname{Var}(X_t)
=\operatorname{Var}\left(\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\psi_jW_{t-j}\right)
=\sigma^2\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\psi_j^2,
$$

because the white-noise terms are uncorrelated.

### Autocovariance of a linear process

For $h\ge0$,

$$
\gamma(h)=\operatorname{Cov}(X_{t+h},X_t).
$$

Using

$$
X_{t+h}=\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\psi_jW_{t+h-j}
$$

and

$$
X_t=\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\psi_kW_{t-k},
$$

only terms with equal noise indices contribute to covariance. The condition

$$
t+h-j=t-k
$$

is equivalent to $j=k+h$. Therefore,

$$
\gamma(h)=\sigma^2\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\psi_{k+h}\psi_k,
\qquad h\ge0.
$$

Because autocovariance is symmetric,

$$
\gamma(-h)=\gamma(h).
$$

::: {.callout-note}
## Central formula
For a causal linear process
$$
X_t=\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\psi_jW_{t-j},
$$
the autocovariance is
$$
\gamma(h)=\sigma^2\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\psi_j\psi_{j+|h|}.
$$
This formula is one of the most useful bridges between model parameters and ACF behavior.
:::

## 4.4 Moving-average models

A **moving-average model of order $q$**, written MA($q$), is

$$
X_t=W_t+\theta_1W_{t-1}+\cdots+\theta_qW_{t-q},
$$

where $\{W_t\}\sim WN(0,\sigma^2)$.

Using the backshift operator,

$$
X_t=(1+\theta_1B+\cdots+\theta_qB^q)W_t.
$$

Define the MA polynomial

$$
\theta(B)=1+\theta_1B+\cdots+\theta_qB^q.
$$

Then

$$
X_t=\theta(B)W_t.
$$

An MA($q$) process is a finite linear process with

$$
\psi_0=1,\qquad \psi_j=\theta_j \quad (1\le j\le q),\qquad \psi_j=0\quad(j>q).
$$

Therefore every MA($q$) process is weakly stationary.

### Autocovariance of MA($q$)

For $h\ge0$,

$$
\gamma(h)=\sigma^2\sum_{j=0}^{q-h}\theta_j\theta_{j+h},\qquad 0\le h\le q,
$$

where $\theta_0=1$. For $h>q$,

$$
\gamma(h)=0.
$$

Therefore the ACF of an MA($q$) process **cuts off** after lag $q$.

::: {.callout-tip}
## Diagnostic signature of MA models
An MA($q$) process has theoretical ACF equal to zero after lag $q$. In real data, the sample ACF will not be exactly zero after lag $q$, but it should fluctuate near zero if the model is appropriate.
:::

## 4.5 Example: MA(1)

Let

$$
X_t=W_t+\theta W_{t-1}.
$$

Then

$$
E[X_t]=0.
$$

The variance is

$$
\gamma(0)=\operatorname{Var}(X_t)
=\operatorname{Var}(W_t+\theta W_{t-1})
=\sigma^2(1+\theta^2).
$$

The lag-one autocovariance is

$$
\gamma(1)=\operatorname{Cov}(X_{t+1},X_t).
$$

Since

$$
X_{t+1}=W_{t+1}+\theta W_t,
$$

we have

$$
\gamma(1)
=\operatorname{Cov}(W_{t+1}+\theta W_t, W_t+\theta W_{t-1})
=\theta\operatorname{Var}(W_t)
=\theta\sigma^2.
$$

For $|h|>1$,

$$
\gamma(h)=0.
$$

Thus

$$
\rho(1)=\frac{\theta}{1+\theta^2},
$$

and

$$
\rho(h)=0\qquad |h|>1.
$$

Notice that the map

$$
\theta\mapsto \frac{\theta}{1+\theta^2}
$$

is not one-to-one. In fact,

$$
\frac{\theta}{1+\theta^2}=\frac{1/\theta}{1+1/\theta^2}
$$

for $\theta\ne0$. This is the first warning that MA models may not have unique parameterizations unless we impose invertibility.

## 4.6 Autoregressive models

An **autoregressive model of order $p$**, written AR($p$), is

$$
X_t=\phi_1X_{t-1}+\phi_2X_{t-2}+\cdots+\phi_pX_{t-p}+W_t,
$$

where $\{W_t\}\sim WN(0,\sigma^2)$.

Using backshift notation,

$$
(1-\phi_1B-\phi_2B^2-\cdots-\phi_pB^p)X_t=W_t.
$$

Define the AR polynomial

$$
\phi(B)=1-\phi_1B-\phi_2B^2-\cdots-\phi_pB^p.
$$

Then

$$
\phi(B)X_t=W_t.
$$

An AR process explains the present by regressing it on its own past plus a new shock.

::: {.callout-note}
## AR versus MA intuition
An MA model remembers a finite number of shocks. An AR model remembers past observations. Because each past observation already contains earlier shocks, a causal AR process usually has infinitely long but decaying memory.
:::

## 4.7 Example: AR(1)

Consider

$$
X_t=\phi X_{t-1}+W_t,
$$

or

$$
(1-\phi B)X_t=W_t.
$$

If $|\phi|<1$, then we can formally invert $1-\phi B$:

$$
\frac{1}{1-\phi B}=1+\phi B+\phi^2B^2+\cdots.
$$

Thus

$$
X_t=(1+\phi B+\phi^2B^2+\cdots)W_t
$$

and

$$
X_t=\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\phi^jW_{t-j}.
$$

This is a causal linear process with impulse-response weights

$$
\psi_j=\phi^j.
$$

### Variance

Using the linear-process variance formula,

$$
\gamma(0)=\sigma^2\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\phi^{2j}
=\frac{\sigma^2}{1-\phi^2}.
$$

### Autocovariance

For $h\ge0$,

$$
\gamma(h)=\sigma^2\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\phi^j\phi^{j+h}
=\sigma^2\phi^h\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\phi^{2j}
=\frac{\sigma^2\phi^h}{1-\phi^2}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\rho(h)=\phi^h,\qquad h\ge0,
$$

and by symmetry,

$$
\rho(h)=\phi^{|h|}.
$$

If $0<\phi<1$, the ACF decays positively and geometrically. If $-1<\phi<0$, the ACF alternates signs and decays in magnitude.

### The random walk as a boundary case

If $\phi=1$, then

$$
X_t=X_{t-1}+W_t,
$$

which is the random walk. It is not stationary. The root of $1-\phi z=0$ is $z=1$, exactly on the unit circle. This boundary between stationary and nonstationary behavior is central in ARIMA modeling.

## 4.8 ARMA models

An **autoregressive moving-average model**, ARMA($p,q$), combines AR and MA structure:

$$
X_t-\phi_1X_{t-1}-\cdots-\phi_pX_{t-p}
= W_t+\theta_1W_{t-1}+\cdots+\theta_qW_{t-q}.
$$

Using backshift notation,

$$
\phi(B)X_t=\theta(B)W_t,
$$

where

$$
\phi(B)=1-\phi_1B-\cdots-\phi_pB^p
$$

and

$$
\theta(B)=1+\theta_1B+\cdots+\theta_qB^q.
$$

Special cases:

$$
\text{AR}(p)=\text{ARMA}(p,0),
$$

and

$$
\text{MA}(q)=\text{ARMA}(0,q).
$$

ARMA models are popular because they can represent many short-memory stationary dependence patterns with relatively few parameters.

::: {.callout-important}
## ARMA modeling goal
ARMA models do not try to explain every observation independently. They model the **dependence structure**: how shocks propagate and how the present depends on the past.
:::

## 4.9 Parameter redundancy and common factors

Usually we require that $\phi(z)$ and $\theta(z)$ have no common factors. Otherwise, the same process can be written as a higher-order ARMA model than necessary.

For example, suppose

$$
X_t=W_t.
$$

Then $X_t$ is white noise. But it also satisfies

$$
(1-B+0.5B^2)X_t=(1-B+0.5B^2)W_t.
$$

This looks like an ARMA(2,2) model, but the same polynomial appears on both sides and cancels. The process is still just white noise.

::: {.callout-warning}
## Model order is not only notation
An ARMA$(p,q)$ representation is meaningful only after removing common factors. Otherwise, the apparent order may be artificially high.
:::

## 4.10 Causality

A process is **causal** with respect to $\{W_t\}$ if it can be written as

$$
X_t=\psi(B)W_t=\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\psi_jW_{t-j},
$$

where

$$
\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}|\psi_j|<\infty.
$$

Causality means $X_t$ is determined by current and past innovations only. This is the form we want for forecasting: the future should not be needed to explain the present.

For an ARMA model

$$
\phi(B)X_t=\theta(B)W_t,
$$

a causal representation exists when

$$
\psi(B)=\frac{\theta(B)}{\phi(B)}
$$

has a convergent power-series expansion in nonnegative powers of $B$.

### Root condition for causality

For the ARMA model with no common factors, the process is causal if all roots of

$$
\phi(z)=0
$$

lie outside the unit circle:

$$
|z|>1.
$$

For AR(1),

$$
\phi(z)=1-\phi z.
$$

The root is

$$
z=\frac{1}{\phi}.
$$

The condition $|z|>1$ is equivalent to

$$
|\phi|<1.
$$

## 4.11 Invertibility

A process is **invertible** with respect to $\{W_t\}$ if the innovations can be recovered from current and past observations:

$$
W_t=\pi(B)X_t=\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\pi_jX_{t-j},
$$

where

$$
\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}|\pi_j|<\infty.
$$

For an ARMA model,

$$
\phi(B)X_t=\theta(B)W_t,
$$

invertibility means

$$
W_t=\frac{\phi(B)}{\theta(B)}X_t
$$

has a stable power-series expansion.

### Root condition for invertibility

The model is invertible if all roots of

$$
\theta(z)=0
$$

lie outside the unit circle:

$$
|z|>1.
$$

For MA(1),

$$
X_t=W_t+\theta W_{t-1}=(1+\theta B)W_t.
$$

The MA polynomial is

$$
\theta(z)=1+\theta z.
$$

Its root is

$$
z=-\frac{1}{\theta}.
$$

The root is outside the unit circle exactly when

$$
|\theta|<1.
$$

If $|\theta|<1$, then

$$
W_t=\frac{1}{1+\theta B}X_t
=(1-\theta B+\theta^2B^2-\theta^3B^3+\cdots)X_t.
$$

Thus

$$
W_t=X_t-\theta X_{t-1}+\theta^2X_{t-2}-\theta^3X_{t-3}+\cdots.
$$

::: {.callout-note}
## Why invertibility matters
Invertibility gives a unique and stable way to recover shocks from observations. Without it, different MA parameter values can produce the same autocovariance structure, making estimation and interpretation ambiguous.
:::

## 4.12 Stationarity, causality, and invertibility: summary

For

$$
\phi(B)X_t=\theta(B)W_t,
$$

with $\phi$ and $\theta$ sharing no common factors:

- a stationary solution exists if the roots of $\phi(z)=0$ do not lie on the unit circle;
- a causal stationary solution exists if the roots of $\phi(z)=0$ lie outside the unit circle;
- the model is invertible if the roots of $\theta(z)=0$ lie outside the unit circle.

In most statistical software, fitted ARMA models are typically constrained or reported in a causal and invertible parameterization because that parameterization is identifiable and forecastable.

::: {.callout-important}
## Practical convention
For standard time-series forecasting, when we say an ARMA model is valid, we usually mean it is stationary, causal, and invertible unless stated otherwise.
:::

## 4.13 Matching coefficients: computing $\psi$-weights

Suppose

$$
\phi(B)X_t=\theta(B)W_t
$$

and we want the causal representation

$$
X_t=\psi(B)W_t.
$$

Substituting gives

$$
\phi(B)\psi(B)W_t=\theta(B)W_t.
$$

Thus

$$
\phi(B)\psi(B)=\theta(B).
$$

Write

$$
\psi(B)=\psi_0+\psi_1B+\psi_2B^2+\cdots.
$$

Because $\theta_0=1$, we usually have

$$
\psi_0=1.
$$

For ARMA($p,q$), matching the coefficient of $B^k$ gives

$$
\psi_k-\phi_1\psi_{k-1}-\cdots-\phi_p\psi_{k-p}=\theta_k,
$$

where

$$
\theta_k=0\quad \text{for } k>q,
$$

and

$$
\psi_j=0\quad \text{for }j<0.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
\psi_k=\theta_k+\phi_1\psi_{k-1}+\cdots+\phi_p\psi_{k-p}.
$$

This recursion computes the impulse response.

### Example: ARMA(1,1)

Let

$$
(1-\phi B)X_t=(1+\theta B)W_t.
$$

We seek

$$
X_t=(\psi_0+\psi_1B+\psi_2B^2+\cdots)W_t.
$$

Matching coefficients in

$$
(1-\phi B)(\psi_0+\psi_1B+\psi_2B^2+\cdots)=1+\theta B
$$

gives

$$
\psi_0=1,
$$

$$
\psi_1-\phi\psi_0=\theta,
$$

so

$$
\psi_1=\phi+\theta.
$$

For $k\ge2$,

$$
\psi_k-\phi\psi_{k-1}=0,
$$

hence

$$
\psi_k=\phi\psi_{k-1}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\psi_k=(\phi+\theta)\phi^{k-1},\qquad k\ge1.
$$

If $|\phi|<1$, this impulse response decays geometrically.

## 4.14 Autocovariance of ARMA models

One way to compute autocovariances is to use the causal linear-process representation:

$$
X_t=\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\psi_jW_{t-j}.
$$

Then

$$
\gamma(h)=\sigma^2\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\psi_j\psi_{j+|h|}.
$$

This is conceptually simple and computationally useful. In practice, we truncate the infinite sum after enough terms when the $\psi_j$ decay quickly.

Another way is to multiply the ARMA equation by $X_{t-h}$ and take expectations. For large enough lags, the right-hand side involving finite MA terms disappears, leaving a homogeneous difference equation for $\gamma(h)$.

For example, an AR($p$) process satisfies the **Yule-Walker equations**:

$$
\gamma(h)=\phi_1\gamma(h-1)+\cdots+\phi_p\gamma(h-p),\qquad h\ge1.
$$

These equations show that the ACF of an AR process follows the same recurrence structure as the process itself.

## 4.15 ACF and PACF signatures

Classical model identification often uses the sample ACF and PACF.

### MA($q$)

The theoretical ACF cuts off after lag $q$:

$$
\rho(h)=0\qquad h>q.
$$

The PACF usually decays gradually.

### AR($p$)

The theoretical PACF cuts off after lag $p$, while the ACF usually decays gradually according to the roots of the AR polynomial.

### ARMA($p,q$)

Both ACF and PACF usually decay gradually. The orders $p$ and $q$ are not usually obvious from visual inspection alone.

::: {.callout-warning}
## Do not overread ACF/PACF plots
Sample ACF and PACF are noisy. They are diagnostic tools, not proofs. Model identification should combine plots, parameter estimation, information criteria, residual diagnostics, and forecasting validation.
:::

## 4.18 Python example: sample ACF from scratch

The sample ACF helps reveal dependence patterns.

#| label: py-ch04-acf-from-scratch

import numpy as np

def sample_acf(x, max_lag):
    x = np.asarray(x)
    x = x - x.mean()

## 4.19 Python example: root checking

AR and MA roots are central for causality and invertibility.

For an AR($p$) model

$$
\phi(z)=1-\phi_1z-\cdots-\phi_pz^p.
$$

For an MA($q$) model

$$
\theta(z)=1+\theta_1z+\cdots+\theta_qz^q.
$$

In Python, `numpy.roots` expects coefficients in descending powers. The following helper functions compute roots of these polynomials.

```{python}
#| label: py-ch04-root-checking
#| echo: true
#| message: false
#| warning: false

import numpy as np

def ar_roots(phi):
    """Roots of phi(z)=1-phi1 z-...-phip z^p."""
    phi = np.asarray(phi, dtype=float)
    coeff_desc = np.r_[-phi[::-1], 1.0]
    return np.roots(coeff_desc)

def ma_roots(theta):
    """Roots of theta(z)=1+theta1 z+...+thetaq z^q."""
    theta = np.asarray(theta, dtype=float)
    coeff_desc = np.r_[theta[::-1], 1.0]
    return np.roots(coeff_desc)

def outside_unit_circle(roots, tol=1e-10):
    return np.all(np.abs(roots) > 1 + tol)

examples = {
    "AR(1), phi=0.75": ar_roots([0.75]),
    "AR(1), phi=1.10": ar_roots([1.10]),
    "MA(1), theta=0.70": ma_roots([0.70]),
    "MA(1), theta=1.40": ma_roots([1.40]),
}

for name, roots in examples.items():
    print(name)
    print("  roots:", roots)
    print("  moduli:", np.abs(roots))
    print("  outside unit circle:", outside_unit_circle(roots))
```

Interpretation:

- AR roots outside the unit circle imply causality.
- MA roots outside the unit circle imply invertibility.
- Roots near the unit circle imply slow decay and numerical sensitivity.

## 4.20 Python example: compute $\psi$-weights recursively

The following function computes the causal impulse-response coefficients of an ARMA model by matching coefficients in

$$
\phi(B)\psi(B)=\theta(B).
$$

```{python}
#| label: py-ch04-psi-weights
#| echo: true
#| message: false
#| warning: false

import numpy as np

def psi_weights(ar=None, ma=None, horizon=30):
    """
    Compute psi_0,...,psi_horizon for
    phi(B) X_t = theta(B) W_t,
    phi(B)=1 - ar[0]B - ...,
    theta(B)=1 + ma[0]B + ... .
    """
    ar = np.asarray([] if ar is None else ar, dtype=float)
    ma = np.asarray([] if ma is None else ma, dtype=float)
    p = len(ar)
    q = len(ma)
    psi = np.zeros(horizon + 1)
    psi[0] = 1.0
    for k in range(1, horizon + 1):
        theta_k = ma[k - 1] if k <= q else 0.0
        ar_part = 0.0
        for j in range(1, p + 1):
            if k - j >= 0:
                ar_part += ar[j - 1] * psi[k - j]
        psi[k] = theta_k + ar_part
    return psi

psi_ar1 = psi_weights(ar=[0.75], ma=[], horizon=20)
psi_ma1 = psi_weights(ar=[], ma=[0.70], horizon=20)
psi_arma11 = psi_weights(ar=[0.65], ma=[0.50], horizon=20)

pd.DataFrame({
    "lag": np.arange(21),
    "AR(1) psi": psi_ar1,
    "MA(1) psi": psi_ma1,
    "ARMA(1,1) psi": psi_arma11
}).head(12)
```

```{python}
#| label: py-ch04-psi-plot
#| echo: true
#| message: false
#| warning: false

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(9, 4))
ax.plot(psi_ar1, marker="o", label="AR(1)")
ax.plot(psi_ma1, marker="o", label="MA(1)")
ax.plot(psi_arma11, marker="o", label="ARMA(1,1)")
ax.axhline(0, linewidth=0.8)
ax.set_xlabel("lag j")
ax.set_ylabel("psi_j")
ax.set_title("Impulse-response weights")

plt.show()
```

The MA(1) impulse response cuts off immediately after lag $1$. The AR(1) impulse response decays geometrically. The ARMA(1,1) impulse response has a first coefficient affected by the MA term, then decays according to the AR coefficient.

## 4.21 Python example: autocovariance from $\psi$-weights

For a causal linear process,

$$
\gamma(h)=\sigma^2\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\psi_j\psi_{j+h}.
$$

The following function approximates this by truncating the $\psi$ sequence.

```{python}
#| label: py-ch04-autocov-from-psi
#| echo: true
#| message: false
#| warning: false

def acf_from_psi(psi, max_lag, sigma2=1.0):
    psi = np.asarray(psi, dtype=float)
    gamma = []
    for h in range(max_lag + 1):
        gamma_h = sigma2 * np.sum(psi[:len(psi) - h] * psi[h:])
        gamma.append(gamma_h)
    gamma = np.array(gamma)
    return gamma / gamma[0]

long_psi_ar1 = psi_weights(ar=[0.75], horizon=300)
acf_ar1_approx = acf_from_psi(long_psi_ar1, max_lag=20)
acf_ar1_exact = 0.75 ** np.arange(21)

pd.DataFrame({
    "lag": np.arange(21),
    "approx from psi": acf_ar1_approx,
    "exact AR(1)": acf_ar1_exact
}).head(10)
```

The approximation matches the exact AR(1) ACF very closely because the impulse response decays geometrically.

## 4.24 Connection to machine learning

ARMA models can be viewed as a simple and interpretable machine-learning architecture for sequential data.

### AR as linear supervised learning on lag features

An AR($p$) model is

$$
X_t=\phi_1X_{t-1}+\cdots+\phi_pX_{t-p}+W_t.
$$

This is linear regression with features

$$
(X_{t-1},X_{t-2},\dots,X_{t-p}).
$$

The difference from ordinary regression is that the features are generated from the same dependent time series, so validation and uncertainty require time-series methods.

### MA as latent-shock modeling

An MA($q$) model uses unobserved shocks:

$$
X_t=W_t+\theta_1W_{t-1}+\cdots+\theta_qW_{t-q}.
$$

The predictors $W_{t-j}$ are not directly observed. They must be inferred through model fitting.

### ARMA as a linear recurrent system

An ARMA model combines recurrence through $X_{t-j}$ and shock filtering through $W_{t-j}$. This makes it an ancestor of modern sequence models: it has hidden memory, impulse responses, and recursive prediction. RNNs, LSTMs, temporal convolutional networks, and state-space sequence models generalize these ideas in nonlinear and high-dimensional ways.

::: {.callout-ai}
## AI connection
A modern AI model may learn complex nonlinear sequence representations, but the same questions remain:

- What past information matters?
- How long does memory persist?
- Are residuals still dependent?
- Is the model stable when rolled forward?
- Does it generalize under time-aware validation?

ARMA models give a mathematically transparent setting for learning these questions before moving to deep models.
:::

## 4.25 Common mistakes

### Mistake 1: confusing stationarity with causality

A stationary solution may exist even when the model is not causal. For forecasting, we usually need a causal representation because we cannot use future shocks.

### Mistake 2: forgetting invertibility

An MA model can be stationary for any parameter value, but not every parameter value gives a unique and stable innovation representation. Invertibility is needed for canonical estimation and interpretation.

### Mistake 3: treating AR and MA roots the same way without checking signs

AR and MA polynomials are written with different sign conventions:

$$
\phi(z)=1-\phi_1z-\cdots-\phi_pz^p,
$$

while

$$
\theta(z)=1+\theta_1z+\cdots+\theta_qz^q.
$$

When coding root checks, pay close attention to the coefficient order and signs.

### Mistake 4: believing sample ACF cutoffs are exact

The theoretical ACF of MA($q$) cuts off. The sample ACF only fluctuates around zero after lag $q$.

### Mistake 5: fitting ARMA to nonstationary data

ARMA models are for stationary data. If the time plot or ACF suggests trend, seasonality, or changing variance, consider transformation, detrending, differencing, ARIMA, SARIMA, or another model class.

## 4.29 Chapter summary

ARMA models are the central stationary linear models of classical time-series analysis. A linear process writes a time series as a weighted sum of white-noise shocks:

$$
X_t=\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\psi_jW_{t-j}.
$$

An MA($q$) model has finite shock memory:

$$
X_t=W_t+\theta_1W_{t-1}+\cdots+\theta_qW_{t-q}.
$$

An AR($p$) model regresses the present on the past:

$$
X_t=\phi_1X_{t-1}+\cdots+\phi_pX_{t-p}+W_t.
$$

An ARMA($p,q$) model combines both:

$$
\phi(B)X_t=\theta(B)W_t.
$$

Causality concerns whether $X_t$ can be written using current and past shocks. Invertibility concerns whether $W_t$ can be recovered from current and past observations. Root conditions determine both.

The impulse-response coefficients $\psi_j$ connect algebra to interpretation: they show how shocks propagate through time. The autocovariance formula

$$
\gamma(h)=\sigma^2\sum_{j=0}^{\infty}\psi_j\psi_{j+|h|}
$$

connects impulse responses to dependence diagnostics. These ideas prepare us for forecasting, ARIMA, SARIMA, spectral analysis, state-space models, and deep sequence models.
