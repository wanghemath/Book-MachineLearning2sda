Part V. Forecasting at Scale and Machine Learning Methods
Chapter goal: move from point forecasts to distributional forecasts. We study prediction intervals, quantiles, scoring rules, calibration, residual bootstrap methods, conformal prediction, and uncertainty-aware forecasting workflows for classical and machine-learning time-series models.

20.1 Learning objectives

After studying this chapter, you should be able to:

  1. distinguish point forecasting from probabilistic forecasting;
  2. define forecast distributions, prediction intervals, central intervals, quantiles, and prediction sets;
  3. explain why uncertainty usually grows with the forecast horizon;
  4. derive Gaussian prediction intervals from a conditional mean and conditional variance;
  5. compute empirical coverage and interval width from backtesting results;
  6. use quantile loss and proper scoring rules to train and evaluate probabilistic forecasts;
  7. construct residual-bootstrap prediction intervals;
  8. construct split conformal and rolling conformal intervals for time-series forecasts;
  9. diagnose miscalibrated uncertainty using coverage, interval-width, and PIT-style plots;
  10. use AI assistants to critique forecast uncertainty while independently checking validation and assumptions.

20.2 18.1 Why point forecasts are not enough

A point forecast gives a single number, such as

\[ \widehat Y_{t+h|t}. \]

For many decisions, the single number is not enough. A hospital wants to know whether tomorrow’s patient volume might exceed capacity. An energy company wants an upper-tail demand forecast. A financial-risk analyst wants downside risk. A supply-chain planner wants a distribution of possible demand values, not only the conditional mean.

A probabilistic forecast tries to describe the full conditional distribution

\[ F_{t,h}(y)=\mathbb P(Y_{t+h}\le y\mid \mathcal F_t), \]

where \(\mathcal F_t\) is the information available at forecast origin \(t\). From this distribution we can extract:

  • a conditional mean \(\mathbb E[Y_{t+h}\mid\mathcal F_t]\);
  • a conditional median;
  • conditional quantiles;
  • prediction intervals;
  • tail probabilities such as \(\mathbb P(Y_{t+h}>c\mid\mathcal F_t)\).
NoteForecasting as a distributional problem

A forecast should answer not only what value is most likely? but also how wrong could we be, and in which direction?

20.3 18.2 Information sets and forecast distributions

Let \(\{Y_t\}\) be a time series and let

\[ \mathcal F_t=\sigma(Y_s, X_s: s\le t) \]

represent the data known at time \(t\). Here \(X_s\) may include exogenous variables, calendar features, known future schedules, or model-generated state estimates.

A probabilistic forecast for horizon \(h\) is a conditional distribution

\[ \mathcal L(Y_{t+h}\mid \mathcal F_t), \]

or equivalently a conditional cumulative distribution function

\[ F_{t,h}(y)=\mathbb P(Y_{t+h}\le y\mid\mathcal F_t). \]

A point forecast is a functional of this distribution. Under squared loss, the optimal point forecast is

\[ \widehat y_{t+h|t}^{\text{mean}} = \mathbb E[Y_{t+h}\mid\mathcal F_t]. \]

Under absolute loss, the optimal point forecast is a conditional median:

\[ \widehat y_{t+h|t}^{\text{median}} \in \{m:F_{t,h}(m)\ge 1/2,\;1-F_{t,h}(m^- )\ge 1/2\}. \]

For asymmetric cost, the optimal point forecast is a conditional quantile.

20.4 18.3 Quantiles and prediction intervals

For \(\tau\in(0,1)\), the \(\tau\)-quantile of the conditional forecast distribution is

\[ q_{t,h}(\tau)=F_{t,h}^{-1}(\tau) = \inf\{y:F_{t,h}(y)\ge \tau\}. \]

A central \((1-\alpha)\) prediction interval is

\[ \left[q_{t,h}\left(\frac{\alpha}{2}\right), q_{t,h}\left(1-\frac{\alpha}{2}\right)\right]. \]

For example, a 90% central interval uses \(\alpha=0.1\):

\[ \left[q_{t,h}(0.05),q_{t,h}(0.95)\right]. \]

If the forecast distribution is conditionally Gaussian,

\[ Y_{t+h}\mid\mathcal F_t\sim N(\mu_{t,h},\sigma_{t,h}^2), \]

then the central \((1-\alpha)\) interval is

\[ \mu_{t,h}\pm z_{1-\alpha/2}\sigma_{t,h}, \]

where \(z_{1-\alpha/2}\) is the corresponding standard normal quantile.

20.5 The interval has two jobs

A prediction interval should be calibrated and sharp. Calibration means that the event \(Y_{t+h}\) falls inside the nominal 90% interval about 90% of the time. Sharpness means that, subject to calibration, the interval should be as narrow as possible.

20.6 18.4 Forecast error decomposition

Suppose a model produces a conditional mean forecast

\[ \widehat\mu_{t,h}=\widehat{\mathbb E}[Y_{t+h}\mid\mathcal F_t]. \]

The forecast error is

\[ e_{t,h}=Y_{t+h}-\widehat\mu_{t,h}. \]

A useful conceptual decomposition is

\[ e_{t,h} = \underbrace{Y_{t+h}-\mathbb E[Y_{t+h}\mid\mathcal F_t]}_{\text{irreducible uncertainty}} + \underbrace{\mathbb E[Y_{t+h}\mid\mathcal F_t]-\widehat\mu_{t,h}}_{\text{model and estimation error}}. \]

The first term is unavoidable even with the correct model. The second term can be reduced through better modeling, more data, and better validation.

In time-series forecasting, uncertainty often increases with horizon because the model must propagate uncertainty through future unobserved states or future forecast errors.

20.7 18.5 Gaussian forecast intervals for AR(1)

Consider the stationary AR(1) model

\[ Y_t=\phi Y_{t-1}+W_t,\qquad W_t\sim WN(0,\sigma_W^2),\qquad |\phi|<1. \]

The \(h\)-step conditional mean is

\[ \mathbb E[Y_{t+h}\mid Y_t]=\phi^hY_t. \]

The forecast error is

\[ Y_{t+h}-\phi^hY_t = W_{t+h}+\phi W_{t+h-1}+\cdots+\phi^{h-1}W_{t+1}. \]

Therefore,

\[ \operatorname{Var}(Y_{t+h}-\phi^hY_t\mid\mathcal F_t) = \sigma_W^2\sum_{j=0}^{h-1}\phi^{2j} = \sigma_W^2\frac{1-\phi^{2h}}{1-\phi^2}. \]

Under Gaussian noise, a central \((1-\alpha)\) prediction interval is

\[ \phi^hY_t \pm z_{1-\alpha/2}\sigma_W \sqrt{\frac{1-\phi^{2h}}{1-\phi^2}}. \]

As \(h\to\infty\), the conditional mean goes to zero and the forecast variance approaches the unconditional variance

\[ \frac{\sigma_W^2}{1-\phi^2}. \]

ImportantModel-based intervals depend on model assumptions

The AR(1) interval above is valid only to the extent that the AR(1), stationarity, and Gaussian-noise assumptions are a good approximation to the data-generating process.

20.9 18.7 Python example: model-based AR(1) forecast intervals

Code 
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(7339)

phi = 0.75
sigma_w = 1.0
n = 220
y = np.zeros(n)
for t in range(1, n):
    y[t] = phi * y[t-1] + rng.normal(0, sigma_w)

origin = 180
H = 30
last = y[origin]
h = np.arange(1, H + 1)
mean_fcst = (phi ** h) * last
var_fcst = sigma_w**2 * (1 - phi ** (2 * h)) / (1 - phi**2)
se_fcst = np.sqrt(var_fcst)
z90 = 1.6448536269514722
lower = mean_fcst - z90 * se_fcst
upper = mean_fcst + z90 * se_fcst

future_index = np.arange(origin + 1, origin + H + 1)

plt.figure(figsize=(9, 4))
plt.plot(np.arange(origin - 80, origin + 1), y[origin-80:origin+1], label="observed")
plt.plot(future_index, y[origin+1:origin+H+1], label="future observed")
plt.plot(future_index, mean_fcst, label="AR(1) mean forecast")
plt.fill_between(future_index, lower, upper, alpha=0.25, label="90% interval")
plt.axvline(origin, linestyle="--", linewidth=1)
plt.xlabel("time")
plt.ylabel("value")
plt.title("Model-based AR(1) prediction interval")
plt.legend()
plt.show()

The formula used in the code is

\[ \widehat Y_{t+h|t}=\phi^hY_t, \qquad \widehat{\operatorname{Var}}(Y_{t+h}-\widehat Y_{t+h|t}) =\sigma_W^2\sum_{j=0}^{h-1}\phi^{2j}. \]

In practice, \(\phi\) and \(\sigma_W\) are estimated. Estimation uncertainty is usually ignored in simple textbook intervals but may matter for small data sets.

20.10 18.8 Empirical coverage and interval width

Suppose we have forecasts for origins \(t\in\mathcal T\) and horizon \(h\). Let

\[ [L_{t,h},U_{t,h}] \]

be a nominal \((1-\alpha)\) prediction interval. The empirical coverage is

\[ \widehat{\operatorname{Cov}}_h = \frac{1}{|\mathcal T|}\sum_{t\in\mathcal T} \mathbf 1\{L_{t,h}\le Y_{t+h}\le U_{t,h}\}. \]

The average interval width is

\[ \widehat W_h = \frac{1}{|\mathcal T|}\sum_{t\in\mathcal T}(U_{t,h}-L_{t,h}). \]

Good intervals should have empirical coverage near the nominal level and should not be unnecessarily wide.

Code 
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(18)
origins = np.arange(80)
horizons = np.arange(1, 8)
rows = []
for h in horizons:
    for t in origins:
        true_mean = 20 + 0.1 * t + 0.4 * h
        true_sd = 0.8 + 0.15 * h
        y_true = true_mean + rng.normal(0, true_sd)
        # Deliberately underestimates uncertainty at long horizons.
        model_sd = 0.9 + 0.08 * h
        lo = true_mean - 1.64485 * model_sd
        hi = true_mean + 1.64485 * model_sd
        rows.append((t, h, y_true, lo, hi))

bt = pd.DataFrame(rows, columns=["origin", "horizon", "y", "lower", "upper"])
bt["covered"] = (bt["y"] >= bt["lower"]) & (bt["y"] <= bt["upper"])
bt["width"] = bt["upper"] - bt["lower"]
summary = bt.groupby("horizon").agg(
    coverage=("covered", "mean"),
    avg_width=("width", "mean")
).reset_index()

print(summary)

plt.figure(figsize=(7, 4))
plt.plot(summary["horizon"], summary["coverage"], marker="o", label="empirical coverage")
plt.axhline(0.90, linestyle="--", label="nominal 90%")
plt.xlabel("forecast horizon")
plt.ylabel("coverage")
plt.ylim(0.4, 1.0)
plt.title("Interval coverage by horizon")
plt.legend()

20.11 18.9 Proper scoring rules

A point forecast can be evaluated using MAE or RMSE. A probabilistic forecast needs a score that rewards calibrated and sharp distributions.

A scoring rule \(S(F,y)\) assigns a loss to forecast distribution \(F\) when the realized value is \(y\). It is proper if the expected score is minimized when \(F\) equals the true conditional distribution.

20.11.1 Log score

If the forecast density is \(f\), the log score is

\[ S_{\log}(f,y)=-\log f(y). \]

It strongly penalizes assigning very low probability density to the observed outcome.

20.11.2 Pinball loss for quantiles

For a quantile level \(\tau\in(0,1)\) and quantile forecast \(q\), the pinball loss is

\[ \rho_\tau(y-q) =(\tau-\mathbf 1\{y<q\})(y-q). \]

Equivalently,

\[ \ell_\tau(y,q)= \begin{cases} \tau(y-q), & y\ge q,\\ (1-\tau)(q-y), & y<q. \end{cases} \]

The conditional minimizer is the \(\tau\)-quantile:

\[ q^*(x)\in\arg\min_q \mathbb E[\ell_\tau(Y,q)\mid X=x]. \]

20.11.3 Winkler interval score

For a central \((1-\alpha)\) interval \([L,U]\), the Winkler interval score is

\[ S_\alpha(L,U;y) =(U-L) +\frac{2}{\alpha}(L-y)\mathbf 1\{y<L\} +\frac{2}{\alpha}(y-U)\mathbf 1\{y>U\}. \]

It rewards narrow intervals but penalizes misses.

Code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def pinball(y, q, tau):
    u = y - q
    return np.maximum(tau * u, (tau - 1) * u)

q_grid = np.linspace(-3, 3, 300)
y_obs = 0.0
plt.figure(figsize=(7, 4))
for tau in [0.1, 0.5, 0.9]:
    plt.plot(q_grid, pinball(y_obs, q_grid, tau), label=f"tau={tau}")
plt.xlabel("quantile forecast q")
plt.ylabel("pinball loss")
plt.title("Pinball loss for one observed value")
plt.legend()
plt.show()

20.12 18.10 Quantile regression for forecasting

A machine-learning forecast can directly estimate conditional quantiles. For horizon \(h\), choose a feature vector \(X_t\) and fit

\[ \widehat q_h(\tau,x) \approx F^{-1}_{Y_{t+h}\mid X_t=x}(\tau). \]

The empirical quantile-risk problem is

\[ \widehat q_{h,\tau} \in \arg\min_{g\in\mathcal G} \frac{1}{n}\sum_{t\in\mathcal T_{\text{train}}} \ell_\tau(Y_{t+h},g(X_t)). \]

By fitting two models, one for \(\tau=0.05\) and one for \(\tau=0.95\), we obtain a 90% interval.

Code 
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from sklearn.ensemble import GradientBoostingRegressor

rng = np.random.default_rng(7339)
n = 420
t = np.arange(n)
season = np.sin(2 * np.pi * t / 24)
trend = 0.01 * t
sigma = 0.4 + 0.5 * (season + 1)  # larger uncertainty at seasonal peaks
y = 5 + trend + 2 * season + rng.normal(0, sigma)

def make_lag_table(y, max_lag=24, horizon=1):
    rows = []
    targets = []
    times = []
    for i in range(max_lag, len(y) - horizon):
        rows.append(y[i-max_lag+1:i+1][::-1])
        targets.append(y[i+horizon])
        times.append(i)
    return np.asarray(rows), np.asarray(targets), np.asarray(times)

X, target, times = make_lag_table(y, max_lag=24, horizon=1)
train = times < 300
X_train, y_train = X[train], target[train]
X_test, y_test, test_times = X[~train], target[~train], times[~train] + 1

models = {}
for tau in [0.05, 0.5, 0.95]:
    model = GradientBoostingRegressor(
        loss="quantile" if tau != 0.5 else "squared_error",
        alpha=tau if tau != 0.5 else 0.9,
        n_estimators=150,
        max_depth=2,
        learning_rate=0.05,
        random_state=18
    )
    model.fit(X_train, y_train)
    models[tau] = model

q05 = models[0.05].predict(X_test)
q50 = models[0.5].predict(X_test)
q95 = models[0.95].predict(X_test)
coverage = np.mean((y_test >= q05) & (y_test <= q95))
print(f"Empirical 90% interval coverage: {coverage:.3f}")

plt.figure(figsize=(9, 4))
plt.plot(t, y, linewidth=1, label="observed")
plt.plot(test_times, q50, label="median forecast")
plt.fill_between(test_times, q05, q95, alpha=0.25, label="estimated 90% interval")
plt.axvline(300, linestyle="--", linewidth=1)
plt.xlabel("time")
plt.ylabel("value")
plt.title("Quantile-regression forecast interval")
plt.legend()
plt.show()
Empirical 90% interval coverage: 0.891

20.13 18.11 Quantile crossing

If we fit quantile models independently, we may get crossing quantiles:

\[ \widehat q(0.95,x)<\widehat q(0.05,x), \]

which is impossible for a valid distribution. Common fixes include:

  1. sorting predicted quantiles after prediction;
  2. fitting models with monotonic constraints;
  3. fitting a full distributional model;
  4. using conformal calibration on top of point or quantile forecasts;
  5. using joint neural-network quantile outputs with a non-crossing parameterization.

For a classroom project, post-processing by sorting quantiles is a reasonable first fix, but students should state that it is a repair rather than a statistical model.

20.14 18.12 Residual-bootstrap prediction intervals

A residual bootstrap approximates the forecast-error distribution from past errors.

Suppose a fitted model gives one-step forecasts \(\widehat Y_{t+1|t}\). The residuals are

\[ \widehat e_{t,1}=Y_{t+1}-\widehat Y_{t+1|t}. \]

A simple bootstrap interval for a new one-step forecast \(\widehat Y_{T+1|T}\) is

\[ \left[ \widehat Y_{T+1|T}+\widehat Q_{\alpha/2}(\widehat e), \widehat Y_{T+1|T}+\widehat Q_{1-\alpha/2}(\widehat e) \right], \]

where \(\widehat Q_\tau(\widehat e)\) is an empirical residual quantile.

For multi-step forecasts, residuals should be grouped by horizon:

\[ \widehat e_{t,h}=Y_{t+h}-\widehat Y_{t+h|t}. \]

Then horizon-specific intervals use horizon-specific residual quantiles.

Code 
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from sklearn.linear_model import Ridge

rng = np.random.default_rng(2026)
n = 360
t = np.arange(n)
y = 12 + 0.02*t + 1.5*np.sin(2*np.pi*t/7) + rng.normal(0, 0.8, size=n)

max_lag = 14
X, target, times = make_lag_table(y, max_lag=max_lag, horizon=1)
train = times < 260
cal = (times >= 260) & (times < 310)
test = times >= 310

model = Ridge(alpha=1.0)
model.fit(X[train], target[train])

cal_pred = model.predict(X[cal])
cal_resid = target[cal] - cal_pred
lo_resid, hi_resid = np.quantile(cal_resid, [0.05, 0.95])

test_pred = model.predict(X[test])
lo = test_pred + lo_resid
hi = test_pred + hi_resid
coverage = np.mean((target[test] >= lo) & (target[test] <= hi))
print(f"Bootstrap-style 90% coverage on test set: {coverage:.3f}")

plt.figure(figsize=(9, 4))
test_index = times[test] + 1
plt.plot(t, y, linewidth=1, label="observed")
plt.plot(test_index, test_pred, label="point forecast")
plt.fill_between(test_index, lo, hi, alpha=0.25, label="residual-quantile interval")
plt.axvline(310, linestyle="--", linewidth=1)
plt.xlabel("time")
plt.ylabel("value")
plt.title("Residual-bootstrap style interval")
plt.legend()
plt.show()
Bootstrap-style 90% coverage on test set: 0.878
WarningBootstrap residuals are not automatically valid

Residual bootstrap intervals can fail when residuals are dependent, heteroskedastic, seasonally structured, or drawn from a distribution that changes over time. For time series, calibration residuals should be chosen from a validation period that resembles future deployment.

20.15 18.13 Conformal prediction: distribution-free calibration idea

Conformal prediction constructs intervals with finite-sample coverage guarantees under exchangeability. In time series, exchangeability is usually not exactly true, but conformal methods are still useful when adapted carefully through rolling calibration windows, blocked calibration, or online updates.

For split conformal regression, fit a point model \(\widehat f\) on a training set. On a separate calibration set, compute conformity scores

\[ s_i=|Y_i-\widehat f(X_i)|. \]

Let \(\widehat q_{1-\alpha}\) be an appropriate empirical quantile of the calibration scores. Then the conformal interval for a new feature vector \(X_{\text{new}}\) is

\[ \left[ \widehat f(X_{\text{new}})-\widehat q_{1-\alpha}, \widehat f(X_{\text{new}})+\widehat q_{1-\alpha} \right]. \]

A finite-sample conformal quantile is often chosen as

\[ \widehat q_{1-\alpha} =\text{the }\left\lceil (n_{\text{cal}}+1)(1-\alpha)\right\rceil\text{-th smallest calibration score}. \]

Code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestRegressor

rng = np.random.default_rng(333)
n = 480
t = np.arange(n)
y = 20 + 2*np.sin(2*np.pi*t/12) + 0.8*np.sin(2*np.pi*t/48)
y = y + rng.normal(0, 0.9 + 0.2*np.sin(2*np.pi*t/12)**2, size=n)

X, target, times = make_lag_table(y, max_lag=24, horizon=1)
train = times < 280
cal = (times >= 280) & (times < 380)
test = times >= 380

rf = RandomForestRegressor(n_estimators=200, min_samples_leaf=4, random_state=18)
rf.fit(X[train], target[train])

cal_pred = rf.predict(X[cal])
scores = np.abs(target[cal] - cal_pred)
alpha = 0.10
n_cal = len(scores)
rank = int(np.ceil((n_cal + 1) * (1 - alpha)))
rank = min(rank, n_cal)
qhat = np.sort(scores)[rank - 1]

pred = rf.predict(X[test])
lo = pred - qhat
hi = pred + qhat
covered = (target[test] >= lo) & (target[test] <= hi)
print(f"Conformal qhat: {qhat:.3f}")
print(f"Test coverage: {covered.mean():.3f}")

plt.figure(figsize=(9, 4))
test_index = times[test] + 1
plt.plot(t, y, linewidth=1, label="observed")
plt.plot(test_index, pred, label="RF point forecast")
plt.fill_between(test_index, lo, hi, alpha=0.25, label="split conformal interval")
plt.axvline(380, linestyle="--", linewidth=1)
plt.xlabel("time")
plt.ylabel("value")
plt.title("Split conformal interval for time-series forecasting")
plt.legend()
plt.show()

Test coverage: 0.929

20.16 18.14 Time-series conformal prediction

Standard split conformal prediction assumes exchangeable calibration and test points. Time series violate this because observations are ordered and dependent. Several practical adaptations are useful:

  1. Chronological split conformal. Train on early data, calibrate on later data, test on the future. This respects time order but relies on the calibration period being representative.
  2. Rolling conformal. Use a moving calibration window near the forecast origin.
  3. Horizon-specific conformal. Calibrate a separate score distribution for each horizon \(h\).
  4. Locally weighted conformal. Weight recent calibration errors more heavily.
  5. Normalized conformal. Calibrate scaled scores such as

\[ s_t=\frac{|Y_t-\widehat\mu_t|}{\widehat\sigma_t}, \]

then form

\[ [\widehat\mu_t-\widehat q\widehat\sigma_t, \widehat\mu_t+\widehat q\widehat\sigma_t]. \]

NotePractical rule

For this course, use chronological or rolling calibration and report empirical coverage on a later test period. Do not randomly shuffle time-series residuals unless the task explicitly justifies exchangeability.

20.17 18.15 Prediction intervals versus confidence intervals

A confidence interval describes uncertainty about a fixed unknown parameter, such as a mean \(\mu\) or regression coefficient \(\beta_j\). A prediction interval describes uncertainty about a future random observation.

For example, in a simple Gaussian model

\[ Y_i\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2), \]

a confidence interval for \(\mu\) shrinks roughly at rate \(1/\sqrt n\). But a prediction interval for a future observation \(Y_{n+1}\) does not shrink to zero width, because the new observation still has noise variance \(\sigma^2\).

In forecasting, confusing these two intervals leads to severe underestimation of uncertainty.

20.18 18.16 Probabilistic forecasting with simulation

Some models do not give an easy closed-form forecast distribution. Simulation is often the simplest method.

For a fitted model, generate future paths

\[ Y_{T+1}^{(b)},\ldots,Y_{T+H}^{(b)},\qquad b=1,\ldots,B. \]

Then estimate quantiles at each horizon:

\[ \widehat q_h(\tau)=\operatorname{Quantile}_\tau\{Y_{T+h}^{(1)},\ldots,Y_{T+h}^{(B)}\}. \]

Simulation is especially useful for:

  • nonlinear state-space models;
  • models with non-Gaussian errors;
  • models with constraints, such as nonnegative demand;
  • hierarchical forecasts;
  • neural-network ensembles;
  • scenario analysis.
Code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(91)

20.19 18.17 Bayesian predictive distributions

In Bayesian forecasting, parameters are treated as random variables. If \(\theta\) denotes model parameters, then the posterior predictive distribution is

\[ p(y_{T+h}\mid y_{1:T}) = \int p(y_{T+h}\mid y_{1:T},\theta)p(\theta\mid y_{1:T})\,d\theta. \]

This integral includes two kinds of uncertainty:

  1. observation uncertainty, from \(p(y_{T+h}\mid y_{1:T},\theta)\);
  2. parameter uncertainty, from \(p(\theta\mid y_{1:T})\).

In many classical plug-in intervals, we estimate \(\theta\) once and ignore parameter uncertainty:

\[ p(y_{T+h}\mid y_{1:T},\widehat\theta). \]

Bayesian and ensemble methods often produce wider intervals when the training data are limited.

20.20 18.18 Ensembles as approximate uncertainty tools

An ensemble trains multiple models and combines their forecasts. Let

\[ \widehat y_{t+h|t}^{(1)},\ldots,\widehat y_{t+h|t}^{(B)} \]

be forecasts from \(B\) models. The ensemble mean is

\[ \overline y_{t+h|t}=\frac1B\sum_{b=1}^B\widehat y_{t+h|t}^{(b)}. \]

The spread of the ensemble can approximate model uncertainty, but it is not automatically a calibrated prediction interval. Ensemble spread usually captures only part of total uncertainty. Observation noise and calibration still need to be included.

A practical method is:

  1. train an ensemble;
  2. compute point forecasts and ensemble spread;
  3. calibrate residual or normalized residual scores on a validation period;
  4. evaluate coverage on a future test period.

20.21 18.19 Distributional neural forecasts

A neural network can forecast distributional parameters instead of a single value. For example, assume

\[ Y_{t+h}\mid X_t\sim N(\mu_\theta(X_t,h),\sigma_\theta^2(X_t,h)). \]

The network outputs \(\mu_\theta\) and a positive scale \(\sigma_\theta\). Positivity can be enforced by

\[ \sigma_\theta=\operatorname{softplus}(a_\theta)+\epsilon. \]

The negative log-likelihood loss is

\[ \mathcal L(\theta) = \frac12\log(2\pi) + \log \sigma_\theta(X_t,h) + \frac{(Y_{t+h}-\mu_\theta(X_t,h))^2}{2\sigma_\theta^2(X_t,h)}. \]

Other distributional choices include Student-\(t\), negative binomial, Poisson, log-normal, mixture density networks, and quantile-output neural networks.

20.23 18.20 Calibration diagnostics

A probabilistic forecast is calibrated if events predicted to happen with probability \(p\) happen about proportion \(p\) of the time.

For intervals, calibration means

\[ \mathbb P(L_{t,h}\le Y_{t+h}\le U_{t,h})\approx 1-\alpha. \]

For a full forecast CDF \(F_{t,h}\), the probability integral transform is

\[ U_{t,h}=F_{t,h}(Y_{t+h}). \]

If the predictive CDFs are calibrated and continuous, then \(U_{t,h}\) should be approximately uniform on \([0,1]\).

Common diagnostic plots include:

  1. empirical coverage by horizon;
  2. average interval width by horizon;
  3. coverage by season, regime, or demand level;
  4. PIT histogram;
  5. forecast residuals versus predicted uncertainty;
  6. interval misses over time;
  7. separate upper-tail and lower-tail miss rates.

20.24 18.21 Tail risk and decision thresholds

Probabilistic forecasting is especially useful for threshold decisions. Suppose a decision maker cares whether

\[ Y_{t+h}>c. \]

A forecast distribution provides

\[ \mathbb P(Y_{t+h}>c\mid\mathcal F_t)=1-F_{t,h}(c). \]

Examples:

  • probability hospital demand exceeds staffed beds;
  • probability energy demand exceeds capacity;
  • probability inventory demand exceeds stock;
  • probability a sensor reading crosses a safety threshold;
  • probability revenue falls below a target.

The forecast can be connected to expected loss. If under-staffing costs \(C_u\) and over-staffing costs \(C_o\), the optimal quantile level in a newsvendor-style problem is

\[ \tau^*=\frac{C_u}{C_u+C_o}. \]

This is why quantile forecasts are directly decision-relevant.

20.30 18.24 Common mistakes

  1. Reporting only RMSE. RMSE says little about uncertainty calibration.
  2. Using confidence intervals instead of prediction intervals. Prediction intervals must include future observation noise.
  3. Random calibration split. This can leak future regimes into calibration.
  4. Ignoring horizon. Coverage often deteriorates at longer horizons.
  5. Assuming Gaussian residuals without checking. Heavy tails and skewness matter for intervals.
  6. Using training residuals for calibration. Training residuals are often too optimistic.
  7. Forgetting exogenous uncertainty. Future covariates may themselves need forecasts.
  8. Ignoring heteroskedasticity. Constant-width intervals fail when uncertainty changes with level, season, or regime.
  9. Overtrusting ensemble spread. Ensemble spread is not the same as total predictive uncertainty.
  10. Not connecting uncertainty to decisions. A 90% interval may not be the right object for an asymmetric-cost problem.

## 18.1 Why point forecasts are not enough

A point forecast gives a single number, such as

$$
\widehat Y_{t+h|t}.
$$

For many decisions, the single number is not enough. A hospital wants to know whether tomorrow's patient volume might exceed capacity. An energy company wants an upper-tail demand forecast. A financial-risk analyst wants downside risk. A supply-chain planner wants a distribution of possible demand values, not only the conditional mean.

A probabilistic forecast tries to describe the full conditional distribution

$$
F_{t,h}(y)=\mathbb P(Y_{t+h}\le y\mid \mathcal F_t),
$$

where $\mathcal F_t$ is the information available at forecast origin $t$. From this distribution we can extract:

- a conditional mean $\mathbb E[Y_{t+h}\mid\mathcal F_t]$;
- a conditional median;
- conditional quantiles;
- prediction intervals;
- tail probabilities such as $\mathbb P(Y_{t+h}>c\mid\mathcal F_t)$.

::: {.callout-note}
## Forecasting as a distributional problem
A forecast should answer not only **what value is most likely?** but also **how wrong could we be, and in which direction?**
:::

## 18.2 Information sets and forecast distributions

Let $\{Y_t\}$ be a time series and let

$$
\mathcal F_t=\sigma(Y_s, X_s: s\le t)
$$

represent the data known at time $t$. Here $X_s$ may include exogenous variables, calendar features, known future schedules, or model-generated state estimates.

A probabilistic forecast for horizon $h$ is a conditional distribution

$$
\mathcal L(Y_{t+h}\mid \mathcal F_t),
$$

or equivalently a conditional cumulative distribution function

$$
F_{t,h}(y)=\mathbb P(Y_{t+h}\le y\mid\mathcal F_t).
$$

A point forecast is a functional of this distribution. Under squared loss, the optimal point forecast is

$$
\widehat y_{t+h|t}^{\text{mean}}
=
\mathbb E[Y_{t+h}\mid\mathcal F_t].
$$

Under absolute loss, the optimal point forecast is a conditional median:

$$
\widehat y_{t+h|t}^{\text{median}}
\in
\{m:F_{t,h}(m)\ge 1/2,\;1-F_{t,h}(m^- )\ge 1/2\}.
$$

For asymmetric cost, the optimal point forecast is a conditional quantile.

## 18.3 Quantiles and prediction intervals

For $\tau\in(0,1)$, the $\tau$-quantile of the conditional forecast distribution is

$$
q_{t,h}(\tau)=F_{t,h}^{-1}(\tau)
=
\inf\{y:F_{t,h}(y)\ge \tau\}.
$$

A central $(1-\alpha)$ prediction interval is

$$
\left[q_{t,h}\left(\frac{\alpha}{2}\right),
q_{t,h}\left(1-\frac{\alpha}{2}\right)\right].
$$

For example, a 90% central interval uses $\alpha=0.1$:

$$
\left[q_{t,h}(0.05),q_{t,h}(0.95)\right].
$$

If the forecast distribution is conditionally Gaussian,

$$
Y_{t+h}\mid\mathcal F_t\sim N(\mu_{t,h},\sigma_{t,h}^2),
$$

then the central $(1-\alpha)$ interval is

$$
\mu_{t,h}\pm z_{1-\alpha/2}\sigma_{t,h},
$$

where $z_{1-\alpha/2}$ is the corresponding standard normal quantile.

::: {.theorem-box}
## The interval has two jobs
A prediction interval should be **calibrated** and **sharp**. Calibration means that the event $Y_{t+h}$ falls inside the nominal 90% interval about 90% of the time. Sharpness means that, subject to calibration, the interval should be as narrow as possible.
:::

## 18.4 Forecast error decomposition

Suppose a model produces a conditional mean forecast

$$
\widehat\mu_{t,h}=\widehat{\mathbb E}[Y_{t+h}\mid\mathcal F_t].
$$

The forecast error is

$$
e_{t,h}=Y_{t+h}-\widehat\mu_{t,h}.
$$

A useful conceptual decomposition is

$$
e_{t,h}
=
\underbrace{Y_{t+h}-\mathbb E[Y_{t+h}\mid\mathcal F_t]}_{\text{irreducible uncertainty}}
+
\underbrace{\mathbb E[Y_{t+h}\mid\mathcal F_t]-\widehat\mu_{t,h}}_{\text{model and estimation error}}.
$$

The first term is unavoidable even with the correct model. The second term can be reduced through better modeling, more data, and better validation.

In time-series forecasting, uncertainty often increases with horizon because the model must propagate uncertainty through future unobserved states or future forecast errors.

## 18.5 Gaussian forecast intervals for AR(1)

Consider the stationary AR(1) model

$$
Y_t=\phi Y_{t-1}+W_t,\qquad W_t\sim WN(0,\sigma_W^2),\qquad |\phi|<1.
$$

The $h$-step conditional mean is

$$
\mathbb E[Y_{t+h}\mid Y_t]=\phi^hY_t.
$$

The forecast error is

$$
Y_{t+h}-\phi^hY_t
=
W_{t+h}+\phi W_{t+h-1}+\cdots+\phi^{h-1}W_{t+1}.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\operatorname{Var}(Y_{t+h}-\phi^hY_t\mid\mathcal F_t)
=
\sigma_W^2\sum_{j=0}^{h-1}\phi^{2j}
=
\sigma_W^2\frac{1-\phi^{2h}}{1-\phi^2}.
$$

Under Gaussian noise, a central $(1-\alpha)$ prediction interval is

$$
\phi^hY_t
\pm
z_{1-\alpha/2}\sigma_W
\sqrt{\frac{1-\phi^{2h}}{1-\phi^2}}.
$$

As $h\to\infty$, the conditional mean goes to zero and the forecast variance approaches the unconditional variance

$$
\frac{\sigma_W^2}{1-\phi^2}.
$$

::: {.callout-important}
## Model-based intervals depend on model assumptions
The AR(1) interval above is valid only to the extent that the AR(1), stationarity, and Gaussian-noise assumptions are a good approximation to the data-generating process.
:::

[L_{t,h},U_{t,h}]
$$

be a nominal $(1-\alpha)$ prediction interval. The empirical coverage is

$$
\widehat{\operatorname{Cov}}_h
=
\frac{1}{|\mathcal T|}\sum_{t\in\mathcal T}
\mathbf 1\{L_{t,h}\le Y_{t+h}\le U_{t,h}\}.
$$

The average interval width is

$$
\widehat W_h
=
\frac{1}{|\mathcal T|}\sum_{t\in\mathcal T}(U_{t,h}-L_{t,h}).
$$

Good intervals should have empirical coverage near the nominal level and should not be unnecessarily wide.

```{python}
#| label: ch18-coverage-width
#| fig-cap: "Coverage and width can be summarized by forecast horizon."
#| warning: false
#| message: false
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(18)
origins = np.arange(80)

for h in horizons:
    for t in origins:
```

## 18.9 Proper scoring rules

A point forecast can be evaluated using MAE or RMSE. A probabilistic forecast needs a score that rewards calibrated and sharp distributions.

A scoring rule $S(F,y)$ assigns a loss to forecast distribution $F$ when the realized value is $y$. It is **proper** if the expected score is minimized when $F$ equals the true conditional distribution.

### Log score

If the forecast density is $f$, the log score is

$$
S_{\log}(f,y)=-\log f(y).
$$

It strongly penalizes assigning very low probability density to the observed outcome.

### Pinball loss for quantiles

For a quantile level $\tau\in(0,1)$ and quantile forecast $q$, the pinball loss is

$$
\rho_\tau(y-q)
=(\tau-\mathbf 1\{y<q\})(y-q).
$$

Equivalently,

$$
\ell_\tau(y,q)=
\begin{cases}
\tau(y-q), & y\ge q,\\
(1-\tau)(q-y), & y<q.
\end{cases}
$$

The conditional minimizer is the $\tau$-quantile:

$$
q^*(x)\in\arg\min_q \mathbb E[\ell_\tau(Y,q)\mid X=x].
$$

### Winkler interval score

For a central $(1-\alpha)$ interval $[L,U]$, the Winkler interval score is

$$
S_\alpha(L,U;y)
=(U-L)
+\frac{2}{\alpha}(L-y)\mathbf 1\{y<L\}
+\frac{2}{\alpha}(y-U)\mathbf 1\{y>U\}.
$$

It rewards narrow intervals but penalizes misses.

```{python}
#| label: ch18-pinball-loss
#| fig-cap: "Pinball loss is asymmetric and targets a conditional quantile."
#| warning: false
#| message: false
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def pinball(y, q, tau):
    u = y - q
    return np.maximum(tau * u, (tau - 1) * u)

q_grid = np.linspace(-3, 3, 300)
y_obs = 0.0
plt.figure(figsize=(7, 4))
for tau in [0.1, 0.5, 0.9]:
    plt.plot(q_grid, pinball(y_obs, q_grid, tau), label=f"tau={tau}")
plt.xlabel("quantile forecast q")
plt.ylabel("pinball loss")
plt.title("Pinball loss for one observed value")
plt.legend()
plt.show()
```

## 18.10 Quantile regression for forecasting

A machine-learning forecast can directly estimate conditional quantiles. For horizon $h$, choose a feature vector $X_t$ and fit

$$
\widehat q_h(\tau,x)
\approx
F^{-1}_{Y_{t+h}\mid X_t=x}(\tau).
$$

The empirical quantile-risk problem is

$$
\widehat q_{h,\tau}
\in
\arg\min_{g\in\mathcal G}
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{t\in\mathcal T_{\text{train}}}
\ell_\tau(Y_{t+h},g(X_t)).
$$

By fitting two models, one for $\tau=0.05$ and one for $\tau=0.95$, we obtain a 90% interval.

```{python}
#| label: ch18-quantile-regression
#| fig-cap: "Quantile regression produces asymmetric intervals when the data are heteroskedastic."
#| warning: false
#| message: false
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from sklearn.ensemble import GradientBoostingRegressor

rng = np.random.default_rng(7339)
n = 420
t = np.arange(n)
season = np.sin(2 * np.pi * t / 24)
trend = 0.01 * t
sigma = 0.4 + 0.5 * (season + 1)  # larger uncertainty at seasonal peaks
y = 5 + trend + 2 * season + rng.normal(0, sigma)

def make_lag_table(y, max_lag=24, horizon=1):
    rows = []
    targets = []
    times = []
    for i in range(max_lag, len(y) - horizon):
        rows.append(y[i-max_lag+1:i+1][::-1])
        targets.append(y[i+horizon])
        times.append(i)
    return np.asarray(rows), np.asarray(targets), np.asarray(times)

X, target, times = make_lag_table(y, max_lag=24, horizon=1)
train = times < 300
X_train, y_train = X[train], target[train]
X_test, y_test, test_times = X[~train], target[~train], times[~train] + 1

models = {}
for tau in [0.05, 0.5, 0.95]:
    model = GradientBoostingRegressor(
        loss="quantile" if tau != 0.5 else "squared_error",
        alpha=tau if tau != 0.5 else 0.9,
        n_estimators=150,
        max_depth=2,
        learning_rate=0.05,
        random_state=18
    )
    model.fit(X_train, y_train)
    models[tau] = model

q05 = models[0.05].predict(X_test)
q50 = models[0.5].predict(X_test)
q95 = models[0.95].predict(X_test)
coverage = np.mean((y_test >= q05) & (y_test <= q95))
print(f"Empirical 90% interval coverage: {coverage:.3f}")

plt.figure(figsize=(9, 4))
plt.plot(t, y, linewidth=1, label="observed")
plt.plot(test_times, q50, label="median forecast")
plt.fill_between(test_times, q05, q95, alpha=0.25, label="estimated 90% interval")
plt.axvline(300, linestyle="--", linewidth=1)
plt.xlabel("time")
plt.ylabel("value")
plt.title("Quantile-regression forecast interval")
plt.legend()
plt.show()
```

## 18.11 Quantile crossing

If we fit quantile models independently, we may get crossing quantiles:

$$
\widehat q(0.95,x)<\widehat q(0.05,x),
$$

which is impossible for a valid distribution. Common fixes include:

1. sorting predicted quantiles after prediction;
2. fitting models with monotonic constraints;
3. fitting a full distributional model;
4. using conformal calibration on top of point or quantile forecasts;
5. using joint neural-network quantile outputs with a non-crossing parameterization.

For a classroom project, post-processing by sorting quantiles is a reasonable first fix, but students should state that it is a repair rather than a statistical model.

## 18.12 Residual-bootstrap prediction intervals

A residual bootstrap approximates the forecast-error distribution from past errors.

Suppose a fitted model gives one-step forecasts $\widehat Y_{t+1|t}$. The residuals are

$$
\widehat e_{t,1}=Y_{t+1}-\widehat Y_{t+1|t}.
$$

A simple bootstrap interval for a new one-step forecast $\widehat Y_{T+1|T}$ is

$$
\left[
\widehat Y_{T+1|T}+\widehat Q_{\alpha/2}(\widehat e),
\widehat Y_{T+1|T}+\widehat Q_{1-\alpha/2}(\widehat e)
\right],
$$

where $\widehat Q_\tau(\widehat e)$ is an empirical residual quantile.

For multi-step forecasts, residuals should be grouped by horizon:

$$
\widehat e_{t,h}=Y_{t+h}-\widehat Y_{t+h|t}.
$$

Then horizon-specific intervals use horizon-specific residual quantiles.

```{python}
#| label: ch18-residual-bootstrap
#| fig-cap: "Residual quantiles give a simple nonparametric prediction interval."
#| warning: false
#| message: false
import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from sklearn.linear_model import Ridge

rng = np.random.default_rng(2026)
n = 360
t = np.arange(n)
y = 12 + 0.02*t + 1.5*np.sin(2*np.pi*t/7) + rng.normal(0, 0.8, size=n)

max_lag = 14
X, target, times = make_lag_table(y, max_lag=max_lag, horizon=1)
train = times < 260
cal = (times >= 260) & (times < 310)
test = times >= 310

model = Ridge(alpha=1.0)
model.fit(X[train], target[train])

cal_pred = model.predict(X[cal])
cal_resid = target[cal] - cal_pred
lo_resid, hi_resid = np.quantile(cal_resid, [0.05, 0.95])

test_pred = model.predict(X[test])
lo = test_pred + lo_resid
hi = test_pred + hi_resid
coverage = np.mean((target[test] >= lo) & (target[test] <= hi))
print(f"Bootstrap-style 90% coverage on test set: {coverage:.3f}")

plt.figure(figsize=(9, 4))
test_index = times[test] + 1
plt.plot(t, y, linewidth=1, label="observed")
plt.plot(test_index, test_pred, label="point forecast")
plt.fill_between(test_index, lo, hi, alpha=0.25, label="residual-quantile interval")
plt.axvline(310, linestyle="--", linewidth=1)
plt.xlabel("time")
plt.ylabel("value")
plt.title("Residual-bootstrap style interval")
plt.legend()
plt.show()
```

::: {.callout-warning}
## Bootstrap residuals are not automatically valid
Residual bootstrap intervals can fail when residuals are dependent, heteroskedastic, seasonally structured, or drawn from a distribution that changes over time. For time series, calibration residuals should be chosen from a validation period that resembles future deployment.
:::

## 18.13 Conformal prediction: distribution-free calibration idea

Conformal prediction constructs intervals with finite-sample coverage guarantees under exchangeability. In time series, exchangeability is usually not exactly true, but conformal methods are still useful when adapted carefully through rolling calibration windows, blocked calibration, or online updates.

For split conformal regression, fit a point model $\widehat f$ on a training set. On a separate calibration set, compute conformity scores

$$
s_i=|Y_i-\widehat f(X_i)|.
$$

Let $\widehat q_{1-\alpha}$ be an appropriate empirical quantile of the calibration scores. Then the conformal interval for a new feature vector $X_{\text{new}}$ is

$$
\left[
\widehat f(X_{\text{new}})-\widehat q_{1-\alpha},
\widehat f(X_{\text{new}})+\widehat q_{1-\alpha}
\right].
$$

A finite-sample conformal quantile is often chosen as

$$
\widehat q_{1-\alpha}
=\text{the }\left\lceil (n_{\text{cal}}+1)(1-\alpha)\right\rceil\text{-th smallest calibration score}.
$$

```{python}
#| label: ch18-split-conformal
#| fig-cap: "Split conformal intervals calibrate a point forecaster using held-out residual scores."
#| warning: false
#| message: false
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestRegressor

rng = np.random.default_rng(333)
n = 480
t = np.arange(n)
y = 20 + 2*np.sin(2*np.pi*t/12) + 0.8*np.sin(2*np.pi*t/48)
y = y + rng.normal(0, 0.9 + 0.2*np.sin(2*np.pi*t/12)**2, size=n)

X, target, times = make_lag_table(y, max_lag=24, horizon=1)
train = times < 280
cal = (times >= 280) & (times < 380)
test = times >= 380

rf = RandomForestRegressor(n_estimators=200, min_samples_leaf=4, random_state=18)
rf.fit(X[train], target[train])

cal_pred = rf.predict(X[cal])
scores = np.abs(target[cal] - cal_pred)
alpha = 0.10
n_cal = len(scores)
rank = int(np.ceil((n_cal + 1) * (1 - alpha)))
rank = min(rank, n_cal)
qhat = np.sort(scores)[rank - 1]

pred = rf.predict(X[test])
lo = pred - qhat
hi = pred + qhat
covered = (target[test] >= lo) & (target[test] <= hi)
print(f"Conformal qhat: {qhat:.3f}")
print(f"Test coverage: {covered.mean():.3f}")

plt.figure(figsize=(9, 4))
test_index = times[test] + 1
plt.plot(t, y, linewidth=1, label="observed")
plt.plot(test_index, pred, label="RF point forecast")
plt.fill_between(test_index, lo, hi, alpha=0.25, label="split conformal interval")
plt.axvline(380, linestyle="--", linewidth=1)
plt.xlabel("time")
plt.ylabel("value")
plt.title("Split conformal interval for time-series forecasting")
plt.legend()
plt.show()
```

## 18.14 Time-series conformal prediction

Standard split conformal prediction assumes exchangeable calibration and test points. Time series violate this because observations are ordered and dependent. Several practical adaptations are useful:

1. **Chronological split conformal.** Train on early data, calibrate on later data, test on the future. This respects time order but relies on the calibration period being representative.
2. **Rolling conformal.** Use a moving calibration window near the forecast origin.
3. **Horizon-specific conformal.** Calibrate a separate score distribution for each horizon $h$.
4. **Locally weighted conformal.** Weight recent calibration errors more heavily.
5. **Normalized conformal.** Calibrate scaled scores such as

$$
s_t=\frac{|Y_t-\widehat\mu_t|}{\widehat\sigma_t},
$$

then form

$$
[\widehat\mu_t-\widehat q\widehat\sigma_t,
\widehat\mu_t+\widehat q\widehat\sigma_t].
$$

::: {.callout-note}
## Practical rule
For this course, use chronological or rolling calibration and report empirical coverage on a later test period. Do not randomly shuffle time-series residuals unless the task explicitly justifies exchangeability.
:::

## 18.15 Prediction intervals versus confidence intervals

A confidence interval describes uncertainty about a fixed unknown parameter, such as a mean $\mu$ or regression coefficient $\beta_j$. A prediction interval describes uncertainty about a future random observation.

For example, in a simple Gaussian model

$$
Y_i\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2),
$$

a confidence interval for $\mu$ shrinks roughly at rate $1/\sqrt n$. But a prediction interval for a future observation $Y_{n+1}$ does not shrink to zero width, because the new observation still has noise variance $\sigma^2$.

In forecasting, confusing these two intervals leads to severe underestimation of uncertainty.

## 18.16 Probabilistic forecasting with simulation

Some models do not give an easy closed-form forecast distribution. Simulation is often the simplest method.

For a fitted model, generate future paths

$$
Y_{T+1}^{(b)},\ldots,Y_{T+H}^{(b)},\qquad b=1,\ldots,B.
$$

Then estimate quantiles at each horizon:

$$
\widehat q_h(\tau)=\operatorname{Quantile}_\tau\{Y_{T+h}^{(1)},\ldots,Y_{T+h}^{(B)}\}.
$$

Simulation is especially useful for:

- nonlinear state-space models;
- models with non-Gaussian errors;
- models with constraints, such as nonnegative demand;
- hierarchical forecasts;
- neural-network ensembles;
- scenario analysis.

```{python}
#| label: ch18-simulation-forecast-paths
#| fig-cap: "Simulated forecast paths give an empirical forecast distribution."
#| warning: false
#| message: false
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(91)
H = 40
B = 500
last_y = 15.0
phi = 0.82
level = 12.0
sigma = 0.7
paths = np.zeros((B, H))
for b in range(B):
    y_prev = last_y
    for h in range(H):
        y_new = level + phi * (y_prev - level) + rng.normal(0, sigma)
        paths[b, h] = y_new
        y_prev = y_new

median = np.quantile(paths, 0.5, axis=0)
lo = np.quantile(paths, 0.05, axis=0)
hi = np.quantile(paths, 0.95, axis=0)

plt.figure(figsize=(9, 4))
for b in range(30):
    plt.plot(np.arange(1, H+1), paths[b], alpha=0.2, linewidth=0.8)
plt.plot(np.arange(1, H+1), median, linewidth=2.5, label="median")
plt.fill_between(np.arange(1, H+1), lo, hi, alpha=0.25, label="90% simulation interval")
plt.xlabel("horizon")
plt.ylabel("future value")
plt.title("Simulated future paths")
plt.legend()
plt.show()
```

## 18.17 Bayesian predictive distributions

In Bayesian forecasting, parameters are treated as random variables. If $\theta$ denotes model parameters, then the posterior predictive distribution is

$$
p(y_{T+h}\mid y_{1:T})
=
\int p(y_{T+h}\mid y_{1:T},\theta)p(\theta\mid y_{1:T})\,d\theta.
$$

This integral includes two kinds of uncertainty:

1. **observation uncertainty**, from $p(y_{T+h}\mid y_{1:T},\theta)$;
2. **parameter uncertainty**, from $p(\theta\mid y_{1:T})$.

In many classical plug-in intervals, we estimate $\theta$ once and ignore parameter uncertainty:

$$
p(y_{T+h}\mid y_{1:T},\widehat\theta).
$$

Bayesian and ensemble methods often produce wider intervals when the training data are limited.

## 18.18 Ensembles as approximate uncertainty tools

An ensemble trains multiple models and combines their forecasts. Let

$$
\widehat y_{t+h|t}^{(1)},\ldots,\widehat y_{t+h|t}^{(B)}
$$

be forecasts from $B$ models. The ensemble mean is

$$
\overline y_{t+h|t}=\frac1B\sum_{b=1}^B\widehat y_{t+h|t}^{(b)}.
$$

The spread of the ensemble can approximate model uncertainty, but it is not automatically a calibrated prediction interval. Ensemble spread usually captures only part of total uncertainty. Observation noise and calibration still need to be included.

A practical method is:

1. train an ensemble;
2. compute point forecasts and ensemble spread;
3. calibrate residual or normalized residual scores on a validation period;
4. evaluate coverage on a future test period.

## 18.19 Distributional neural forecasts

A neural network can forecast distributional parameters instead of a single value. For example, assume

$$
Y_{t+h}\mid X_t\sim N(\mu_\theta(X_t,h),\sigma_\theta^2(X_t,h)).
$$

The network outputs $\mu_\theta$ and a positive scale $\sigma_\theta$. Positivity can be enforced by

$$
\sigma_\theta=\operatorname{softplus}(a_\theta)+\epsilon.
$$

The negative log-likelihood loss is

$$
\mathcal L(\theta)
=
\frac12\log(2\pi)
+
\log \sigma_\theta(X_t,h)
+
\frac{(Y_{t+h}-\mu_\theta(X_t,h))^2}{2\sigma_\theta^2(X_t,h)}.
$$

Other distributional choices include Student-$t$, negative binomial, Poisson, log-normal, mixture density networks, and quantile-output neural networks.

## 18.20 Calibration diagnostics

A probabilistic forecast is calibrated if events predicted to happen with probability $p$ happen about proportion $p$ of the time.

For intervals, calibration means

$$
\mathbb P(L_{t,h}\le Y_{t+h}\le U_{t,h})\approx 1-\alpha.
$$

For a full forecast CDF $F_{t,h}$, the probability integral transform is

$$
U_{t,h}=F_{t,h}(Y_{t+h}).
$$

If the predictive CDFs are calibrated and continuous, then $U_{t,h}$ should be approximately uniform on $[0,1]$.

Common diagnostic plots include:

1. empirical coverage by horizon;
2. average interval width by horizon;
3. coverage by season, regime, or demand level;
4. PIT histogram;
5. forecast residuals versus predicted uncertainty;
6. interval misses over time;
7. separate upper-tail and lower-tail miss rates.

```{python}
#| label: ch18-pit-diagnostic
#| fig-cap: "A PIT histogram can reveal forecast distribution miscalibration."
#| warning: false
#| message: false
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from math import erf, sqrt

rng = np.random.default_rng(44)
n = 1200
# True observations have heavier tails than the forecast distribution assumes.

forecast_mu = np.zeros(n)
forecast_sigma = np.ones(n)

def norm_cdf_array(x):

plt.axhline(1.0, linestyle="--", linewidth=1)
plt.xlabel("PIT value")
plt.ylabel("density")
plt.title("PIT histogram: heavy-tailed observations under Gaussian forecasts")
plt.show()
```

## 18.21 Tail risk and decision thresholds

Probabilistic forecasting is especially useful for threshold decisions. Suppose a decision maker cares whether

$$
Y_{t+h}>c.
$$

A forecast distribution provides

$$
\mathbb P(Y_{t+h}>c\mid\mathcal F_t)=1-F_{t,h}(c).
$$

Examples:

- probability hospital demand exceeds staffed beds;
- probability energy demand exceeds capacity;
- probability inventory demand exceeds stock;
- probability a sensor reading crosses a safety threshold;
- probability revenue falls below a target.

The forecast can be connected to expected loss. If under-staffing costs $C_u$ and over-staffing costs $C_o$, the optimal quantile level in a newsvendor-style problem is

$$
\tau^*=\frac{C_u}{C_u+C_o}.
$$

This is why quantile forecasts are directly decision-relevant.

## 18.24 Common mistakes

1. **Reporting only RMSE.** RMSE says little about uncertainty calibration.
2. **Using confidence intervals instead of prediction intervals.** Prediction intervals must include future observation noise.
3. **Random calibration split.** This can leak future regimes into calibration.
4. **Ignoring horizon.** Coverage often deteriorates at longer horizons.
5. **Assuming Gaussian residuals without checking.** Heavy tails and skewness matter for intervals.
6. **Using training residuals for calibration.** Training residuals are often too optimistic.
7. **Forgetting exogenous uncertainty.** Future covariates may themselves need forecasts.
8. **Ignoring heteroskedasticity.** Constant-width intervals fail when uncertainty changes with level, season, or regime.
9. **Overtrusting ensemble spread.** Ensemble spread is not the same as total predictive uncertainty.
10. **Not connecting uncertainty to decisions.** A 90% interval may not be the right object for an asymmetric-cost problem.
