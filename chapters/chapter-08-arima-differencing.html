Part III. Nonstationary and Seasonal Models
Chapter goal: learn how to model nonstationary time series by transforming them into stationary series. We develop differencing operators, integrated processes, ARIMA models, model identification after differencing, forecasting on the original scale, diagnostics, and AI-assisted checks for under-differencing and over-differencing.

10.1 Learning objectives

After studying this chapter, you should be able to:

  1. explain why many real time series are nonstationary and why ARMA models cannot be applied directly;
  2. use the backshift operator \(B\) and the difference operator \(\nabla=1-B\);
  3. define ARIMA(\(p,d,q\)) models and interpret \(p\), \(d\), and \(q\);
  4. distinguish deterministic trends from stochastic trends;
  5. identify when first or second differencing is appropriate;
  6. recognize over-differencing and under-differencing from time plots and ACF behavior;
  7. use ACF and PACF of the differenced series to propose \(p\) and \(q\);
  8. fit ARIMA models in Python and diagnose residuals;
  9. transform forecasts of differenced data back to forecasts of the original series;
  10. use AI tools to critique a proposed ARIMA workflow while verifying all mathematical and diagnostic claims.

10.2 8.1 Why ARIMA is needed

So far, the core linear model class has been ARMA(\(p,q\)):

\[ \phi(B)X_t=\theta(B)W_t, \]

where \(\{X_t\}\) is assumed stationary. This assumption is powerful because it lets us summarize dependence using a time-invariant autocovariance function

\[ \gamma(h)=\operatorname{Cov}(X_{t+h},X_t), \]

and a time-invariant autocorrelation function

\[ \rho(h)=\frac{\gamma(h)}{\gamma(0)}. \]

However, many real time series are not stationary. A stock index, cumulative sales, population count, temperature anomaly, ridership total, or electricity demand series may have trend, changing level, seasonality, or structural changes. Applying a stationary ARMA model directly to such a series often produces misleading forecasts and misleading standard errors.

ARIMA models solve one important kind of nonstationarity by differencing. Instead of assuming \(X_t\) itself is stationary, we assume that a differenced version of \(X_t\) is stationary.

NoteCore idea

An ARIMA model is an ARMA model applied after differencing:

\[ \nabla^d X_t \quad \text{is modeled as ARMA}(p,q). \]

This idea is simple but subtle. Differencing can remove trend, but it also changes the dependence structure. Choosing the differencing order \(d\) is therefore a modeling decision, not a mechanical preprocessing step.

10.3 8.2 Backshift and difference operators

The backshift operator \(B\) acts on a time series by shifting it one time step backward:

\[ BX_t=X_{t-1}. \]

The first difference operator is

\[ \nabla X_t=X_t-X_{t-1}=(1-B)X_t. \]

The second difference is

\[ \nabla^2X_t=\nabla(\nabla X_t)=(1-B)^2X_t. \]

Expanding gives

\[ \nabla^2X_t=X_t-2X_{t-1}+X_{t-2}. \]

More generally,

\[ \nabla^dX_t=(1-B)^dX_t. \]

Using the binomial theorem,

\[ (1-B)^d = \sum_{j=0}^{d} {d \choose j}(-1)^j B^j, \]

so

\[ \nabla^dX_t=\sum_{j=0}^{d}{d \choose j}(-1)^jX_{t-j}. \]

For example,

\[ \nabla^3X_t=X_t-3X_{t-1}+3X_{t-2}-X_{t-3}. \]

In practice, we rarely use \(d>2\) for ordinary nonseasonal differencing. Large \(d\) can create artificial negative autocorrelation and unnecessarily complex models.

WarningPractical warning

Differencing is not harmless. It removes low-frequency behavior, but it also changes the stochastic part of the series. Over-differencing can turn a simple signal into a noisier signal with avoidable moving-average structure.

10.4 8.3 Deterministic trend versus stochastic trend

A common model for a nonstationary-looking series is

\[ X_t=\mu_t+Y_t, \]

where \(\mu_t\) is a deterministic trend and \(Y_t\) is stationary. For example,

\[ X_t=\beta_0+\beta_1t+Y_t. \]

Then

\[ \nabla X_t=X_t-X_{t-1} =\beta_1+Y_t-Y_{t-1}. \]

If \(Y_t\) is stationary, then \(Y_t-Y_{t-1}\) is also stationary. Therefore, the first difference is stationary around mean \(\beta_1\).

But there is another kind of nonstationarity: a stochastic trend. The random walk is the basic example:

\[ X_t=X_{t-1}+W_t, \]

where \(W_t\sim WN(0,\sigma^2)\). Then

\[ \nabla X_t=W_t, \]

which is stationary white noise. The original process is nonstationary because

\[ \operatorname{Var}(X_t)=t\sigma^2 \]

when \(X_0=0\).

These two cases can look similar in a time plot but have different interpretations.

Type Example What differencing does Interpretation
Deterministic trend \(X_t=\beta_0+\beta_1t+Y_t\) removes the linear trend series fluctuates around a deterministic path
Stochastic trend \(X_t=X_{t-1}+W_t\) removes the unit root shocks have permanent effects
TipModeling interpretation

A deterministic trend says the long-run path is predictable up to stationary noise. A stochastic trend says shocks accumulate and permanently change the level.

10.5 8.4 Integrated processes

A process \(X_t\) is called integrated of order \(d\), written \(X_t\sim I(d)\), if

\[ \nabla^d X_t \]

is stationary, while \(\nabla^{d-1}X_t\) is not stationary.

Examples:

  1. White noise is \(I(0)\).
  2. A stationary ARMA process is \(I(0)\).
  3. A random walk is \(I(1)\).
  4. A process whose second difference is stationary but whose first difference is not stationary is \(I(2)\).

The notation \(I(d)\) is about the number of differences needed to reach stationarity. It is not a statement that the original series has finite variance for all time. In fact, many integrated processes are nonstationary and have variance that grows with time.

10.6 8.5 Definition of ARIMA(\(p,d,q\))

A time series \(\{X_t\}\) is an ARIMA(\(p,d,q\)) process if

\[ Y_t=\nabla^dX_t=(1-B)^dX_t \]

is an ARMA(\(p,q\)) process. That is,

\[ \phi(B)Y_t=\theta(B)W_t, \]

where

\[ \phi(B)=1-\phi_1B-\cdots-\phi_pB^p, \]

and

\[ \theta(B)=1+\theta_1B+\cdots+\theta_qB^q. \]

Substituting \(Y_t=(1-B)^dX_t\) gives the ARIMA equation

\[ \phi(B)(1-B)^dX_t=\theta(B)W_t. \]

If the differenced series has a nonzero mean, we write

\[ \phi(B)(1-B)^dX_t=\alpha+\theta(B)W_t. \]

The three orders have different meanings:

Symbol Meaning Role
\(p\) autoregressive order dependence on past differenced values
\(d\) differencing order number of nonseasonal differences needed for stationarity
\(q\) moving-average order dependence on past shocks/innovations
NoteARMA as a special case

ARMA(\(p,q\)) is the same as ARIMA(\(p,0,q\)). ARIMA extends ARMA by allowing integrated nonstationarity.

10.7 8.6 Basic examples

10.7.1 Example 1: White noise

If

\[ X_t=W_t, \]

then \(X_t\) is ARIMA(\(0,0,0\)). No differencing is needed.

10.7.2 Example 2: Random walk

If

\[ X_t=X_{t-1}+W_t, \]

then

\[ (1-B)X_t=W_t. \]

Thus \(X_t\) is ARIMA(\(0,1,0\)).

10.7.3 Example 3: Random walk with drift

If

\[ X_t=\delta+X_{t-1}+W_t, \]

then

\[ \nabla X_t=\delta+W_t. \]

This is ARIMA(\(0,1,0\)) with drift. The \(h\)-step forecast from time \(n\) is

\[ \widehat X_{n+h|n}=X_n+h\widehat\delta. \]

10.7.4 Example 4: ARIMA(\(1,1,0\))

If

\[ \nabla X_t=\phi \nabla X_{t-1}+W_t, \]

then

\[ (1-\phi B)(1-B)X_t=W_t. \]

This is ARIMA(\(1,1,0\)). The first differences behave like an AR(1) process.

10.7.5 Example 5: ARIMA(\(0,1,1\))

If

\[ \nabla X_t=W_t+\theta W_{t-1}, \]

then

\[ (1-B)X_t=(1+\theta B)W_t. \]

This is ARIMA(\(0,1,1\)). Some exponential smoothing models are closely related to ARIMA(\(0,1,1\)).

10.8 8.7 Differencing a deterministic trend

Suppose

\[ X_t=\beta_0+\beta_1t+Y_t, \]

where \(Y_t\) is stationary. Then

\[ \nabla X_t=\beta_1+(Y_t-Y_{t-1}). \]

This is stationary if \(Y_t\) is stationary. But notice that the noise structure has changed. If \(Y_t\) is white noise, then

\[ Y_t-Y_{t-1}=W_t-W_{t-1}, \]

which is MA(1) with coefficient \(-1\).

This explains a common phenomenon: differencing a deterministic trend often creates moving-average behavior.

For a quadratic trend,

\[ X_t=\beta_0+\beta_1t+\beta_2t^2+Y_t, \]

the first difference still has a linear trend:

\[ \nabla X_t=\beta_1+\beta_2(2t-1)+Y_t-Y_{t-1}. \]

The second difference removes the quadratic trend:

\[ \nabla^2 X_t=2\beta_2+Y_t-2Y_{t-1}+Y_{t-2}. \]

WarningDetrending versus differencing

Regression detrending estimates and subtracts \(\widehat\mu_t\). Differencing removes polynomial trend without estimating all trend coefficients, but it changes the stationary component. Both tools are useful, and neither should be applied blindly.

10.9 8.8 How to choose the differencing order \(d\)

The differencing order \(d\) should be chosen before identifying \(p\) and \(q\). The usual workflow is:

  1. Plot \(X_t\).
  2. Check whether the level and variance appear stable.
  3. Inspect the ACF of \(X_t\).
  4. Difference once and inspect \(\nabla X_t\).
  5. Difference twice only if necessary.
  6. Once the differenced series appears stationary, inspect ACF/PACF to choose \(p\) and \(q\).

A slowly decaying ACF is a common sign of nonstationarity. For a random walk or a strongly trended series, the sample ACF often remains large for many lags.

Typical clues:

Observation Possible decision
time plot has stable level and variance try \(d=0\)
time plot has persistent trend try \(d=1\)
first difference still has trend consider \(d=2\)
ACF decays very slowly increase \(d\) or remove trend
lag-1 ACF of differenced series is strongly negative possible over-differencing

Formal tests can help, but they do not replace plots and subject-matter reasoning. Two common tests are:

  • the augmented Dickey–Fuller test, where the null hypothesis is often a unit root;
  • the KPSS test, where the null hypothesis is often stationarity around a level or trend.

These tests can disagree in finite samples. For this course, the key principle is to combine mathematical structure, plots, ACF/PACF, residual diagnostics, and forecasting performance.

10.10 8.9 Under-differencing and over-differencing

10.10.1 Under-differencing

A series is under-differenced if nonstationarity remains after transformation. Symptoms include:

  • a visible trend in the transformed series;
  • slowly decaying ACF;
  • residual autocorrelation after fitting;
  • forecasts that lag behind changes in level.

10.10.2 Over-differencing

A series is over-differenced if too many differences are applied. Symptoms include:

  • unnecessary roughness;
  • strong negative autocorrelation at lag 1;
  • inflated forecast variance;
  • fitted MA coefficients near noninvertibility;
  • worse out-of-sample forecasts despite apparently stationary transformed data.

A simple example shows why. If \(X_t\) is already white noise, then

\[ Y_t=\nabla X_t=X_t-X_{t-1}=W_t-W_{t-1}. \]

This is MA(1) with coefficient \(-1\). Its lag-1 autocorrelation is

\[ \rho_Y(1)=\frac{-\sigma^2}{2\sigma^2}=-\frac12. \]

Thus differencing white noise creates artificial negative dependence.

TipDiagnostic rule of thumb

If the differenced series has a very negative lag-1 autocorrelation, ask whether the original series may have been differenced too much.

10.11 8.10 Identifying \(p\) and \(q\) after differencing

Once \(Y_t=\nabla^dX_t\) looks stationary, identify the ARMA model for \(Y_t\).

For \(Y_t\):

Candidate for \(Y_t\) ACF PACF
AR(\(p\)) decays gradually cuts off after lag \(p\)
MA(\(q\)) cuts off after lag \(q\) decays gradually
ARMA(\(p,q\)) decays gradually decays gradually
white noise insignificant after lag 0 insignificant after lag 0

Then \(X_t\) is ARIMA(\(p,d,q\)).

Example: If \(\nabla X_t\) has an ACF that cuts off after lag 1 and a PACF that decays gradually, then \(\nabla X_t\) may be MA(1), so \(X_t\) may be ARIMA(\(0,1,1\)).

Example: If \(\nabla X_t\) has a PACF that cuts off after lag 2 and an ACF that decays gradually, then \(\nabla X_t\) may be AR(2), so \(X_t\) may be ARIMA(\(2,1,0\)).

10.12 8.11 Forecasting ARIMA models

Forecasting an ARIMA model happens in two stages:

  1. Forecast the stationary differenced series.
  2. Convert the forecast back to the original scale.

For ARIMA(\(p,1,q\)), define

\[ Y_t=\nabla X_t=X_t-X_{t-1}. \]

Suppose we forecast future differences:

\[ \widehat Y_{n+1|n},\widehat Y_{n+2|n},\ldots,\widehat Y_{n+h|n}. \]

Then forecasts of the original series satisfy

\[ \widehat X_{n+1|n}=X_n+\widehat Y_{n+1|n}, \]

\[ \widehat X_{n+2|n}=\widehat X_{n+1|n}+\widehat Y_{n+2|n}, \]

and in general

\[ \widehat X_{n+h|n}=X_n+\sum_{j=1}^{h}\widehat Y_{n+j|n}. \]

For \(d=2\), the reconstruction accumulates second-difference forecasts twice. Software handles this automatically, but students should understand that ARIMA forecasts are produced by modeling changes and then integrating the forecasted changes.

NoteForecast intervals widen

Integrated models often produce forecast intervals that widen with horizon because future shocks accumulate on the original scale.

10.13 8.12 Constants, drift, and trend in ARIMA

Constants in ARIMA models require care. Consider

\[ \phi(B)(1-B)^dX_t=\alpha+\theta(B)W_t. \]

When \(d=0\), a constant usually corresponds to a nonzero mean of the stationary ARMA process.

When \(d=1\), a constant in the differenced equation corresponds to drift in the original series. For example,

\[ \nabla X_t=\delta+W_t \]

implies

\[ X_t=X_0+\delta t+\sum_{j=1}^{t}W_j. \]

Thus \(\delta\) creates a linear mean path on the original scale.

When \(d=2\), a constant in the second-differenced equation can induce a quadratic mean path on the original scale. This is one reason to avoid high differencing orders unless there is a clear reason.

10.14 8.13 Model estimation and diagnostics

An ARIMA workflow usually follows these steps:

  1. Transform the data if needed, such as log transform for increasing variance.
  2. Choose \(d\) using plots, ACF, and domain knowledge.
  3. Inspect ACF/PACF of \(\nabla^dX_t\) to propose \(p\) and \(q\).
  4. Fit several candidate ARIMA models.
  5. Compare using AIC, AICc, BIC, and forecast validation.
  6. Check residuals.
  7. Revise if residuals show autocorrelation, changing variance, outliers, or nonnormal heavy tails.
  8. Forecast and communicate uncertainty.

Residuals should behave like white noise:

\[ \widehat e_t \approx WN(0,\sigma^2). \]

Useful residual diagnostics include:

  • residual time plot;
  • residual ACF;
  • Ljung–Box test;
  • histogram and Q–Q plot;
  • rolling variance check;
  • forecast backtesting.
WarningDo not choose by AIC alone

AIC can help compare fitted candidate models, but a model with the lowest AIC may still have bad residual diagnostics or poor forecasting performance on future data.

10.16 8.15 Python example: deterministic trend, random walk, and differencing

The following example compares deterministic and stochastic trend. Both can look nonstationary, but their meanings are different.

Code 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

np.random.seed(7339)

n = 250
t = np.arange(n)

# Deterministic trend plus stationary noise
phi = 0.6
noise = np.random.normal(size=n)
y = np.zeros(n)
for i in range(1, n):
    y[i] = phi * y[i-1] + noise[i]

x_det = 10 + 0.05 * t + y

# Stochastic trend: random walk with drift
w = np.random.normal(scale=1.0, size=n)
x_rw = np.cumsum(0.05 + w)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(9, 3.5))
ax.plot(t, x_det, label="deterministic trend + AR(1)")
ax.plot(t, x_rw, label="random walk with drift")
ax.set_title("Two nonstationary-looking series")
ax.set_xlabel("time")
ax.legend()
plt.show()

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(9, 3.5))
ax.plot(t[1:], np.diff(x_det), label="difference of deterministic trend model")
ax.plot(t[1:], np.diff(x_rw), label="difference of random walk")
ax.axhline(0, linewidth=1)
ax.set_title("First differences")
ax.set_xlabel("time")
ax.legend()
plt.show()

The first difference of the random walk is close to white noise with drift. The first difference of the deterministic trend model is stationary, but it has a different dependence structure from the original stationary component.

10.18 8.17 Python example: fitting ARIMA models

Here we simulate an ARIMA(\(1,1,1\)) series and fit several candidate models.

Code 
import warnings
import pandas as pd
from statsmodels.tsa.arima.model import ARIMA

warnings.filterwarnings("ignore")

np.random.seed(2026)

n = 350
burn = 100
phi = 0.65
theta = -0.45
w = np.random.normal(size=n + burn + 1)
y = np.zeros(n + burn + 1)

# y_t = phi y_{t-1} + w_t + theta w_{t-1}
for i in range(1, n + burn + 1):
    y[i] = phi * y[i-1] + w[i] + theta * w[i-1]

# x_t is integrated: difference x_t is y_t
x = np.cumsum(y)[burn:]

candidates = []
for p in range(3):
    for d in [0, 1, 2]:
        for q in range(3):
            if p == 0 and d == 0 and q == 0:
                continue
            try:
                fit = ARIMA(x, order=(p, d, q), trend="n").fit()
                candidates.append({
                    "p": p,
                    "d": d,
                    "q": q,
                    "AIC": fit.aic,
                    "BIC": fit.bic
                })
            except Exception:
                pass

arima_table = pd.DataFrame(candidates).sort_values("AIC")

10.19 8.18 Python example: forecasting on the original scale

The next example fits an ARIMA model and produces forecasts. Notice that statsmodels automatically returns forecasts on the original scale of \(X_t\), not on the differenced scale.

Code 
h = 40
fit = ARIMA(x, order=best_order, trend="n").fit()
forecast_result = fit.get_forecast(steps=h)
forecast_mean = forecast_result.predicted_mean
forecast_ci = forecast_result.conf_int()

future_index = np.arange(len(x), len(x) + h)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(9, 4))
ax.plot(np.arange(len(x)), x, label="observed")
ax.plot(future_index, forecast_mean, label="forecast")
ax.fill_between(
    future_index,
    forecast_ci[:, 0],
    forecast_ci[:, 1],
    alpha=0.25,
    label="95% interval"
)

10.21 8.20 Common mistakes

  1. Fitting ARMA directly to a nonstationary series.
    This often leads to spurious dependence and unreliable forecasts.

  2. Differencing before looking at the data.
    The time plot, variance behavior, and domain meaning should be considered first.

  3. Using \(d=2\) too quickly.
    Second differencing can create unnecessary complexity and unstable forecasts.

  4. Ignoring transformations.
    If variability increases with the level, a log or Box–Cox transformation may be needed before differencing.

  5. Choosing \(p,d,q\) only by AIC.
    AIC should be combined with diagnostics and forecasting validation.

  6. Interpreting differenced forecasts incorrectly.
    Forecasts of \(\nabla X_t\) must be accumulated to recover forecasts of \(X_t\).

  7. Forgetting constants and drift.
    A constant in a differenced model has a different meaning from a mean in a stationary model.

  8. Ignoring outliers and structural breaks.
    A single level shift can look like nonstationarity and mislead differencing decisions.

## 8.1 Why ARIMA is needed

So far, the core linear model class has been ARMA($p,q$):

$$
\phi(B)X_t=\theta(B)W_t,
$$

where $\{X_t\}$ is assumed stationary. This assumption is powerful because it lets us summarize dependence using a time-invariant autocovariance function

$$
\gamma(h)=\operatorname{Cov}(X_{t+h},X_t),
$$

and a time-invariant autocorrelation function

$$
\rho(h)=\frac{\gamma(h)}{\gamma(0)}.
$$

However, many real time series are not stationary. A stock index, cumulative sales, population count, temperature anomaly, ridership total, or electricity demand series may have trend, changing level, seasonality, or structural changes. Applying a stationary ARMA model directly to such a series often produces misleading forecasts and misleading standard errors.

ARIMA models solve one important kind of nonstationarity by **differencing**. Instead of assuming $X_t$ itself is stationary, we assume that a differenced version of $X_t$ is stationary.

::: {.callout-note}
## Core idea
An ARIMA model is an ARMA model applied after differencing:

$$
\nabla^d X_t \quad \text{is modeled as ARMA}(p,q).
$$
:::

This idea is simple but subtle. Differencing can remove trend, but it also changes the dependence structure. Choosing the differencing order $d$ is therefore a modeling decision, not a mechanical preprocessing step.

## 8.2 Backshift and difference operators

The **backshift operator** $B$ acts on a time series by shifting it one time step backward:

$$
BX_t=X_{t-1}.
$$

The first difference operator is

$$
\nabla X_t=X_t-X_{t-1}=(1-B)X_t.
$$

The second difference is

$$
\nabla^2X_t=\nabla(\nabla X_t)=(1-B)^2X_t.
$$

Expanding gives

$$
\nabla^2X_t=X_t-2X_{t-1}+X_{t-2}.
$$

More generally,

$$
\nabla^dX_t=(1-B)^dX_t.
$$

Using the binomial theorem,

$$
(1-B)^d = \sum_{j=0}^{d} {d \choose j}(-1)^j B^j,
$$

so

$$
\nabla^dX_t=\sum_{j=0}^{d}{d \choose j}(-1)^jX_{t-j}.
$$

For example,

$$
\nabla^3X_t=X_t-3X_{t-1}+3X_{t-2}-X_{t-3}.
$$

In practice, we rarely use $d>2$ for ordinary nonseasonal differencing. Large $d$ can create artificial negative autocorrelation and unnecessarily complex models.

::: {.callout-warning}
## Practical warning
Differencing is not harmless. It removes low-frequency behavior, but it also changes the stochastic part of the series. Over-differencing can turn a simple signal into a noisier signal with avoidable moving-average structure.
:::

## 8.3 Deterministic trend versus stochastic trend

A common model for a nonstationary-looking series is

$$
X_t=\mu_t+Y_t,
$$

where $\mu_t$ is a deterministic trend and $Y_t$ is stationary. For example,

$$
X_t=\beta_0+\beta_1t+Y_t.
$$

Then

$$
\nabla X_t=X_t-X_{t-1}
=\beta_1+Y_t-Y_{t-1}.
$$

If $Y_t$ is stationary, then $Y_t-Y_{t-1}$ is also stationary. Therefore, the first difference is stationary around mean $\beta_1$.

But there is another kind of nonstationarity: a **stochastic trend**. The random walk is the basic example:

$$
X_t=X_{t-1}+W_t,
$$

where $W_t\sim WN(0,\sigma^2)$. Then

$$
\nabla X_t=W_t,
$$

which is stationary white noise. The original process is nonstationary because

$$
\operatorname{Var}(X_t)=t\sigma^2
$$

when $X_0=0$.

These two cases can look similar in a time plot but have different interpretations.

| Type | Example | What differencing does | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Deterministic trend | $X_t=\beta_0+\beta_1t+Y_t$ | removes the linear trend | series fluctuates around a deterministic path |
| Stochastic trend | $X_t=X_{t-1}+W_t$ | removes the unit root | shocks have permanent effects |

::: {.callout-tip}
## Modeling interpretation
A deterministic trend says the long-run path is predictable up to stationary noise. A stochastic trend says shocks accumulate and permanently change the level.
:::

## 8.4 Integrated processes

A process $X_t$ is called **integrated of order $d$**, written $X_t\sim I(d)$, if

$$
\nabla^d X_t
$$

is stationary, while $\nabla^{d-1}X_t$ is not stationary.

Examples:

1. White noise is $I(0)$.
2. A stationary ARMA process is $I(0)$.
3. A random walk is $I(1)$.
4. A process whose second difference is stationary but whose first difference is not stationary is $I(2)$.

The notation $I(d)$ is about the number of differences needed to reach stationarity. It is not a statement that the original series has finite variance for all time. In fact, many integrated processes are nonstationary and have variance that grows with time.

## 8.5 Definition of ARIMA($p,d,q$)

A time series $\{X_t\}$ is an ARIMA($p,d,q$) process if

$$
Y_t=\nabla^dX_t=(1-B)^dX_t
$$

is an ARMA($p,q$) process. That is,

$$
\phi(B)Y_t=\theta(B)W_t,
$$

where

$$
\phi(B)=1-\phi_1B-\cdots-\phi_pB^p,
$$

and

$$
\theta(B)=1+\theta_1B+\cdots+\theta_qB^q.
$$

Substituting $Y_t=(1-B)^dX_t$ gives the ARIMA equation

$$
\phi(B)(1-B)^dX_t=\theta(B)W_t.
$$

If the differenced series has a nonzero mean, we write

$$
\phi(B)(1-B)^dX_t=\alpha+\theta(B)W_t.
$$

The three orders have different meanings:

| Symbol | Meaning | Role |
|---|---|---|
| $p$ | autoregressive order | dependence on past differenced values |
| $d$ | differencing order | number of nonseasonal differences needed for stationarity |
| $q$ | moving-average order | dependence on past shocks/innovations |

::: {.callout-note}
## ARMA as a special case
ARMA($p,q$) is the same as ARIMA($p,0,q$). ARIMA extends ARMA by allowing integrated nonstationarity.
:::

## 8.6 Basic examples

### Example 1: White noise

If

$$
X_t=W_t,
$$

then $X_t$ is ARIMA($0,0,0$). No differencing is needed.

### Example 2: Random walk

If

$$
X_t=X_{t-1}+W_t,
$$

then

$$
(1-B)X_t=W_t.
$$

Thus $X_t$ is ARIMA($0,1,0$).

### Example 3: Random walk with drift

If

$$
X_t=\delta+X_{t-1}+W_t,
$$

then

$$
\nabla X_t=\delta+W_t.
$$

This is ARIMA($0,1,0$) with drift. The $h$-step forecast from time $n$ is

$$
\widehat X_{n+h|n}=X_n+h\widehat\delta.
$$

### Example 4: ARIMA($1,1,0$)

If

$$
\nabla X_t=\phi \nabla X_{t-1}+W_t,
$$

then

$$
(1-\phi B)(1-B)X_t=W_t.
$$

This is ARIMA($1,1,0$). The first differences behave like an AR(1) process.

### Example 5: ARIMA($0,1,1$)

If

$$
\nabla X_t=W_t+\theta W_{t-1},
$$

then

$$
(1-B)X_t=(1+\theta B)W_t.
$$

This is ARIMA($0,1,1$). Some exponential smoothing models are closely related to ARIMA($0,1,1$).

## 8.7 Differencing a deterministic trend

Suppose

$$
X_t=\beta_0+\beta_1t+Y_t,
$$

where $Y_t$ is stationary. Then

$$
\nabla X_t=\beta_1+(Y_t-Y_{t-1}).
$$

This is stationary if $Y_t$ is stationary. But notice that the noise structure has changed. If $Y_t$ is white noise, then

$$
Y_t-Y_{t-1}=W_t-W_{t-1},
$$

which is MA(1) with coefficient $-1$.

This explains a common phenomenon: differencing a deterministic trend often creates moving-average behavior.

For a quadratic trend,

$$
X_t=\beta_0+\beta_1t+\beta_2t^2+Y_t,
$$

the first difference still has a linear trend:

$$
\nabla X_t=\beta_1+\beta_2(2t-1)+Y_t-Y_{t-1}.
$$

The second difference removes the quadratic trend:

$$
\nabla^2 X_t=2\beta_2+Y_t-2Y_{t-1}+Y_{t-2}.
$$

::: {.callout-warning}
## Detrending versus differencing
Regression detrending estimates and subtracts $\widehat\mu_t$. Differencing removes polynomial trend without estimating all trend coefficients, but it changes the stationary component. Both tools are useful, and neither should be applied blindly.
:::

## 8.8 How to choose the differencing order $d$

The differencing order $d$ should be chosen before identifying $p$ and $q$. The usual workflow is:

1. Plot $X_t$.
2. Check whether the level and variance appear stable.
3. Inspect the ACF of $X_t$.
4. Difference once and inspect $\nabla X_t$.
5. Difference twice only if necessary.
6. Once the differenced series appears stationary, inspect ACF/PACF to choose $p$ and $q$.

A slowly decaying ACF is a common sign of nonstationarity. For a random walk or a strongly trended series, the sample ACF often remains large for many lags.

Typical clues:

| Observation | Possible decision |
|---|---|
| time plot has stable level and variance | try $d=0$ |
| time plot has persistent trend | try $d=1$ |
| first difference still has trend | consider $d=2$ |
| ACF decays very slowly | increase $d$ or remove trend |
| lag-1 ACF of differenced series is strongly negative | possible over-differencing |

Formal tests can help, but they do not replace plots and subject-matter reasoning. Two common tests are:

- the augmented Dickey--Fuller test, where the null hypothesis is often a unit root;
- the KPSS test, where the null hypothesis is often stationarity around a level or trend.

These tests can disagree in finite samples. For this course, the key principle is to combine mathematical structure, plots, ACF/PACF, residual diagnostics, and forecasting performance.

## 8.9 Under-differencing and over-differencing

### Under-differencing

A series is under-differenced if nonstationarity remains after transformation. Symptoms include:

- a visible trend in the transformed series;
- slowly decaying ACF;
- residual autocorrelation after fitting;
- forecasts that lag behind changes in level.

### Over-differencing

A series is over-differenced if too many differences are applied. Symptoms include:

- unnecessary roughness;
- strong negative autocorrelation at lag 1;
- inflated forecast variance;
- fitted MA coefficients near noninvertibility;
- worse out-of-sample forecasts despite apparently stationary transformed data.

A simple example shows why. If $X_t$ is already white noise, then

$$
Y_t=\nabla X_t=X_t-X_{t-1}=W_t-W_{t-1}.
$$

This is MA(1) with coefficient $-1$. Its lag-1 autocorrelation is

$$
\rho_Y(1)=\frac{-\sigma^2}{2\sigma^2}=-\frac12.
$$

Thus differencing white noise creates artificial negative dependence.

::: {.callout-tip}
## Diagnostic rule of thumb
If the differenced series has a very negative lag-1 autocorrelation, ask whether the original series may have been differenced too much.
:::

## 8.10 Identifying $p$ and $q$ after differencing

Once $Y_t=\nabla^dX_t$ looks stationary, identify the ARMA model for $Y_t$.

For $Y_t$:

| Candidate for $Y_t$ | ACF | PACF |
|---|---|---|
| AR($p$) | decays gradually | cuts off after lag $p$ |
| MA($q$) | cuts off after lag $q$ | decays gradually |
| ARMA($p,q$) | decays gradually | decays gradually |
| white noise | insignificant after lag 0 | insignificant after lag 0 |

Then $X_t$ is ARIMA($p,d,q$).

Example: If $\nabla X_t$ has an ACF that cuts off after lag 1 and a PACF that decays gradually, then $\nabla X_t$ may be MA(1), so $X_t$ may be ARIMA($0,1,1$).

Example: If $\nabla X_t$ has a PACF that cuts off after lag 2 and an ACF that decays gradually, then $\nabla X_t$ may be AR(2), so $X_t$ may be ARIMA($2,1,0$).

## 8.11 Forecasting ARIMA models

Forecasting an ARIMA model happens in two stages:

1. Forecast the stationary differenced series.
2. Convert the forecast back to the original scale.

For ARIMA($p,1,q$), define

$$
Y_t=\nabla X_t=X_t-X_{t-1}.
$$

Suppose we forecast future differences:

$$
\widehat Y_{n+1|n},\widehat Y_{n+2|n},\ldots,\widehat Y_{n+h|n}.
$$

Then forecasts of the original series satisfy

$$
\widehat X_{n+1|n}=X_n+\widehat Y_{n+1|n},
$$

$$
\widehat X_{n+2|n}=\widehat X_{n+1|n}+\widehat Y_{n+2|n},
$$

and in general

$$
\widehat X_{n+h|n}=X_n+\sum_{j=1}^{h}\widehat Y_{n+j|n}.
$$

For $d=2$, the reconstruction accumulates second-difference forecasts twice. Software handles this automatically, but students should understand that ARIMA forecasts are produced by modeling changes and then integrating the forecasted changes.

::: {.callout-note}
## Forecast intervals widen
Integrated models often produce forecast intervals that widen with horizon because future shocks accumulate on the original scale.
:::

## 8.12 Constants, drift, and trend in ARIMA

Constants in ARIMA models require care. Consider

$$
\phi(B)(1-B)^dX_t=\alpha+\theta(B)W_t.
$$

When $d=0$, a constant usually corresponds to a nonzero mean of the stationary ARMA process.

When $d=1$, a constant in the differenced equation corresponds to drift in the original series. For example,

$$
\nabla X_t=\delta+W_t
$$

implies

$$
X_t=X_0+\delta t+\sum_{j=1}^{t}W_j.
$$

Thus $\delta$ creates a linear mean path on the original scale.

When $d=2$, a constant in the second-differenced equation can induce a quadratic mean path on the original scale. This is one reason to avoid high differencing orders unless there is a clear reason.

## 8.13 Model estimation and diagnostics

An ARIMA workflow usually follows these steps:

1. Transform the data if needed, such as log transform for increasing variance.
2. Choose $d$ using plots, ACF, and domain knowledge.
3. Inspect ACF/PACF of $\nabla^dX_t$ to propose $p$ and $q$.
4. Fit several candidate ARIMA models.
5. Compare using AIC, AICc, BIC, and forecast validation.
6. Check residuals.
7. Revise if residuals show autocorrelation, changing variance, outliers, or nonnormal heavy tails.
8. Forecast and communicate uncertainty.

Residuals should behave like white noise:

$$
\widehat e_t \approx WN(0,\sigma^2).
$$

Useful residual diagnostics include:

- residual time plot;
- residual ACF;
- Ljung--Box test;
- histogram and Q--Q plot;
- rolling variance check;
- forecast backtesting.

::: {.callout-warning}
## Do not choose by AIC alone
AIC can help compare fitted candidate models, but a model with the lowest AIC may still have bad residual diagnostics or poor forecasting performance on future data.
:::

## 8.15 Python example: deterministic trend, random walk, and differencing

The following example compares deterministic and stochastic trend. Both can look nonstationary, but their meanings are different.

```{python}
#| label: py-ch08-deterministic-vs-stochastic-trend
#| fig-cap: "Deterministic trend and stochastic trend before and after differencing."
#| warning: false
#| message: false

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

np.random.seed(7339)

n = 250
t = np.arange(n)

# Deterministic trend plus stationary noise
phi = 0.6
noise = np.random.normal(size=n)
y = np.zeros(n)
for i in range(1, n):
    y[i] = phi * y[i-1] + noise[i]

x_det = 10 + 0.05 * t + y

# Stochastic trend: random walk with drift
w = np.random.normal(scale=1.0, size=n)
x_rw = np.cumsum(0.05 + w)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(9, 3.5))
ax.plot(t, x_det, label="deterministic trend + AR(1)")
ax.plot(t, x_rw, label="random walk with drift")
ax.set_title("Two nonstationary-looking series")
ax.set_xlabel("time")
ax.legend()
plt.show()

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(9, 3.5))
ax.plot(t[1:], np.diff(x_det), label="difference of deterministic trend model")
ax.plot(t[1:], np.diff(x_rw), label="difference of random walk")
ax.axhline(0, linewidth=1)
ax.set_title("First differences")
ax.set_xlabel("time")
ax.legend()
plt.show()
```

The first difference of the random walk is close to white noise with drift. The first difference of the deterministic trend model is stationary, but it has a different dependence structure from the original stationary component.

## 8.17 Python example: fitting ARIMA models

Here we simulate an ARIMA($1,1,1$) series and fit several candidate models.

```{python}
#| label: py-ch08-fit-arima-grid
#| warning: false
#| message: false

import warnings
import pandas as pd
from statsmodels.tsa.arima.model import ARIMA

warnings.filterwarnings("ignore")

np.random.seed(2026)

n = 350
burn = 100
phi = 0.65
theta = -0.45
w = np.random.normal(size=n + burn + 1)
y = np.zeros(n + burn + 1)

# y_t = phi y_{t-1} + w_t + theta w_{t-1}
for i in range(1, n + burn + 1):
    y[i] = phi * y[i-1] + w[i] + theta * w[i-1]

# x_t is integrated: difference x_t is y_t
x = np.cumsum(y)[burn:]

candidates = []
for p in range(3):
    for d in [0, 1, 2]:
        for q in range(3):
            if p == 0 and d == 0 and q == 0:
                continue
            try:
                fit = ARIMA(x, order=(p, d, q), trend="n").fit()
                candidates.append({
                    "p": p,
                    "d": d,
                    "q": q,
                    "AIC": fit.aic,
                    "BIC": fit.bic
                })
            except Exception:
                pass

arima_table = pd.DataFrame(candidates).sort_values("AIC")
arima_table.head(10)
```

Fit the best candidate and inspect residuals.

```{python}
#| label: py-ch08-arima-residuals
#| fig-cap: "Residual diagnostics for an ARIMA model."
#| warning: false
#| message: false

from statsmodels.stats.diagnostic import acorr_ljungbox
from scipy import stats

best = arima_table.iloc[0]
best_order = (int(best.p), int(best.d), int(best.q))
best_fit = ARIMA(x, order=best_order, trend="n").fit()
resid = np.asarray(best_fit.resid)

print("Selected order:", best_order)
print(acorr_ljungbox(resid, lags=[10, 20], return_df=True))

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(9, 3))
ax.plot(resid)
ax.axhline(0, linewidth=1)
ax.set_title("ARIMA residuals")
ax.set_xlabel("time")
plt.show()

plot_acf(resid, lags=30)
plt.title("Residual ACF")
plt.show()

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(5, 5))
stats.probplot(resid, dist="norm", plot=ax)
ax.set_title("Residual Q-Q plot")
plt.show()
```

## 8.18 Python example: forecasting on the original scale

The next example fits an ARIMA model and produces forecasts. Notice that `statsmodels` automatically returns forecasts on the original scale of $X_t$, not on the differenced scale.

```{python}
#| label: py-ch08-arima-forecast
#| fig-cap: "ARIMA forecasts and forecast intervals on the original scale."
#| warning: false
#| message: false

h = 40
fit = ARIMA(x, order=best_order, trend="n").fit()
forecast_result = fit.get_forecast(steps=h)
forecast_mean = forecast_result.predicted_mean
forecast_ci = forecast_result.conf_int()

future_index = np.arange(len(x), len(x) + h)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(9, 4))
ax.plot(np.arange(len(x)), x, label="observed")
ax.plot(future_index, forecast_mean, label="forecast")
ax.fill_between(
    future_index,
    forecast_ci[:, 0],
    forecast_ci[:, 1],
    alpha=0.25,
    label="95% interval"
)
ax.set_title(f"Forecast from ARIMA{best_order}")
ax.set_xlabel("time")
ax.legend()
plt.show()
```

Forecast intervals typically widen as the horizon increases because future innovations accumulate through the integrated structure.

## 8.20 Common mistakes

1. **Fitting ARMA directly to a nonstationary series.**  
   This often leads to spurious dependence and unreliable forecasts.

2. **Differencing before looking at the data.**  
   The time plot, variance behavior, and domain meaning should be considered first.

3. **Using $d=2$ too quickly.**  
   Second differencing can create unnecessary complexity and unstable forecasts.

4. **Ignoring transformations.**  
   If variability increases with the level, a log or Box--Cox transformation may be needed before differencing.

5. **Choosing $p,d,q$ only by AIC.**  
   AIC should be combined with diagnostics and forecasting validation.

6. **Interpreting differenced forecasts incorrectly.**  
   Forecasts of $\nabla X_t$ must be accumulated to recover forecasts of $X_t$.

7. **Forgetting constants and drift.**  
   A constant in a differenced model has a different meaning from a mean in a stationary model.

8. **Ignoring outliers and structural breaks.**  
   A single level shift can look like nonstationarity and mislead differencing decisions.
